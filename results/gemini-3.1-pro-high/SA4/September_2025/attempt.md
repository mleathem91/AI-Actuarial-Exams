## Question 1

### Part (i) [14 marks]

**(a) From the perspective of the sponsoring employer:**

*   **Defined Benefit (DB):** 
    *   *Advantages:* Excellent tool for recruitment and retention of long-serving staff. Helps with workforce management as employees can afford to retire at the target age. 
    *   *Disadvantages:* The employer bears all demographic (longevity) and financial (investment, inflation) risks. Open-ended and highly volatile costs. Large potential liabilities cause balance sheet volatility and can restrict corporate activity/dividends. High regulatory and administrative burden.
*   **Defined Contribution (DC):**
    *   *Advantages:* Cost certainty—the employer liability is strictly limited to the agreed contributions. No open-ended balance sheet risks. Simpler and cheaper to administer and communicate than DB.
    *   *Disadvantages:* Weaker retention tool. If market performance is poor, employees may not be able to afford to retire, causing workforce succession issues.
*   **Cash Balance:**
    *   *Advantages:* Employer avoids post-retirement longevity and investment risks entirely (as members take a pot at retirement). Offers a more attractive hybrid benefit than pure DC, aiding recruitment.
    *   *Disadvantages:* Employer still bears the pre-retirement investment risk to ensure the guaranteed pot size is met. Still requires actuarial valuations and creates balance sheet liabilities.
*   **Collective Defined Contribution (CDC):**
    *   *Advantages:* Provides a predictable, fixed cost structure (like DC) with no deficit risk on the balance sheet, while offering higher expected pensions (like DB), making it highly attractive for HR purposes.
    *   *Disadvantages:* Extremely complex to establish, communicate, and govern. Risk of severe reputational damage and poor industrial relations if target pensions ever need to be cut.
*   **Defined Contribution with DB underpin:**
    *   *Advantages:* Highly attractive and paternalistic benefit for employees, offering downside protection.
    *   *Disadvantages:* Very expensive to guarantee. It presents an asymmetric risk to the employer (employer pays out if markets perform poorly, but keeps no surplus if markets perform well). Complex administration. 

**(b) From the perspective of the employees:**

*   **Defined Benefit (DB):**
    *   *Advantages:* Provides a guaranteed, predictable income for life. Includes valuable built-in benefits like inflation protection and spouse's pensions. The employee bears no investment risk.
    *   *Disadvantages:* Inflexible at retirement. Complete reliance on the employer's ongoing solvency (covenant) and the PPF. Poorer value for early leavers due to the broken salary link.
*   **Defined Contribution (DC):**
    *   *Advantages:* Total flexibility at retirement (Freedom & Choice—can use drawdown, lump sum, or annuity). Highly portable when changing jobs. The pot size is visible, making it easier to understand as an asset.
    *   *Disadvantages:* The member bears all investment, inflation, and longevity risks. Outcomes are uncertain. Managing decumulation (drawdown) requires high financial literacy and carries the risk of ruin (running out of money).
*   **Cash Balance:**
    *   *Advantages:* Provides a guaranteed capital sum at retirement, shielding the member from pre-retirement market crashes. Easier to understand than a standard DB pension.
    *   *Disadvantages:* Members still bear post-retirement risks (they must self-manage drawdown or buy an expensive annuity). The guaranteed return may be lower than what could be achieved in a pure DC scheme.
*   **Collective Defined Contribution (CDC):**
    *   *Advantages:* Less complex decision-making at retirement compared to individual DC. By pooling longevity and investment risks chronologically and cross-sectionally, members may achieve smoother, higher payouts than individual DC annuitisation.
    *   *Disadvantages:* The pension is a target, not a guarantee, and can be legally reduced. Lacks the individual flexibility of pure DC pots. 
*   **Defined Contribution with DB underpin:**
    *   *Advantages:* The ultimate 'best of both worlds'. Upside potential if markets soar, with a cast-iron safety net if markets crash.
    *   *Disadvantages:* Difficult to understand how the underpin operates. The underlying DC investments are often managed very conservatively by the employer to minimize the risk of the underpin biting, suppressing potential high returns.

### Part (ii) [8 marks]

**Impact on existing members of the DB scheme:**
*   **Past Service:** Their legacy DB benefits are protected, but the link to final salary is broken upon closure. Future increases in deferment will be based on statutory revaluation (e.g., CPI) rather than salary growth. Assuming salary growth typically exceeds inflation, the real value of their DB pension will be lower than expected.
*   **Future Service:** They will now accrue benefits in the new DC scheme. They immediately assume investment, inflation, and longevity risks for this portion of their career. 
*   **Overall Impact:** The total combined expected retirement income is almost certainly lower, as the employer's stated goal is to introduce a scheme with a "lower overall cost". Retirement planning becomes more complex as they must manage a split strategy (legacy DB income + a DC pot). They will gain new decumulation flexibility on the DC portion.

**Impact on future new employees:**
*   **No Guarantees:** New employees will participate entirely in the DC scheme, bearing 100% of the investment, inflation, and longevity risks for their retirement.
*   **Lower Expected Value:** Because the new scheme's design deliberately lowers employer costs, the new employees will receive a less valuable total remuneration package compared to historical hires.
*   **Portability:** Their benefits will be much more portable than DB benefits when changing jobs.
*   **Member Responsibility:** They must make active decisions regarding contribution levels and investment funds, and eventually navigate complex decumulation strategies at retirement. 

### Part (iii) [3 marks]

Factors the trustees should consider in relation to the closure proposal:
*   **Trust Deed and Rules:** Do the rules grant the employer the unilateral power to close the scheme to future accrual, or is trustee consent required? 
*   **Employer Covenant:** Is the employer financially distressed? Reassessing the covenant is vital, as the scheme will now be a pure run-off vehicle entirely reliant on the sponsor to fund existing gaps.
*   **Funding Implications:** Will the cessation of accrual trigger a Section 75 debt, or otherwise alter the timing of cash flows, necessitating a new actuarial valuation or revised recovery plan?
*   **Statutory Compliance:** Have the employer's statutory consultation requirements (e.g., 60-day consultation with affected employees) been adhered to correctly?

### Part (iv) [7 marks]

**Immediate actions:**
*   **Obtain Legal Advice:** To interpret the Trust Deed and Rules ensuring the employer's actions are legally permissible and procedural requirements are met.
*   **Communicate with Members:** Issue clear, factual communications outlining what the closure means for member benefits, ensuring no misleading promises are made.
*   **Data Capture:** Instruct administrators to accurately record "closure date" data (particularly pensionable service and final pensionable salaries) to correctly calculate deferred pensions.
*   **Review Ongoing Valuations:** If a funding valuation is underway, pause and adjust it to reflect the scheme's new status. 
*   **Assess Covenant:** Commission professional covenant advice to assess the employer's ongoing ability to support the closed scheme.

**Longer-term actions:**
*   **Investment Strategy Review:** As the scheme is now closed, its liability duration will shorten and it will mature faster. The trustees should progressively de-risk the investment portfolio (e.g., increasing LDI/bond allocations, reducing equities) to match the maturing cashflows.
*   **Set a Long-Term Objective (LTO):** Shift the governance focus towards a defined end-game, such as reaching self-sufficiency or targeting a full buy-out with an insurer.
*   **Data Cleansing:** Embark on a thorough data cleansing exercise to prepare the scheme for potential future risk transfer exercises (buy-in/buy-out).
*   **Expense Management:** Negotiate and manage administrative and advisory fees closely, as the per-member running costs will proportionately increase as the membership base naturally shrinks.
*   **Covenant Monitoring:** Establish enhanced, continuous monitoring of the sponsor's business performance.

### Part (v) [12 marks]

**Considerations for the Employer:**

**1. Enhanced Transfer Values (ETVs):**
*   **Cost/Benefit Analysis:** The cost of the enhancement operates as a premium paid to remove long-term demographic, investment, and inflation risk from the corporate balance sheet. The employer must assess if this premium represents good value.
*   **Cash Flow and Liquidity:** ETVs require immediate cash payouts. The scheme must have liquid assets; if it does not, or if the scheme is in deficit, the employer may need to inject direct cash to fund the enhancements to avoid worsening the funding position for remaining members.
*   **Take-up Rates:** Exercise costs are highly sensitive to take-up analysis. If take-up is too low, the fixed costs of running the exercise render it uneconomical. 
*   **Regulatory Compliance:** Strict adherence to voluntary Codes of Good Practice requires fair communication and mandates that the employer pays for members to receive mandatory Independent Financial Advice (IFA).

**2. PIE to DC (Transferring PV of future increases to drawdown):**
*   **Tax and Legislative Feasibility:** This is a highly unusual partial transfer. Transferring a portion of the liability to a DC pot while leaving the flat DB pension in payment introduces extreme tax complexities. It risks triggering Unauthorized Payment charges or complicating Lifetime/Annual Allowance calculations depending on the exact tax jurisdiction. Robust legal and tax advice is paramount.
*   **Statutory Protection:** The employer can only offer exchange on discretionary or excess increases. Statutory increases (like GMP or mandatory inflation-linked tranches) usually cannot be surrendered.
*   **Selection Risk:** Unhealthy members with short life expectancies are highly incentivized to take the cash equivalent of increases now. Thus, the scheme pays out the capital but does not experience the expected longevity savings.

**Practicalities of Implementing Both:**
*   **Data Accuracy:** The scheme's underlying data must be perfectly accurate and fully updated (including GMP equalization) before legally binding financial offers can be calculated and issued. 
*   **Securing IFA Capacity:** Finding IFA firms with the regulatory permissions, capacity, and willingness to process hundreds of complex DB transfers and 'partial pie' drawdown conversations simultaneously is a major logistical hurdle.
*   **Administrative Burden:** The sheer volume of queries, bespoke calculations, and processing requirements will overwhelm standard administration teams. Dedicated project teams and helplines are required.
*   **Timeline:** These exercises require extensive planning phases, data processing, communication drafting, and IFA consultation windows, typically taking 12 to 18 months to execute.
*   **Ongoing Administration (PIE):** Managing a pensioner who is simultaneously receiving a flat DB pension and managing a DC drawdown pot from the same trust is an ongoing, heavily complex administrative burden.

### Part (vi) [8 marks]

**Factors for Deferred Members (ETV Offer):**
*   **Value of the Enhancement:** Is the financial uplift sufficiently generous compared to the projected value of the guaranteed DB pension?
*   **Risk Appetite and Capacity:** Can the member withstand a market crash? They are trading guaranteed security for investment and longevity risk.
*   **Health and Longevity:** Members in poor health may benefit from a transfer, securing a larger capital sum that can be passed fully to their estate upon early death, whereas DB spouse pensions are typically smaller fractions.
*   **Flexibility and Personal Needs:** ETVs offer access to Freedom and Choice—allowing members to draw lump sums, retire early, or tailor income to personal spending phases, which DB does not allow.
*   **IFA Recommendation:** The mandatory professional advice will explicitly recommend whether the transfer is in their best financial interest.

**Factors for Pensioner Members (PIE-to-DC Offer):**
*   **Inflation Exposure:** Giving up future increases leaves the core DB pension entirely exposed to inflation. Over a 20-30 year retirement, the real purchasing power of the flat pension will be devastated.
*   **Immediate Liquidity Needs:** The member might desperately need a large immediate capital sum to clear debt, fund medical care, or make lifestyle purchases, making the DC pot highly attractive.
*   **Longevity Expectations:** If the member expects to live a very long time, retaining the compounded DB increases is almost always mathematically superior. 
*   **Desire for Simplicity:** Maintaining a DB pension is effortless. Managing a separate DC drawdown pot introduces complex investment decisions, ongoing advisory fees, and stress in late old age.
*   **Tax Efficiency:** Drawing from the newly created DC pot might push the pensioner into a higher marginal income tax band, eroding the value of the exchange.

## Question 2

### Part (i) [8 marks]

**1. Purpose of Assumptions:**
*   Assumptions are necessary to project the timing and magnitude of future benefit cash flows over many decades.
*   They are used to discount those future cash flows to determine a present value (the 'Technical Provisions' or funding target), allowing the scheme to summarize liabilities into a single figure to compare against current assets.
*   Assumptions are required by law/regulation and form the basis of the actuarial control cycle.

**2. Margin for Prudence:**
*   Funding assumptions intentionally are not "best estimates" (a 50/50 chance of being right). They include a margin for prudence. This deliberate caution increases the likelihood that sufficient assets will be accumulated, protecting member benefits.

**3. Financial Assumptions (High Importance):**
*   *Discount Rate:* **Extremely important.** It reflects the expected return on scheme assets (or a risk-free rate plus a premium). Because liabilities stretch decades into the future, a change of just 0.5% in the discount rate drastically changes the liability value.
*   *Price Inflation (RPI/CPI):* **Very important.** Drives both the revaluation of deferred pensions and increases to pensions in payment. Highly sensitive.
*   *Pension Increases:* Derived from price inflation but adjusted for scheme-specific rules (e.g., Limited Price Indexation caps and collars).

**4. Demographic Assumptions:**
*   *Mortality / Longevity:* **Very important.** Consists of a base table describing current death rates and an allowance for future improvements in life expectancy. People living longer than projected creates massive strain on the scheme.
*   *Proportions Married / Age Difference:* Required to value contingent spouse's pensions. **Lower importance** compared to mortality or discount rates.
*   *Early/Late Retirements and Withdrawals:* Assumptions on when members will take their benefits. **Moderate to low importance**, particularly in a closed scheme where active withdrawal is no longer a factor.

### Part (ii) [6 marks]

**Reply to the Trustee:**

*   **Acknowledge Uncertainty:** It is fundamentally correct that predicting demographic and economic trends precisely over an 80-year horizon is impossible. Individual assumptions will inevitably prove to be 'wrong' when compared to actual future experience.
*   **Prudence mitigates Risk:** We do not aim to be perfectly 'right' on average; we deliberately aim for prudence. By building in safety margins, we reduce the risk that being 'wrong' results in catastrophic underfunding. 
*   **The Actuarial Control Cycle:** A valuation is not a static, one-and-done guess. It is part of a dynamic steering mechanism. We perform valuations regularly (typically every three years). 
*   **Emerging Experience:** When actual experience deviates from our assumptions, the variance emerges as a measurable gain or loss. We then recalibrate the assumptions and adjust ongoing funding plans (e.g., increasing deficit recovery contributions) to bring the scheme back on target.
*   **The Alternative is Unmanageable:** Without standardized actuarial assumptions, the scheme would operate blindly. This would inevitably lead to either reckless under-resourcing (endangering member security) or punitive, trapped over-resourcing (harming the sponsor's business). Assumptions provide a rational, legal, and auditable framework for budgeting.

### Part (iii) [10 marks]

**(a) Why different discount rates are used pre- and post-retirement [3 marks]**
*   The discount rates directly reflect the underlying dual investment strategy of a traditional DB scheme. 
*   **Pre-retirement:** The liability cash flows are far in the future, so the scheme invests in 'growth' or return-seeking assets (like equities) to generate higher returns, justifying a higher discount rate.
*   **Post-retirement:** Pensions are actively being paid, requiring stable, predictable, and liquid asset backing. The scheme invests heavily in 'matching' assets (like gilts and corporate bonds) which have lower expected returns, justifying a lower discount rate.

**(b) How a yield curve approach works [3 marks]**
*   Instead of summarizing complex future cashflows with one or two flat percentage rates, a yield curve approach maps every single future projected cash flow to its exact corresponding point on a market yield curve.
*   The actuary determines the projected benefit payout for Year 1, Year 2, Year 3... up to Year 80+.
*   The cash flow for Year *n* is then discounted down to its present value using the specific zero-coupon bond yield with a maturity of exactly *n* years. The total liability is the sum of all these individual present values.

**(c) Advantages and disadvantages compared to the current method [4 marks]**
*   **Advantages:**
    *   Theoretically superior and highly accurate, as it perfectly matches the term structure of interest rates to the exact timing of scheme liabilities.
    *   Automatically handles unusual economic anomalies, such as an inverted or humped yield curve, where a flat average rate would distort value.
*   **Disadvantages:**
    *   Computationally more intense and complex. 
    *   It is much harder to communicate a "curve" to non-technical trustees than a single percentage number.
    *   Long-term yields (30+ years) suffer from low market liquidity. Extrapolating the long end of the curve can introduce artificial, theoretical volatility into the valuation.

### Part (iv) [7 marks]

**Impact of the scientific discovery on valuation assumptions:**

*   **Mortality (Base Table):** This is the most direct demographic impact. The exposure to the carcinogen strongly suggests that the life expectancy of the affected factory workers will be significantly reduced compared to standard population tables. The actuary must strengthen the mortality assumption (i.e., assume heavier mortality rates) for this specific sub-group.
*   **Granularity of Membership:** The aggregate scheme assumption is no longer appropriate. The actuary must stratify the scheme data, applying the heavier cancer-adjusted mortality table to the factory workers, and standard tables to unexposed members (e.g., office staff).
*   **Ill-Health Retirements:** The scheme is likely to see a sharp spike in ill-health early retirements as the cancer manifests. Ill-health pensions are typically unreduced and highly valuable. The assumed rate of ill-health decrement must be increased.
*   **Dependent benefits:** Increased mortality in service or early retirement may trigger different mixes of spouse's and dependents' benefits, impacting cash flow timing.
*   **Employer Covenant and Discount Rate:** While financial assumptions aren't directly linked to cancer, the massive impending industrial injury compensation claims could severely weaken the sponsor's financial health (the covenant). Faced with a weakened sponsor, the trustees would be advised to increase the margin for prudence in the discount rate (i.e., lower the rate) to ensure deeper scheme self-sufficiency, which increases the liabilities.

### Part (v) [1 marks]

Two possible approaches to calculating a suitable transfer value:
1.  **Share of Fund:** Transferring a pro-rata slice of the scheme's actual physical assets, proportional to the transferring members' liabilities.
2.  **Actuarial Liability Value:** Calculating a specific money amount based on the ongoing Technical Provisions (funding) basis. 

### Part (vi) [5 marks]

**From Scheme A's Trustees' perspective:**

*   **Approach 1: Share of Fund**
    *   *Advantage:* Mathematically guarantees that the funding level (Assets/Liabilities) of Scheme A remains entirely unaffected. The remaining members are perfectly insulated from the transfer.
    *   *Disadvantage:* The value of the transfer fluctuates daily with market movements until the transfer date. If Scheme A is heavily in deficit, Scheme B and the transferring members may reject the low value, triggering complex industrial relations disputes.
*   **Approach 2: Actuarial Liability Value (Technical Provisions)**
    *   *Advantage:* Provides a stable, auditable cash figure that perfectly mirrors the established funding target of the benefits being transferred out.
    *   *Disadvantage:* If Scheme A is underfunded (e.g., 80% funded), paying out 100% of the transferring members' Technical Provisions value will strip a disproportionate amount of cash from the scheme. This actively worsens the funding level for the remaining members unless Company A simultaneously injects balancing capital.

### Part (vii) [8 marks]

**Assumptions for bulk transfer calculations vs. funding valuation:**

*   **General Principle:** While funding valuations are deliberately prudent to protect benefits, bulk transfer bases are commercial transactions. They are typically negotiated to be on a "Best Estimate" basis (removing margins for prudence) to find a neutral exchange value between the willing buyer and seller.
*   **Discount Rate:** To remove prudence and reflect a neutral value, the transfer discount rate will usually be higher than the funding discount rate (e.g., closer to a best estimate expected return, or based on risk-free gilts flat), resulting in a lower comparative liability value.
*   **Allowance for Benefit Increases:** Funding bases often project inflation with an added premium. The transfer basis will strip this out, using strict market break-even inflation curves without arbitrary conservative padding.
*   **Longevity:** This is crucial. A funding valuation might use aggregate, smoothed assumptions for efficiency. However, because real capital is exiting the scheme, the transfer basis *must* explicitly use demographic assumptions tailored to the precise 15% cohort leaving. Because this sub-cohort comprises the factory workers exposed to the carcinogen, their assumed life expectancy must be significantly lower than the Scheme A average. 
*   **Proportions Married / Age Difference:** Funding valuations frequently rely on standard national averages. For a bulk transfer of this size, the actuary should depart from assumptions and rely on the actual, interrogated administration data of the specific transferring members to price the spouse benefits exactly.

### Part (viii) [3 marks]

**Change to part (vii) if the raw material was NEVER used in the smaller factory:**

*   The demographic risk profile completely reverses. The 15% transferring cohort (the unexposed, smaller factory) now represents the healthiest lives in the scheme. 
*   Therefore, the **longevity assumption used for the transfer** must assume normal, longer life expectancy (standard mortality tables), which would significantly *increase* the transfer value demanded from Scheme A compared to the scenario in part (vii).
*   Conversely, the concentrated risk is left entirely behind. The ongoing funding valuation assumptions for all remaining Scheme A members must be heavily adjusted downwards to reflect the concentrated, severe mortality risk of the carcinogen-exposed workers left behind.
