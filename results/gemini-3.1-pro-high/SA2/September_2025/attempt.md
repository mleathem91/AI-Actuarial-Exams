## Question 1

### Part (i) [20 marks]

**Issues Company A would need to consider when simplifying administration arrangements by moving products onto a smaller number of systems:**

**Strategic and Operational Planning**
*   **Objectives:** The company must establish clear objectives for the system migration, such as cost reduction, improved customer service, or enhanced data security.
*   **Project scope and timeline:** A clear roadmap is needed, detailing which systems are migrating first, the overall timeframe, and key milestones to minimize disruption. 
*   **Budgeting and costs:** The migration will incur significant upfront costs (IT development, staff training, potential penalties for early termination of outsource agreements). A robust cost-benefit analysis is required to justify the investment against long-term savings.
*   **Resource allocation:** Subject matter experts from various departments (IT, actuarial, operations, legal) will be required. Backfilling their regular roles might be necessary.
*   **Risk management:** A comprehensive risk register should be developed, identifying risks such as data loss, system downtime, or regulatory breaches, along with appropriate mitigation plans.

**System and IT Considerations**
*   **Target system selection:** The chosen "smaller number of systems" must be capable of handling the diverse range of legacy products, including complex historical features or guarantees.
*   **Data migration and integrity:** Transferring data from multiple old systems (and external administrators) to the new systems must be done securely and accurately. Extensive data cleansing and mapping will be needed before migration.
*   **System integration:** The new admin systems must interface seamlessly with other critical systems, such as accounting, actuarial valuation models, and customer portals.
*   **Testing:** Rigorous testing (User Acceptance Testing, parallel running) is vital before full cut-over to ensure calculations (e.g., surrender values, maturity payouts) are correct.

**Product and Actuarial Complexities**
*   **Legacy product features:** Legacy products often have unique rules, historical guarantees, or complex unit-pricing mechanisms. Translating these perfectly into a new system can be extremely challenging and prone to error.
*   **With-Profits funds:** The three separate with-profits funds will have their own Principles and Practices of Financial Management (PPFM), bonus declaration methods, and historical asset shares. The administration system must be able to ring-fence and accurately administer these funds simultaneously but separately, if they are not being merged.
*   **Unit-linked administration:** Unitizing processes, bid-offer spreads, and historical charging structures must be replicated or carefully simplified without disadvantaging policyholders.
*   **Actuarial valuation:** The transition requires ensuring that the data extracts needed for statutory reporting (Solvency II, IFRS 17) remain accurate and consistent throughout and after the migration. 

**External Parties and Legal/Regulatory Aspects**
*   **Outsourcing agreements:** If existing external outsourcing contracts are terminated early, there may be substantial exit fees or legal disputes. The transition clauses in these contracts must be reviewed carefully.
*   **Regulatory notification:** The regulator (e.g., PRA/FCA in the UK) may need to be informed of a major operational change. Ensuring compliance with conduct rules (e.g., Consumer Duty, treating customers fairly) during and after the migration is paramount.
*   **Data protection (GDPR):** Transferring large volumes of policyholder data naturally increases the risk of data breaches. Strict adherence to data privacy laws must be maintained.

**Customer and Staff Impacts**
*   **Policyholder communication:** Policyholders should be kept informed if the transition impacts them (e.g., changes to policy numbers, new customer portals, potential temporary service delays). It is essential to manage customer expectations and minimize complaints.
*   **Staff training:** Staff will need thorough training on the new consolidated systems. There may also be staff redundancies or required role changes, necessitating careful HR management to maintain morale and retain key knowledge.
*   **Customer service disruption:** Parallel running or phased cut-overs should be used to minimize the period where customer service is degraded. 

### Part (ii) [10 marks]

**Considerations for Company A when deciding what simplifications may be made to future terms and conditions for in-force business:**

*   **Treating Customers Fairly (TCF) / Consumer Duty:** The overarching principle is that simplifications must not lead to poor customer outcomes. Any simplification must be fair and transparent to the policyholder.
*   **Financial neutrality or enhancement:** Ideally, any change to charges or guaranteed benefits should be financially neutral to the policyholder, or provide an enhancement. For example, moving to a simple annual management charge (AMC) should, on average, not result in policyholders paying significantly more over the lifetime of their policy than under the complex structure.
*   **Policyholder guarantees:** Existing guarantees (e.g., guaranteed annuity rates, guaranteed minimum death benefits) are strictly binding. A company generally cannot simplify these away without offering fair compensation or an inducement, which requires explicit policyholder consent.
*   **Communication and consent:** Significant changes to terms and conditions typically require policyholder consent or at least a 30-day (or longer) notification period, depending on local regulations and the original policy document wording. Explaining complex changes (like moving from bid/offer spreads to a single AMC) in clear, plain language can be difficult.
*   **Legal limitations:** The original policy terms and conditions must be reviewed by legal experts to determine the extent to which Company A has the unilateral right to alter charges or terms.
*   **System capability:** The simplification intends to make administration easier. However, the new system must be able to support the transition mechanism (e.g., converting historical unit holdings into a new simplified unit structure).
*   **Impact on profitability and capital:** Actuaries must model the financial impact on Company A. Will an AMC-only structure result in lower profit margins compared to the old structure? How will this impact Solvency II Best Estimate Liabilities (BEL) and the Risk Margin? A reduction in the variability of future charges might decrease capital requirements, but a reduction in the sheer volume of charges would reduce future profits.
*   **Tax implications:** Simplifications that alter the fundamental nature of the policy or trigger a "chargeable event" could have adverse tax consequences for the policyholder. 
*   **Regulator view:** The regulator may need to approve or review the proposed changes, especially if they are widespread across large blocks of business. They will scrutinize the fairness of the translation from old to new terms.
*   **Reputational risk:** If simplifications are perceived as a way for the company to stealthily increase charges or remove valuable benefits, it could lead to negative press and reputational damage.

### Part (iii) [6 marks]

**Possible reasons for Company A's decision not to merge the three with-profits funds:**

*   **Cost and complexity:** Merging with-profits funds is an incredibly complex, costly, and time-consuming legal process. It typically involves a Court process (e.g., a Part VII transfer in the UK) with the need for Independent Experts, extensive regulatory scrutiny, and significant legal fees. 
*   **Differing asset mixes and investment strategies:** The three funds likely have very different historical asset allocations, risk profiles, and investment performance histories. Merging them safely without cross-subsidization between different cohorts of policyholders is mathematically and practically very difficult.
*   **Different inherited estates / working capital:** The funds may have vastly different levels of inherited estate. Permitting policyholders from a "poor" fund to share in the estate of a "rich" fund is inequitable. Ring-fencing the estates is often easier than trying to fairly distribute them upon merger.
*   **Differing bonus philosophies and PPFMs:** Each fund will have its own Principles and Practices of Financial Management (PPFM), with different rules on smoothing, smoothing accounts, and target payout ratios (e.g., 100% of asset share vs. 90% of asset share). Reconciling these fundamentally different philosophies is highly challenging.
*   **Policyholder opposition:** Policyholders (and their advocates/regulators) in a financially strong fund will almost certainly oppose a merger with a weaker fund, fearing a dilution of their future bonuses or security.
*   **Tax considerations:** A formal merger of funds can sometimes trigger unexpected tax liabilities for the company or alter the tax status of certain policyholder benefits depending on jurisdiction.
*   **Sufficient critical mass:** If the three funds are individually still large enough to achieve economies of scale in investment management and administration, the primary driver for a merger (cost savings) may not be strong enough to justify the immense effort required.

## Question 2

### Part (i) [6 marks]

**Key risks the company is exposed to with respect to the income protection (IP) business:**

*   **Morbidity (Sickness) Risk:**
    *   **Incidence rates:** The risk that the frequency of policyholders falling ill and claiming is higher than expected.
    *   **Recovery/Termination rates:** The risk that claim durations are longer than expected (i.e., sick policyholders take longer to recover or die than anticipated). Both sub-risks lead to higher overall claim payouts.
*   **Expense Risk:** The risk that the costs of writing, administering, and particularly managing claims for the IP business are higher than expected. Claim management for IP is notably resource-intensive due to the need for medical assessments and rehabilitation support.
*   **Lapse/Persistency Risk:**
    *   **Selective withdrawals:** The risk that healthy policyholders lapse their policies, leaving a pool of less healthy lives, concentrated morbidity risk.
    *   **High early lapses:** The risk of failing to recoup initial acquisition and underwriting costs if policies lapse too soon.
*   **Economic / Interest Rate Risk:** IP policies have long durations and claims are paid as annuities over many years. Reinvestment and discounting risks are significant. Additionally, economic downturns strongly correlate with increased IP incidence rates and decreased recovery rates (moral hazard).
*   **Operational Risk:** The risk of errors in the complex underwriting and claims management processes, or system failures disrupting the payment of regular monthly benefits.

### Part (ii) [8 marks]

**Why the company's economic capital (EC) requirements are lower than its regulatory capital requirements (RC):**

*   **Confidence Level and Time Horizon:** Regulatory capital (e.g., Solvency II SCR) often aims for a strictly defined confidence level over a 1-year horizon (e.g., 99.5% VaR). The company's internal EC model might utilize a different confidence level deemed appropriate by internal management, or a multi-year time horizon that allows for management actions over time, resulting in a lower required capital buffer.
*   **Diversification Benefits:** The regulatory standard formula (or even constrained internal models) may prescribe specific, conservative correlation matrices between risks. The company's internal EC model might capture greater diversification benefits between morbidity risk (IP), mortality risk (unit-linked), and market risks based on company-specific historical data, thus lowering the capital requirement. 
*   **Treatment of the Risk Margin:** Solvency II requires a Risk Margin, calculated using a highly prescribed cost-of-capital approach, which is often criticized as overly conservative. The economic view might use a lower cost of capital, allow for more diversification in the run-off, or exclude the risk margin entirely if it views the BEL as fundamentally sufficient on a realistic basis.
*   **Management Actions:** The EC framework may take a much more realistic, dynamic view of future management actions during stress events (e.g., dramatically increasing the AMC on unit-linked products, closing to new business, altering bonus rates if applicable). Regulators often restrict the extent to which management actions can be modeled in regulatory capital.
*   **Asset Calibration:** The regulatory regime might impose a very severe, "one-size-fits-all" stress on certain asset classes (e.g., equities, property). The internal EC model might use more tailored economic scenario generators that reflect a slightly less severe view of extreme tail events for the specific assets the company holds.
*   **Matching Adjustment / Volatility Adjustment:** If the company uses illiquid assets for its IP business, the regulatory framework may restrict the benefit of illiquidity premiums. The EC model might take a more realistic economic view of the yield it can earn and the matched nature of the cashflows, discounting liabilities at a higher rate and requiring less capital.
*   **Purpose:** Regulatory capital must ensure policyholder protection across the whole industry under a prescribed lens. Economic capital is a management tool for optimizing risk-adjusted returns; management may simply have a higher risk appetite than the regulator dictates.

### Part (iii) [10 marks]

**How climate change could impact the key risks faced by the company:**

*   **Morbidity Risk (Income Protection):**
    *   **Physical risks:** Increased frequency of extreme weather events (heatwaves, floods) can directly impact policyholder health, leading to increased IP claims. Heatwaves can cause severe respiratory or cardiovascular issues preventing work.
    *   **Vector-borne diseases:** Changing climates alter the geographic spread of diseases (e.g., tick-borne, mosquito-borne), potentially introducing new illnesses that result in long-term disability claims.
    *   **Mental health:** "Climate anxiety" and the stress of adapting to severe weather disruptions or job losses in transitioning sectors could materially increase mental health-related IP claims, which are historically difficult to manage and have low recovery rates.
*   **Mortality Risk (Minimum guarantees on Unit-Linked):**
    *   **Physical risks:** Extreme weather directly causes fatalities. While the company writes savings products, a spike in mortality means paying out the guaranteed minimum death benefit earlier than expected. If asset values are down due to the same climate shock, the guarantee bites harder.
*   **Market / Investment Risk (Impacting both, but severely impacting UL AMC revenues):**
    *   **Transition risks:** The global shift to a low-carbon economy will strand assets in carbon-intensive sectors. If the company's funds hold these assets, sharp drops in unit values will occur.
    *   **Impact on AMC:** A significant fall in the value of the unit-linked funds directly reduces the Annual Management Charge revenue, destroying future profitability.
    *   **Physical risks on assets:** Direct damage to property investments, or lower GDP growth driven by climate impacts, will result in lower overall investment returns.
*   **Expense Risk:**
    *   Rising inflation driven by supply chain disruptions (climate-induced) or carbon taxes will increase the general administration and claims handling expenses for both product lines. 
*   **Lapse Risk:**
    *   Economic hardship caused by local climate events or broader economic transition shocks may force policyholders to surrender their unit-linked savings to access cash, or cease paying premiums on IP policies.
*   **Operational Risk:**
    *   Physical damage to the company's own offices or those of its IT service providers from extreme weather could lead to severe business interruption, preventing the collection of premiums or payment of claims.

### Part (iv) [6 marks]

**How the company could mitigate the potential climate risks:**

*   **Investment Strategy Adjustments (Transition Risk mitigation):** The company should transition its unit-linked fund offerings and its own shareholder/IP backing assets toward ESG-compliant or low-carbon funds. Actively divesting from stranded assets protects both AMC revenue and the balance sheet.
*   **Reinsurance:** For the IP business, the company could purchase stop-loss or quota share reinsurance to cap the downside of sudden spikes in morbidity claims caused by climate-related events (e.g., pandemics, regional disasters).
*   **Underwriting and Pricing Adjustments:** 
    *   The company could refine IP underwriting to ask about geographic location to assess physical risk, or occupation to assess transition risk (e.g., is the applicant in a carbon-intensive industry at risk of job loss and subsequent mental health claim?).
    *   Pricing could be adjusted to include a larger risk margin for climate uncertainty.
*   **Product Design:** For future unit-linked products, offering lower or dynamically adjusting minimum death guarantees would reduce the associated mortality/market cross-risk.
*   **Operational Resilience:** Enhancing disaster recovery and business continuity plans. Moving servers to the cloud, enabling remote work capabilities, and diversifying the geographic location of key outsourced providers.
*   **Monitoring and Scenario Testing:** Regularly monitoring emerging climate data against morbidity experience. Continually performing climate scenario analysis to understand vulnerabilities and trigger management actions early.

### Part (v) [4 marks]

**Why scenario testing may be more appropriate than stochastic modelling to assess the potential impact of climate change:**

*   **Lack of Historical Data:** Stochastic models rely heavily on historical data to calibrate volatilities and correlations. Climate change is an unprecedented, forward-looking phenomenon. Past data is not a reliable predictor of future climate shocks, making stochastic calibration highly speculative and potentially misleading.
*   **Deep Uncertainty:** The pathways of climate change (e.g., global coordinated policy action vs. complete inaction) involve deep, structural uncertainty. Scenario testing is specifically designed to explore discrete, plausible "what-if" futures (e.g., exactly tracking a 1.5°C Paris-aligned transition vs. a 4°C "hothouse world" scenario). 
*   **Complex Interactions:** Climate change involves complex interactions between physical science, economics, political policy, and human behavior. Stochastic models struggle to capture these non-linear, compounding relationships. Narrative-driven scenario testing can logically link, for example, a new carbon tax policy directly to stranded assets and local job losses (morbidity risk). 
*   **Communication:** Scenario results are much easier to explain to the Board and regulators. Saying "Under a delayed disorderly transition scenario, our capital cover falls by 20%" is an actionable statement, whereas "The 99.5th percentile stochastic climate loss is X" is harder to conceptualize and acting upon it is difficult when the underlying distribution is unverified.

## Question 3

### Part (i) [3 marks]

**Likely IFRS 17 measurement models for each product:**

*   **Non-profit annuities:** **General Measurement Model (GMM)** (also known as the Building Block Approach). Annuities have long durations and highly predictable cash flows, but no link to underlying items. They do not qualify for the Variable Fee Approach (VFA) or the Premium Allocation Approach (PAA).
*   **Non-profit term assurances:** **Premium Allocation Approach (PAA)** or **General Measurement Model (GMM)**. If the term is short (e.g., 1 year) it automatically qualifies for PAA. If the term is longer, they may still use PAA if they can prove the measurement is not materially different from GMM. Otherwise, GMM will be used. Given standard term assurances, GMM is the most robust standard, though PAA is desired for simplicity.
*   **Unit-linked endowment assurance (with death benefit):** **Variable Fee Approach (VFA)**. This product features a clear link to underlying assets (the units), the company pays the policyholder an amount equal to a substantial share of the fair value returns on the underlying items, and the company expects a substantial proportion of any change in the amounts to be paid to vary with the change in fair value of the underlying items.

### Part (ii) [7 marks]

**Reasons for differences between Solvency II BEL and IFRS 17 BEL at YE24 for non-profit products:**

*   **(50) / (40) Expenses:** 
    *   IFRS 17 only allows the inclusion of expenses that are *directly attributable* to fulfilling the insurance contracts. 
    *   Solvency II takes a broader view and typically includes a wider allocation of overheads within the maintenance expense assumption. 
    *   Therefore, the IFRS 17 maintenance expense assumption is lower, resulting in a lower BEL. The effect is proportional to the duration and volume of the business.
*   **– / – Demographic assumptions:** The premise states best estimate assumptions are identical except for RDR and expenses. Therefore, no difference arises from mortality, longevity, or lapsation assumptions. We use the same central estimate.
*   **(30) / (50) Risk Discount Rate (RDR):** 
    *   **Solvency II:** Uses the EIOPA mandated risk-free yield curve, potentially including a Credit Risk Adjustment (CRA) and a Matching Adjustment (MA) or Volatility Adjustment (VA). 
    *   **IFRS 17:** Requires a discount rate that reflects the characteristics of the insurance contracts (liquidity characteristics) and is consistent with observable current market prices. This relies on an entity-specific "bottom-up" (risk-free + illiquidity premium) or "top-down" (reference portfolio yield - credit risk) approach.
    *   The transition shows a negative impact, meaning the IFRS 17 discount rate is *higher* than the Solvency II discount rate. For example, if the company uses a "top-down" approach for IFRS 17, the resulting illiquidity premium might be greater than the EIOPA standard Volatility Adjustment allowed under Solvency II. A higher discount rate results in a lower present value of liabilities. The longer duration of term assurance relative to mature annuities might explain the proportionally larger absolute impact on term.
*   **– / – Inflation:** Both regimes typically derive expense inflation consistently from market-implied inflation curves, leading to no material difference.

### Part (iii) [8 marks]

**How changes in mortality and mortality improvement assumptions impact SI and IFRS 17 results:**

*(Assuming for discussion that the underlying business is predominantly negatively impacted, i.e., strengthening reserves, though the question says directional element is not required).*

**Solvency II Impacts:**
*   **Best Estimate Liability (BEL):** Changing the base mortality table or future improvement factors immediately changes the projected cashflows. The new expected cashflows are discounted back, resulting in a direct corresponding change in the BEL. 
*   **Own Funds:** Because the BEL changes, the Net Asset Value (Assets minus Liabilities) changes immediately. An increase in BEL leads to a direct reduction in Solvency II Own Funds.
*   **Risk Margin (RM):** The Risk Margin is recalculation based on the new, updated profile of the run-off of the SCR. Changes in longevity or mortality risk assumptions will subtly alter the required capital profile over time, thus causing a secondary change to the RM.
*   **Solvency Capital Requirement (SCR):** The demographic stresses (e.g., 20% increase in mortality rates) are now applied to the new, updated base assumptions, altering the absolute size of the required capital buffer.

**IFRS 17 Impacts (General Measurement Model for Non-Profit):**
*   **Present Value of Future Cashflows (PVFCF):** Just like the Solvency II BEL, changing the mortality assumptions directly updates the expected cashflows, changing the PVFCF component of the fulfilment cashflows.
*   **Risk Adjustment (RA):** The RA reflects the compensation the entity requires for bearing non-financial risk. Changing the base probability distribution of mortality alters the perceived risk, leading to an updated RA measurement.
*   **Contractual Service Margin (CSM):** *This is the key difference.* Under IFRS 17, changes in assumptions related to *future* service (like changing future expected mortality rates) do NOT immediately impact profit or equity (Own Funds). Instead, the change in the fulfilment cashflows (PVFCF + RA) is absorbed by the CSM. 
    *   If liabilities increase, the CSM decreases. 
    *   Profit is only impacted if the CSM is driven below zero (creating an onerous contract, recognizing a loss immediately). 
    *   Thus, the assumption change is amortized into the P&L over the remaining life of the cohort via the CSM unwinding, avoiding volatility in current equity.

### Part (iv) [9 marks]

**Suitable refinements to the simplified roll-forward approach for HY25 IFRS 17 (Non-Profit):**

The actuary's concern is that simply projecting the YE24 reserves forward ignores actual experience and market movements that occurred during the 6 months to HY25.

**Refinements:**
*   **Refine the Discount Rate (Economic variances):** The most material driver over 6 months is likely changing interest rates. The company should calculate a new, exact IFRS 17 discount rate curve for HY25. They can then estimate the change in the BEL by calculating the duration, or DV01, of the liabilities at YE24 and applying the change in the yield curve mathematically without a full cashflow run.
*   **True-up for Actual Volumes (New Business):** The projection must not just be of the in-force book at YE24. Any new policies written between Jan 1 and June 30 must be accounted for. The company could apply an average "BEL per policy" or scaled reserve factor based on recent pricing models for the new business.
*   **True-up for Actual Decrements (Experience Variances):** Simply assuming expected deaths and lapses occurred is inaccurate. The company must compare actual deaths/lapses against expected up to HY25. If actual lapses were much higher, the projected BEL must be manually adjusted downward heavily to prevent over-reserving.
*   **Adjust for Actual Inflation/Expenses:** Check if actual maintenance expenses incurred align with the projection. Given recent high inflation volatility, an adjustment to the short-term inflation assumption applied to the projected reserves might be necessary.
*   **Recalculation of CSM Unwind:** The amount of CSM released to profit over the 6 months must be recalculated using the actual coverage units provided during the period, not just the expected coverage units from the YE24 projection.
*   **Materiality Checks:** Perform a high-level check on the total asset value vs. the estimated liabilities. Ensure no major "shocks" (like a massive single claim) occurred that fundamentally break the linearity assumptions of a roll-forward.
*   **Interpolation Models:** Rather than a simple straight-line projection, use slightly more sophisticated techniques like Taylor expansions based on the YE24 model outputs (Delta/Gamma relative to interest rates and mortality scaling).

### Part (v) [3 marks]

**Why analyzing the movement in assets to estimate BEL and CSM (as proposed for unit-linked) may not be appropriate for non-profit business:**

*   For unit-linked business (measured under the VFA), the benefits paid to the policyholder are directly linked to the fair value of the underlying assets (the units). Therefore, tracking the movement in the asset value provides a highly accurate proxy for the movement in the liability (BEL) and heavily influences the CSM movement.
*   For non-profit business (measured under GMM), the liabilities are strictly defined by the policy conditions (e.g., a fixed 1M term life payout, or a 10,000/year annuity) and are completely detached from the specific assets backing them. 
*   If the assets backing the non-profit book perform brilliantly, the non-profit BEL does not increase at all. Movement in the assets merely reflects shareholder profit/loss and asset-liability mismatch, providing absolutely zero information about the movement in the underlying insurance liabilities or changing mortality/expense risk factors. Thus, the asset movement is irrelevant for estimating the GMM fulfilment cashflows.
