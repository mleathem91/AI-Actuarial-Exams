## Question 2

**Answer:** A

A decrease in the cost of production increases supply, shifting the supply curve to the right. This places downward pressure on price and upward pressure on quantity.
A fall in consumer income for a normal good decreases demand, shifting the demand curve to the left. This places downward pressure on price and downward pressure on quantity.
The combined effect on price is an unambiguous fall, while the net effect on quantity will be indeterminate, depending on the relative magnitude of the two shifts.

## Question 4

**Answer:** C

A profit-maximising firm produces where Marginal Revenue (MR) = Marginal Cost (MC). Because MC is generally a positive number, MR must also be positive at the profit-maximising output level. 
For a downward-sloping linear demand curve, MR is only positive when demand is price elastic (elasticity < -1). If the firm is producing in the inelastic region (where MR is negative), raising the price will decrease output, reducing total costs while increasing total revenue, thus unambigously increasing profit. The firm will thus keep raising price until it reaches the elastic region.

## Question 27

**(i) Budget line and the effect of an increase in income**
A budget line graphically maps out the maximum affordable combinations of two goods that a consumer can purchase, given their fixed budget (income) and the fixed market prices of the goods. 
If the consumer's income increases (while prices remain constant), the budget line will undergo a parallel outward shift away from the origin. This represents an expansion of the consumer's purchasing power, allowing them to purchase larger quantities of both goods.

**(ii) Indifference curve and convexity**
An indifference curve is a graph outlining various combinations of two goods between which a consumer receives the same level of total utility (satisfaction). The consumer is "indifferent" to any bundle lying on the same curve.
It is typically convex to the origin due to the principle of the Diminishing Marginal Rate of Substitution (MRS). As a consumer progressively acquires more of Good X and has less of Good Y remaining, the additional marginal utility derived from further units of X falls, while the marginal utility of the increasingly scarce Good Y rises. Therefore, the consumer is willing to give up less and less of Good Y to obtain an additional unit of Good X.

## Question 28

**(i) Main features of an oligopolistic market**
- **Few large dominant firms:** The market is highly concentrated, with a small number of large firms controlling the vast majority of market share.
- **High barriers to entry:** Substantial obstacles (such as economies of scale, patents, or high set-up costs) prevent new firms from entering.
- **Mutual interdependence:** The strategic decisions of one firm (regarding pricing, output, or marketing) will significantly impact the profits of rival firms, triggering reactions.

**(ii) Preference for non-price competition**
Oligopolies tend to avoid price competition because aggressive price cuts will be quickly matched by rivals, triggering a destructive price war that erodes profit margins across the entire industry without necessarily shifting market share.
Instead, they prefer non-price competition to differentiate their product and shift their demand curve to the right without lowering prices. Examples include:
- Branding and advertising campaigns to build customer loyalty.
- Product differentiation through superior quality or product innovation.
- Offering extended warranties or premium after-sales customer service.
- Point-based loyalty schemes.

At this artificially high wage floor, the supply of labour (workers willing to work) significantly exceeds the demand for labour (workers farms can afford to hire), resulting in a surplus of unemployed workers.

## Question 30

**(i) Economies of scale in the aircraft industry**
Economies of scale occur when the long-run average costs of production decrease as the scale of output increases. 
In the aircraft industry, they benefit firms by allowing them to spread their massive initial investments and overheads over a very large number of manufactured planes, continually driving down the unit cost per aircraft as production volume expands.

**(ii) Fixed cost example**
The billions of dollars spent on the Research and Development (R&D) and engineering design phase prior to a single aircraft being manufactured.

**(iii) Impact on the competitive landscape**
Because the minimum efficient scale (where unit costs are minimised) is extraordinarily high, a firm must capture a massive share of the global market to survive. This heavily limits the number of firms the industry can profitably support, resulting in a natural oligopoly or duopoly (e.g., Airbus and Boeing). The enormous scale required acts as an almost insurmountable barrier to entry for new competitors.

## Question 31

**(i) Barriers to entry**
Barriers to entry are the obstacles, sunk costs, or legal hurdles that prevent or deter new competitors from easily entering an industry to compete with incumbent firms.
Two examples are:
1. **Statutory barriers:** Legal protections granted by the government, such as patents on pharmaceuticals, which forbid rivals from copying an invention for a set period.
2. **High sunk capital costs:** Industries that require massive non-recoverable initial investments (e.g., building telecommunication networks) make entry incredibly risky and difficult to finance.

**(ii) Effect on short-term and long-term profitability**
**(a) Monopolistic competition:** 
There are very low or zero barriers to entry. In the short term, incumbent firms may be able to earn supernormal profits due to product differentiation. However, the lack of barriers means these profits quickly act as a signal to attract new entrants. As new firms flood the market, incumbent's market share reduces, eroding these profits until only normal profits are made in the long term.
**(b) Oligopoly:**
There are very high barriers to entry. In the short term, oligopolists can exploit their market power to earn significant supernormal profits. Crucially, because the barriers to entry prevent new rival firms from entering the market, the incumbents' market share is protected, allowing them to sustain and enjoy these supernormal profits into the long term.

## Question 33

**(i) Methods to appreciate the currency**
1. **Direct market intervention:** The central bank can use its accumulated foreign exchange reserves to directly purchase its own domestic currency on the forex market, artificially shifting the demand curve for the currency to the right.
2. **Raising interest rates:** By increasing domestic interest rates, the country becomes a more attractive destination for "hot money" savings. International investors will demand the currency to deposit funds into the higher-yielding domestic banks, driving up its exchange rate.

**(ii) Implications of appreciation**
- **Balance of payments:** A stronger currency means the nation's exports become relatively more expensive to foreign buyers, whilst imported goods become cheaper for domestic consumers. Provided the Marshall-Lerner condition holds (the sum of price elasticities of exports and imports is > 1), this will worsen the current account balance on the balance of payments.
- **Inward Foreign Direct Investment (FDI):** An appreciated currency makes it considerably more expensive for foreign firms to translate their capital into the domestic currency to acquire physical assets or build factories. This higher initial hurdle cost typically causes the level of inward FDI to fall.

## Question 34

**(i) Household and firm interaction in the circular flow of income**
The circular flow model represents the continuous movement of money and resources between two primary economic agents: households and firms.
1. Households own the fundamental factors of production (labour, land, capital, and enterprise) and supply them to the firms.
2. In exchange for utilising these resources, the firms pay factor incomes back to the households in the form of wages, rent, interest, and dividends.
3. Finally, the households use this income to fund consumption expenditure, buying the goods and services produced by the firms, thereby returning the money back to the firms and completing the circle.

**(ii) Government influence on the circular flow**
1. **Taxation:** The government extracts direct and indirect taxes from households and firms. This acts as a withdrawal (leakage) from the flow, dampening consumption and AD.
2. **Government Expenditure:** The government injects money into the circular flow by purchasing goods and services from firms (e.g., infrastructure) or providing transfer payments (e.g., welfare) to households, boosting overall demand.

**(i) Opportunity costs of producing Good X**
In Country A: 2000 / 400 = 5. The opportunity cost of 1 Good X is 5 units of Good Y.
In Country B: 1400 / 200 = 7. The opportunity cost of 1 Good X is 7 units of Good Y.

**(ii) Comparative advantage in Good Y**
Country B has the comparative advantage in Good Y. (The opportunity cost of 1Y in B is 1/7X, which is lower than the opportunity cost of 1Y in A, which is 1/5X).

**(iii) Exported by Country A**
Country A will export Good X, because it holds the comparative advantage in producing it (as its opportunity cost of 5Y is lower than B's cost of 7Y).

**(iv) Mutually beneficial terms of trade**
For trade to be mutually beneficial, the price of Good X must lie perfectly between the two nations' domestic opportunity costs (between 5Y and 7Y). 
Therefore, exactly 6 whole units of Good Y per unit of Good X will be beneficial for both.

**(v) TRUE or FALSE**

## Question 36

**(i) Primary role of mutual funds**
Mutual funds serve a primary role by pooling together the savings of thousands of smaller retail investors. They then use this massive aggregate capital to purchase a highly diversified portfolio of financial securities (like equities and bonds) administered by professional portfolio managers, offering retail investors risk distribution and expertise they could not achieve individually.

**(ii) Two key functions of banks**
1. **Financial Intermediation:** They act as an efficient conduit bridging the gap between economic agents with surplus funds (savers) and economic agents requiring funds (borrowers), overcoming asymmetric information.
2. **Maturity Transformation:** They transform highly liquid, short-term liabilities (individuals' easily accessible current account deposits) into illiquid, long-term assets (capital-intensive business loans and 25-year mortgages). 

**(iii) Importance of insurance companies and pension funds**
- **Financial stability:** Because they are managing money with very long-term horizons (like a 30-year pension), they are not susceptible to sudden "bank runs". This allows them to act as mammoth institutional investors, providing a reliable, stable supply of long-term capital into government infrastructure and corporate bond markets, reducing systemic volatility. 
- **Individual financial security:** Insurance companies offer protection by pooling risk, allowing individuals to survive catastrophic financial shocks (such as a house fire or sudden illness) without descending into poverty. Pension funds allow individuals to smooth their lifetime consumption, ensuring that even after they cease earning an income, they maintain a secure standard of living throughout retirement.

## Question 37

**(i) Methods of product differentiation**
To alleviate intense price competition, a mobile phone company can implement a multifaceted non-price differentiation strategy:
- **Physical Features & Hardware Specifications:** The company can invest heavily in R&D to provide demonstrably superior physical features. This could include unparalleled camera lens technology, significantly longer battery life, unique form factors (such as a foldable screen), or the use of premium, lightweight materials (like titanium frames).
- **Software Ecosystem & Lock-In:** A company can differentiate via its proprietary operating system and interconnected services. By providing seamless synchronisation with laptops, smartwatches, and cloud data—and creating exclusive app platforms (e.g., Apple's ecosystem)—they create a unique user experience that makes it highly difficult for consumers to switch to a competitor. 
- **Aesthetics & Brand Image:** The company can leverage massive marketing campaigns to cultivate a premium, lifestyle-orientated brand identity. By associating the phone with status, luxury, or avant-garde innovation, consumers will view the product as fundamentally superior to technically similar, unbranded rivals.
- **Customer Experience & After-Sales Service:** They can differentiate by ensuring the customer journey is flawless. This could involve offering rapid, in-person repair services in dedicated retail stores, extending warranty periods, or providing 24/7 technical support.

**(ii) Payoff matrix and Game Theory analysis**
Let us construct a simplified payoff matrix for the duopoly, assuming both firms face identical costs and demand structures. The numbers in the brackets represent the hypothetical monetary profits in the format `(Profit of Company A, Profit of Company B)`.

| | Mobile Company B: High Price | Mobile Company B: Low Price |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Mobile Company A: High Price** | (100, 100) | (20, 150) |
| **Mobile Company A: Low Price** | (150, 20) | (50, 50) |

**Analysis of the Matrix:**
- **The Collusive Outcome:** If both companies could trust each other and collude to set a High Price, they would maximise their joint industry profits, earning 100 each. 
- **The Incentive to Cheat:** Unfortunately, there is a massive unilateral incentive to defect. If Company B sets a High Price, Company A's purely rational "best response" is to undercut them by setting a Low Price. By doing so, A steals vast market share and increases its profit to 150. Symmetrically, Company B faces the exact same temptation. 
- **Self-Preservation:** If Company B behaves aggressively and sets a Low Price, Company A's best response is again to set a Low Price. If A naively kept a High Price, their sales would collapse, and their profit would plummet to 20. By matching the Low Price, A secures a profit of 50. 
- **The Nash Equilibrium:** Regardless of what Company B decides to do, Company A always earns a higher profit by choosing the Low Price. Therefore, setting a Low Price is a **strictly dominant strategy** for both firms. 
- **The Result (Prisoner's Dilemma):** Because both firms arrive at the logical conclusion that they must set a Low Price to protect themselves and attempt to steal market share, they both end up in the bottom right quadrant. The final Nash Equilibrium yields profits of just (50, 50). Despite acting rationally as individual entities, both mobile phone providers end up far worse off than if they had managed to maintain the mutually beneficial High Price strategy.
