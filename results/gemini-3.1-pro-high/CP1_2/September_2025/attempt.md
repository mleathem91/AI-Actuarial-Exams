## Question 1

### Part (i) [5 marks]

The global insurance company could have used a formal Enterprise Risk Management (ERM) framework, encompassing the following steps:

1. **Risk Identification:** Systematically identifying the risks faced globally and locally, particularly the exposure to extreme weather events (e.g., hurricanes, floods, wildfires) in specific geographical regions relative to the properties and lives insured. They could have used brainstorming, expert opinion, and catastrophe models.
2. **Risk Measurement and Assessment:** Quantifying the frequency, severity, and correlation of these weather events. This involves using historical data and forward-looking climate models to assess the potential financial impact (probable maximum loss) on each subsidiary and the aggregate group.
3. **Risk Appetite and Tolerance:** The board should have defined the maximum level of extreme weather risk the company is willing to accept (appetite) and the absolute limits it can survive (tolerance) at both the group and subsidiary levels.
4. **Risk Mitigation and Management:** Implementing strategies to manage the identified risks. For extreme weather, this typically involves taking out appropriate catastrophe excess of loss reinsurance, diversifying the geographic spread of the portfolio to avoid risk concentration, or tightening underwriting criteria (e.g., excluding flood cover in high-risk zones).
5. **Risk Monitoring and Reporting:** Continuously observing weather patterns, tracking accumulations of exposure in specific peril regions, and reporting this up to the board against the defined risk appetite.
6. **Risk Control:** Establishing mechanisms to enforce limits, such as halting new business underwriting in a region if a certain exposure threshold is breached.

### Part (ii) [5 marks]

**Reaction of the Financial Regulator:**
* **Intervention and Investigation:** The regulator will act immediately to investigate the causes of the insolvency to see if there were breaches of capital requirements or poor management practices.
* **Protecting Policyholders:** The regulator's primary concern will be the policyholders who are left without cover or with unpaid claims. They may intervene to manage the run-off or attempt to transfer the policies to another insurer.
* **Systemic Contagion:** They will assess if the insolvency poses any systemic risk to the local financial market.
* **Loss of Trust:** The regulator may view the parent company as an unreliable operator and may restrict licenses for foreign-owned insurers or demand higher local capital requirements in the future.

**Reaction of the Government:**
* **Political Fallout:** The government will face public outcry, especially if the extreme weather created a national disaster and the insurer is refusing to pay. They will likely be highly critical of the parent company "abandoning" the country.
* **Compensation Schemes:** The government may have to step in as the insurer of last resort or trigger a national compensation scheme, transferring the cost to taxpayers or a levy on remaining solvent insurers.
* **Legislative Action:** The government may pass stricter laws regarding foreign ownership, capital ring-fencing, or mandatory domestic reinsurance requirements to prevent a recurrence.
* **Diplomatic Pressure:** The government may try to exert pressure on the parent company's home regulator or government to enforce a bailout.

### Part (iii) [5 marks]

The board must consider a wide range of factors before deciding to declare the worst-affected subsidiaries insolvent:

* **Reputational Damage (Global):** Walking away from a subsidiary after a major loss event will cause catastrophic reputational damage to the parent brand globally. Customers and brokers in unaffected countries may cancel policies, fearing the parent won't pay out when needed.
* **Legal and Structural Realities:** Are there any parental guarantees in place? If the parent has explicitly or implicitly guaranteed the subsidiary's liabilities, winding it up won't protect the parent's capital. Furthermore, the corporate veil might be pierced by local courts.
* **Regulatory Retaliation:** Regulators in other jurisdictions where the company operates will notice this action. They may subsequently require the parent to hold significantly higher capital locally or force them to ring-fence assets, reducing the group's capital efficiency.
* **Financial Cost vs. Benefit:** The board must compare the immediate capital required to recapitalize the subsidiary against the long-term lost future profits, potential fines, and global loss of business from winding it up.
* **Social and ESG Responsibility:** Insurance relies on trust. Abandoning policyholders precisely when they have suffered catastrophic weather losses violates the core social purpose of insurance and negatively impacts ESG ratings, potentially alienating institutional investors.
* **Future Trend vs. Isolated Event:** Was this extreme weather a 1-in-200-year tail event, or does climate change mean this will become a regular occurrence making the subsidiary permanently unviable?

### Part (iv) [5 marks]

If the sponsoring employer is declared insolvent, the employer covenant becomes zero. The pension scheme managers (trustees) could take the following protective actions:

* **Claim as a Creditor:** The trustees should immediately lodge a claim against the assets of the insolvent subsidiary for the full pension deficit (often treated as an unsecured creditor).
* **Pursue the Parent Company:** The trustees should investigate whether they can claim against the parent company, either through explicit legal guarantees that were previously agreed upon or through regulatory powers (e.g., anti-avoidance powers to target entities connected to the employer).
* **Pension Protection/Compensation Schemes:** If the country has a national pension protection fund (like the PPF in the UK), the trustees should apply for entry to ensure members receive a guaranteed minimum level of benefits.
* **De-Risk the Investment Strategy:** With the employer gone, the scheme's ability to absorb investment losses is severely diminished. The trustees should immediately shift towards a lower-risk, cashflow-matching investment strategy (e.g., bonds over equities) to protect the remaining assets.
* **Review Benefits:** If the scheme is severely underfunded and no external support is available, the trustees may have to make the difficult decision to halt future accruals, remove discretionary increases, or legally reduce accrued benefits, depending on the scheme rules and local legislation.
* **Secure Benefits (Buyout):** If the scheme's funding level permits, the trustees could seek to secure the members' benefits by purchasing annuities from a solvent insurance company (a buyout) and subsequently winding up the scheme.

### Part (v) [5 marks]

To ensure ongoing viability against extreme weather outsets, the subsidiary could take the following actions:

* **Strengthen Reinsurance:** Purchase more robust reinsurance cover, such as catastrophe excess of loss arrangements with lower attachment points or higher limits, or consider alternative risk transfer like catastrophe bonds.
* **Tighten Underwriting Controls:** Introduce stricter guidelines to limit geographic concentration. For example, introduce aggregate exposure limits for specific postal codes vulnerable to floods or hurricanes.
* **Revise Policy Terms and Conditions:** Introduce specific exclusions for certain severe weather perils, increase the deductibles/excesses for weather-related claims, or introduce co-insurance where the policyholder shares a percentage of the loss.
* **Re-price the Risk:** Significantly increase premium rates for properties located in high-risk zones to accurately reflect the true, potentially changing frequency and severity of extreme weather events indicated by climate models.
* **Diversify the Portfolio:** Attempt to grow the business in commercial lines or life/health insurance, or expand into geographical areas within the country that are less exposed to these specific weather events, diversifying the overall risk profile.
* **Encourage Risk Mitigation:** Offer premium discounts to policyholders who invest in making their properties more resilient (e.g., flood defenses, reinforced roofs), thereby actively reducing the underlying physical risk.

## Question 2

### Part (i) [6 marks]

The travel company might be considering buying cruise ships and expanding into cruise holidays for several strategic reasons:

* **Vertical Integration and Margin Capture:** By owning the ships rather than just selling other companies' cruises as an agent, the company can capture the entire profit margin of the holiday experience.
* **Diversification:** Adding cruises diversifies their product portfolio. It protects them against a downturn in land-based resort holidays and provides a new revenue stream.
* **Customer Demand and Growth:** The cruise sector might be experiencing high growth or strong demographic tailwinds (e.g., an aging population with high disposable income). Expanding meets this customer demand.
* **Leveraging Existing Customer Base:** The company already has a huge database of successful holidaymakers. They can easily cross-sell these new cruise holidays to their existing, loyal customers with low acquisition costs.
* **Control Over Quality:** Owning the assets gives the company absolute control over the quality of the service, the food, and the itineraries, allowing them to better protect and enhance their brand reputation compared to relying on third-party operators.
* **Economies of Scope and Scale:** They can utilize their existing marketing, booking systems, and customer service infrastructure, spreading fixed corporate costs over a wider revenue base.
* **Packaging Opportunities:** They can easily create fully integrated "fly-cruise" packages using their existing flight and hotel networks, offering a seamless experience.

### Part (ii) [8 marks]

Running cruise ships introduces several significant new risks to the company:

* **Capital and Financing Risk:** Cruise ships require massive upfront capital expenditure. Funding this through debt introduces significant interest rate risk and requires consistent massive cash flows to service the debt.
* **Operational Risk:** Operating a ship is highly complex compared to running a travel agency. The company lacks maritime expertise, leading to risks of mechanical failures, poor maintenance, or crewing issues.
* **Health and Safety/Pandemic Risk:** The enclosed environment of a ship is highly susceptible to the rapid spread of diseases (e.g., Norovirus, COVID-19), leading to quarantine, aborted trips, and massive refund liabilities.
* **Maritime and Catastrophe Risks:** Ships are exposed to extreme weather, collisions, grounding, or even piracy. The loss of a ship would result in huge physical, liability, and potentially loss of life claims.
* **Fuel Price Volatility:** A cruise line is highly exposed to fluctuations in global oil prices. A spike in fuel costs can severely erode profit margins if ticket prices cannot be adjusted quickly enough.
* **Reputational Risk:** A high-profile incident (such as an outbreak of illness or a maritime accident) on their own branded ship will cause severe reputational damage that could bleed into their broader travel agency business.
* **Regulatory and Environmental Risk:** The maritime industry faces strict international regulations (e.g., safety at sea, employment law in international waters) and increasing environmental scrutiny regarding emissions and waste dumping, which could lead to massive fines.
* **Demand and Economic Risk:** Cruises are luxury discretionary spending. An economic downturn will severely reduce demand, but the massive fixed costs of the ships remain, leading to heavy losses.

### Part (iii) [8 marks]

The company would likely use a combination of methods to determine how much to charge for its cruises:

* **Cost-Plus Pricing:** The company must establish a baseline price that covers the significant variable costs per passenger (food, port taxes, fuel) and contributes towards the enormous fixed costs (ship depreciation, debt servicing, crew salaries, insurance), plus a target profit margin.
* **Competitor Analysis:** The company will heavily research established cruise operators offering similar itineraries and ship qualities. Since they are a new entrant, they may initially offer penetration pricing (undercutting competitors) to gain market share and fill the ships.
* **Yield / Revenue Management:** This is critical for fixed-capacity assets like ships. Prices will be dynamic. Initial base fares may be high, but as the departure date approaches, any unsold cabins will be heavily discounted (marginal pricing) because an empty cabin generates zero revenue while a heavily discounted one at least generates onboard spending.
* **Value-Based Pricing:** If the company offers unique itineraries, exceptional luxury, or exclusive shore excursions that competitors cannot match, they can charge a premium price based on the perceived value to the customer.
* **Segmentation by Cabin Class:** Prices will be heavily tiered. Interior, windowless cabins will be priced lowest to attract budget-conscious travelers, while large balcony suites will command massive premiums from wealthier segments.
* **Unbundling vs. All-Inclusive:** They must decide the pricing structure. They could advertise a low base ticket price but charge heavily for onboard extras (alcohol, specialty dining, excursions, Wi-Fi) to maximize aggregate revenue.
* **Seasonality Profiling:** Prices will be determined by demand peaks. Cruises during school holidays or peak summer periods will be priced much higher than "shoulder season" sailings.
* **Market Testing/Price Elasticity Studies:** Utilizing their existing customer database to survey willingness-to-pay and testing different price points to understand how sensitive demand is to price changes.

### Part (iv) [4 marks]

The company could fund the expansion through:

* **Internal Resources (Retained Earnings):** Using cash reserves accumulated from past successful trading years.
* **Equity Finance:** Issuing new shares to raise capital (e.g., a rights issue to existing shareholders or an IPO if not currently listed).
* **Debt Finance:** Securing large bank loans or issuing corporate bonds secured against the future value of the ships themselves.
* **Leasing or Chartering:** Instead of buying the ships outright, they could lease them (bareboat or time charter) from existing maritime owners, reducing the massive upfront capital requirement.

### Part (v) [4 marks]

Benefits the company could offer to its employees (both onshore and maritime) include:

* **Retirement Provision:** Access to a defined contribution or defined benefit pension scheme.
* **Health and Wellbeing:** Private medical insurance, dental cover, or an Employee Assistance Programme (EAP).
* **Travel Perks:** Heavily discounted holidays, cheap flights, or free off-season cruise cabins for employees and their families.
* **Financial Protection:** Life assurance (death-in-service benefits) and income protection cover in case of long-term illness or injury.

### Part (vi) [4 marks]

Issues the company may encounter when providing these benefits:

* **Cost and Affordability:** Funding comprehensive benefits (especially pensions and health cover) can be very expensive and volatile, straining profit margins.
* **Cross-Border Complexity and Regulation:** The company operates globally and ships are in international waters. Different tax laws, mandatory state provisions, and employment regulations make a uniform benefits package legally complex or impossible to administer.
* **Equity Between Workforce Segments:** Offering different benefits to onshore office staff versus maritime crew (who might be recruited from lower-wage economies) could cause resentment, low morale, or union disputes.
* **Valuation and Administration Burden:** Tracking eligibility, handling claims for a mobile global workforce, and valuing liabilities (like long-term pension commitments) requires significant HR and actuarial resources.

### Part (vii) [4 marks]

Risks covered under a travel insurance product typically include:

* **Medical Expenses:** Costs of emergency medical treatment, hospital stays, and potentially repatriation flights back to the policyholder's home country due to illness or injury abroad.
* **Cancellation and Curtailment:** Reimbursing the cost of the holiday if the customer has to cancel before departure or cut the trip short due to unforeseen covered events (e.g., sudden illness, bereavement).
* **Baggage and Personal Belongings:** Compensation for lost, stolen, or damaged luggage, passports, or expensive personal items during the trip.
* **Travel Delay or Missed Departure:** Providing funds for accommodation or alternative transport if flights or cruises are severely delayed (e.g., due to extreme weather or strikes).

### Part (viii) [5 marks]

The company could manage the risks relating to this new product by:

* **Strict Underwriting Guidelines:** Exclude high-risk scenarios, such as travelling against government advice, participating in extreme sports without paying an additional premium, or pre-existing medical conditions (unless declared and specifically rated).
* **Imposing Policy Limits and Excesses:** Cap the maximum payout (e.g., £5,000 for baggage) and apply an excess/deductible so the policyholder bears the first portion of any claim, reducing small attritional claims.
* **Reinsurance:** Purchase quota share or excess of loss reinsurance to protect against an accumulation of claims (e.g., a major incident at a resort) or extremely large single claims (e.g., a complex air ambulance repatriation).
* **White-Labelling / Partnership:** Rather than underwriting the risk themselves, act as an intermediary and partner with an established, capitalized insurance company who takes on the underwriting risk. The travel company just takes a commission on the sale.
* **Specialist Claims Handling:** Outsource claims management to specialized Third Party Administrators (TPAs) who have global networks to negotiate medical bills, manage repatriations efficiently, and detect fraud.

### Part (ix) [4 marks]

Suitability of using the company's own data to price the travel insurance product:

* **Helpful for Exposure Management:** The data is excellent for knowing *who* the customers are (age, family size) and *where* they are going (destination, duration, activity type), which are key rating factors.
* **Lack of Claim Frequency Data:** The primary issue is that the travel company has no data on how often these customers actually fall ill, lose their bags, or cancel their trips.
* **Lack of Claim Severity Data:** Even if a customer had to cancel, the travel company wouldn't know the medical costs incurred abroad or the cost of repatriations, making it impossible to calculate a burning cost.
* **Risk of Mispricing:** Without historical claims data, pricing will be based heavily on guesswork, leading to a high risk of underpricing (resulting in severe financial losses) or overpricing (resulting in zero sales against established competitors).

### Part (x) [4 marks]

Issues of using the purchased data from the defunct insurance company:

* **Data is Outdated:** The data stops 3 years ago. Travel patterns, the cost of medical care (health inflation), and the risk landscape (e.g., post-pandemic travel behavior) will have changed significantly, making the data irrelevant.
* **Differences in Target Market:** The defunct insurer may have targeted totally different demographics (e.g., young backpackers) compared to the travel company's customer base (e.g., older, affluent cruise-goers), meaning the frequency and severity underlying the data are incompatible.
* **Unknown Policy Wordings:** The historical claims were paying out based on the defunct insurer's specific terms, conditions, and exclusions. If the travel company designs a product with different limits or tighter exclusions, the past claims data won't accurately map to the new product.
* **Data Quality and Reason for Exit:** The company needs to question *why* the insurer stopped writing this business. If it was due to poor pricing, inadequate data systems, or massive losses, using their flawed data will doom the new product to the same fate.

## Question 3

### Part (i) [4 marks]

The actuary should request the following information from Company X:

* **Strategic Objectives and Timeframe:** Why specifically Country B, and what are the precise market share, volume, and profit targets they wish to achieve within the 12-month window?
* **Available Capital and Risk Appetite:** How much initial capital is the board willing to commit to this expansion, and what level of initial loss or volatility are they willing to tolerate?
* **Intended Product Offering and Pricing:** Do they intend to sell the exact same vehicle and breakdown products, or will they be tailored for Country B? What is their initial pricing strategy?
* **Internal Capabilities and Resources:** Does Company X have existing staff with knowledge of Country B's language, legal system, market dynamics, and claims landscape, or will they need to hire heavily or outsource?

### Part (ii) [4 marks]

Four expansion options for Company X:

1. **Establish a New Subsidiary:** Incorporate a brand-new, legally distinct insurance company localized entirely within Country B.
2. **Establish a Branch Office:** Operate in Country B as an extension of the Country A entity, assuming the new trade regulations allow for branch operations without local incorporation.
3. **Acquire a Local Insurer:** Buy an existing, established insurance company in Country B to gain immediate market share, licenses, and local expertise.
4. **Joint Venture / Strategic Alliance:** Partner with a local company (e.g., a local bank or supermarket) to sell Company X's products using the local partner's distribution network and brand recognition.

### Part (iii) [4 marks]

The actuary could identify risks and dependencies by:

* **Consulting Local Experts:** Engaging local actuaries, lawyers, or market consultants based in Country B to understand the nuanced local risks, claims culture, and competitor behavior.
* **Internal Risk Workshops:** Holding brainstorming sessions with senior management across underwriting, claims, and IT to map out the operational and strategic dependencies required to launch within 12 months.
* **Scenario Analysis and Stress Testing:** Developing adverse scenarios (e.g., failing to gain any market share, a catastrophic surge in early claims) to identify hidden dependencies on parent company capital.
* **Regulatory Review:** Conducting a detailed desktop review of Country B's specific insurance regulations, capital rules, and tax legislation to identify compliance risks.

### Part (iv) [4 marks]

The regulatory regime in Country B could impact success in the following ways:

* **Capital Constraints:** If Country B's regulator dictates high minimum capital requirements or uses a very conservative valuation methodology, it will lock up more of Company X's capital, reducing the overall return on expansion.
* **Product and Pricing Restrictions:** The regulator may impose strict price controls, demand a review of all premium rate changes, or forbid the use of highly predictive rating factors (e.g., age or gender), making it difficult to price risks profitably in competitive segments.
* **Speed to Market:** The regulatory approval process to obtain a license, approve policy wordings, and clear new directors might take longer than the planned 12 months, severely delaying the growth strategy.
* **High Compliance Costs:** Adhering to dual regulatory regimes (A and B) will require duplicating compliance, reporting, and actuarial functions, creating a massive operational drag on early profitability.

### Part (v) [4 marks]

The actuary can use models to assess additional capital requirements by:

* **Stochastic Financial Modeling:** Building an ALM or dynamic financial analysis (DFA) model that simulates thousands of scenarios for claim frequencies and severities in Country B, alongside investment returns, to understand the distribution of potential capital depletion.
* **Mapping to Regulatory Formulae:** Applying Country B's specific regulatory capital models or standard formula to the projected premium and reserve volumes to strictly calculate the statutory minimum capital needed.
* **Stress and Scenario Testing:** Running extreme deterministic scenarios through the capital model (e.g., a 1-in-200-year local storm causing mass vehicle damage, or a sudden legal change inflating bodily injury claims) to determine the capital buffer required.
* **Calculating Diversification Benefits:** Using correlation matrices within an internal group capital model to assess if expanding into Country B provides a diversification benefit against Country A risks, which might lower the overall capital intensity of the project.

### Part (vi) [4 marks]

Company X would assess success after 12 months by measuring:

* **Financial Metrics vs Plan:** Comparing actual premium written, earned premium, and incurred claims against the original business plan. Calculating the loss ratio to see if pricing assumptions were accurate.
* **Capital Efficiency:** Assessing the actual capital consumed by the expansion versus the initial forecast, and measuring the early Return on Capital Employed (ROCE) or calculating the internal rate of return against the hurdle rate.
* **Market Share and Penetration:** Looking at the volume of policies sold to determine if they successfully captured their target percentage of Country B's vehicle insurance market.
* **Operational Effectiveness:** Reviewing whether the expansion was delivered within the 12-month timeframe on budget, and assessing the performance of local distribution channels and claims handling efficiency (e.g., speed of claim settlement in Country B).
