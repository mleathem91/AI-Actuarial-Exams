## Question 13

**Difficulties in Predicting a Post-Rights Issue Share Price [5]**

- **Market Perception of the Use of Funds:** While the theoretical ex-rights price (TERP) provides a mathematical baseline, the actual price will be heavily influenced by how the market perceives the purpose of the issue. If the funds are for a high-NPV project, the price may rise above TERP; if they are for a financial rescue, the price may fall below.
- **General Market Movements:** Capital markets are dynamic. The macroeconomic environment or the performance of the chosen market sector may change significantly during the subscription period, independent of the rights issue.
- **Signalling Effects:** A rights issue can be interpreted as a negative signal (e.g., management believes the current share price is overvalued or the company cannot secure debt). This can arbitrarily depress the price.
- **Take-Up Uncertainty / Overhang:** If a large portion of existing shareholders do not take up their rights and sell them, or if underwriters have to purchase a massive block of unwanted shares, the resulting supply "overhang" can artificially depress the market price.
- **Changes to Capital Structure:** The newly raised equity alters the company’s gearing ratio, which could change its Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC) and overall risk profile, affecting valuation models used by analysts.

## Question 14

**Advantages and Disadvantages of Non-Recourse Factoring for Company K [5]**

**Advantages:**
- **Immediate Improved Cash Flow:** Non-recourse factoring quickly converts trade receivables into cash, providing immediate liquidity for Company K to cover operational costs (like salaries) without waiting for client payments.
- **Risk Transfer:** Because the factoring is non-recourse, the factor bears the risk of bad debts. If a client defaults, Company K does not have to repay the advance.
- **Reduced Administrative Burden:** Company K no longer needs an internal credit-control department for factored invoices, allowing the owners to focus on their core actuarial consulting services.
- **Easier Qualification than Loans:** Factoring depends largely on the creditworthiness of Company K’s clients rather than its own nascent financial history, making it easier for a newly created business to access finance.

**Disadvantages:**
- **High Cost:** Non-recourse factoring is an expensive form of short-term finance. The factor will charge a premium fee for taking on the bad-debt risk and managing credit control.
- **Loss of Client Relationship Control:** The factor will take over the collection of receivables. Aggressive or insensitive collection tactics by the factor could severely damage the consulting firm’s relationships with its clients.
- **Signalling Effect:** Clients might perceive the use of a factoring service as a sign that Company K is financially unstable, making them hesitant to engage the firm for critical, long-term consulting.
- **Applicability to Services:** Factoring is often more suited to tangible goods than services. In actuarial consulting, clients might dispute an invoice based on advice quality or scope changes; a factor could refuse to bear this specific "performance dispute" risk.

## Question 15

**Disadvantages of Company G’s Approach (12% Target Return) [5]**

- **Ignores Project-Specific Risk:** Utilizing a single, flat 12% discount rate across all projects fails to account for varied risk profiles. High-risk projects should have a higher discount rate, whilst low-risk projects should have a lower one.
- **Rejection of Safe and Essential Projects:** Crucial, low-risk investments (like equipment replacement or mandatory compliance upgrades) might be incorrectly rejected if their guaranteed but modest returns fall below the arbitrary 12% hurdle.
- **Acceptance of Excessively Risky Projects:** High-risk projects that promise a 13% return might be accepted because they clear the hurdle, even if their risk-adjusted cost of capital should properly be evaluated at 18%.

- **Does Not Capture Qualitative Value:** An inflexible quantitative NPV focus fails to capture unquantifiable strategic benefits, such as environmental improvements, staff morale, or brand enhancement.

## Question 16

**Recommendation Regarding Geared or Ungeared Beta [5]**

**Recommendation:** The investor should use Company T’s **geared beta** (also known as the equity beta). 

**Reasons:**
1. **Target Investment Type:** The individual is assessing an investment in Company T's *equity shares*, making them a prospective shareholder.
2. **Measurement of Relevant Risk:** Shareholders rank behind debt holders in the event of liquidation and therefore bear the full brunt of the financial risk introduced by the firm’s debt (gearing).
3. **Difference Between Betas:** The *ungeared (asset) beta* reflects only the fundamental business/operating risk of the company's assets, assuming it had no debt. The *geared (equity) beta* reflects both this underlying business risk *and* the additional financial risk imposed by the specific capital structure.
4. **Application in CAPM:** To calculate the required rate of return (cost of equity) using the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) to evaluate the equity shares, the investor must incorporate all systematic risks faced by those shares. Therefore, the geared beta is the appropriate parameter.

## Question 18

**Evaluation of the Policy of Investing in High Dividend Yield Companies [5]**

**Advantages:**
- **Income Stream:** The policy guarantees a steady and passive stream of cash flow, making it particularly advantageous for investors requiring regular income, such as retirees.
- **Indicator of Maturity and Stability:** Companies with high dividend yields are often established, mature businesses with strong, reliable cash-generating capabilities and proven business models, inherently carrying less business risk than growth stocks.
- **Reduced Agency Costs (Discipline):** Paying out large amounts of cash systematically reduces the free cash flow available to managers, restricting their ability to invest in value-destroying "pet" projects or empire-building.

**Disadvantages:**
- **The "Value Trap":** A high dividend yield (Dividend / Share Price) is frequently the result of a collapsing share price, suggesting the market expects the company is in severe distress and that the dividend is unsustainable and about to be cut.
- **Limited Growth Potential:** By distributing cash to investors, these companies retain significantly less capital for reinvestment into new, positive NPV projects, leading to low capital appreciation over the long term.
- **Tax Inefficiency:** Depending on the investor’s jurisdiction, dividend income may be taxed at a significantly higher marginal rate than long-term capital gains, making this policy highly tax-inefficient compared to holding growth stocks.

### (ii) Usefulness of Valuation of PPE at Cost Less Accumulated Depreciation [5]

- **Objectivity and Reliability:** Valuing at historical cost is highly objective because the figures can be independently verified via purchase invoices, eliminating management bias or subjective estimation.
- **Cost Effectiveness:** It completely bypasses the significant financial costs, time, and practical difficulties associated with commissioning regular professional revaluations of complex manufacturing assets.
- **Matching Principle:** Systematically depreciating an asset over its designated useful life appropriately matches the expense of using the equipment against the economic benefits (revenues) it helps the company generate within the same accounting periods.
- **The Prudence Concept:** By not anticipating unrealized gains, it acts as a conservative safeguard against overstating the asset base and equity of the company.
- **Limitations for Users:** However, it can become highly disconnected from the true economic reality. For example, some property might heavily appreciate in market value, or specific equipment could be affected by rapid technological obsolescence, making the balance sheet an inaccurate reflection of the firm's true current worth or replacement capacity.

## Question 20

### (i) Agency Issues Arising from the Board's Decision (Rights Issue vs. Debt) [6]

- **Managerial Risk Aversion:** Managers typically desire job security and are averse to financial distress. Issuing equity carries no mandatory servicing costs (dividends are optional), whereas debt enforces rigid, periodic interest and principal payments that heighten bankruptcy risk if the product fails. Thus, managers may selfishly prefer a rights issue to insulate themselves from financial pressure, despite debt heavily benefiting shareholders through the tax shield.
- **Dilution of Ownership and EPS:** If current shareholders choose not to take up their rights (e.g., due to a lack of immediate liquidity), their ownership percentage and voting power will be permanently diluted. A debt issue would completely avoid this dilution.
- **Avoidance of Lender Scrutiny:** Taking out further debt typically subjects the company to strict external monitoring from banks (e.g., restrictive debt covenants, rigorous probing of the new product's business plan). Raising equity via existing sympathetic shareholders allows management to sidestep this intense, highly disciplined external scrutiny over their pet project.

### (ii) Difficulties from Dividend Suspension and Recommended Response [8]

**Difficulties:**
- **The Clientele Effect:** Company D's existing shareholder base may be heavily composed of income-seeking investors (e.g., pension funds, retirees) who invested specifically for predictable dividend yields. Suspending the dividend fundamentally breaches their investment criteria, precipitating a widespread sell-off of the stock.
- **Negative Market Signalling:** The market fundamentally treats dividend cuts or suspensions as the ultimate signal of dire financial distress and pessimistic future earnings, regardless of the board's assertions that the cash is for an advertising campaign.
- **Depressed Share Price:** The combination of angry income investors liquidating their positions and the severe asymmetry of information resulting in negative signalling will likely crater the company's share price in the near term.

**Recommended Response:**
Company D must proactively implement an aggressive **Strategic Communication and Transparency Campaign** prior to formally announcing the suspension. 
- **Reasoning:** Management must release a meticulously detailed, highly publicized strategic plan to the market. This plan must quantitatively demonstrate the robust, positive net present value (NPV) of the new environmental formula and explicitly lay out how deploying the dividend capital towards this specific launch directly creates far superior long-term shareholder wealth. By providing transparent, detailed financial forecasts concerning when dividends are expected to resume—and indicating that they will logically be larger than before—management can credibly re-frame the suspension from a "distress signal" into a "temporary, high-return reinvestment signal." 

### (iii) Advantages and Disadvantages of Growth via this New Range [6]

**Advantages:**
- **Alignment with Consumer/Regulatory Trends:** Environmental sustainability is a massive macroeconomic trend. An eco-friendly product can capture significant new market share from conscious consumers while preemptively satisfying tightening future environmental legislation.
- **Premium Pricing and Branding:** An innovative "green" formula can often be positioned as a premium product, achieving higher profit margins while dramatically boosting Company D’s overarching corporate reputation and brand equity.
- **Diversification and Modernization:** It modernizes the company’s portfolio, moving it away from legacy, environmentally harsh chemicals that may face future punitive taxation or outright bans. 

**Disadvantages:**
- **Risk of Over-Capitalization:** There is tremendous upfront financial risk. The firm is raising expensive equity, loading up fixed costs with new equipment, and spending heavily on advertising. If the market rejects the new formula due to poor efficacy or high price points, the massive sunk costs will destroy company value.
- **Cannibalization Risks:** The high-profile launch and heavy advertising could simply persuade existing customers to switch from Company D’s traditional products to the new formula, heavily cannibalizing existing revenue streams without necessarily increasing aggregate market share.
- **Execution Risks:** Competitors with larger advertising budgets might swiftly reverse-engineer the "new formula" and smother Company D's launch, negating the value of the expensive R&D and equipment investments.
