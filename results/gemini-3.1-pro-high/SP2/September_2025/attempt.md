## Question 1

### Part (i) [6 marks]

The introduction of the new unit-linked single premium bond with a focus on ESG standards, which generated ten times the expected premium volume, exposes the company to several risks:

*   **Operational Risk:** The company's administrative systems and staffing levels may be inadequate to process the unexpected volume of applications. This could lead to delays in allocating premiums to units, administrative errors, and poor customer service.
*   **Investment / Sourcing Risk:** Since the funds are managed internally and restricted to ESG-compliant assets, the company may struggle to find sufficient suitable assets quickly. This could lead to a large uninvested cash balance (cash drag affecting returns) or the risk of "greenwashing" if the company lowers its standards to deploy the capital.
*   **Reputational Risk:** If the company fails to deliver administrative efficiency or breaches its stated ESG mandates, it could suffer severe reputational damage.
*   **Regulatory Risk / Mis-selling:** The extremely high sales volume might indicate that the product was mis-sold or aggressively marketed inappropriately (e.g., not properly explaining the risks of unit-linked investments), attracting regulatory scrutiny.
*   **Liquidity Risk:** Although unit-linked, if a large proportion of these policyholders decide to surrender simultaneously in the future (perhaps due to poor performance or an ESG scandal), the company may struggle to liquidate the underlying assets quickly to meet the withdrawal demands.
*   **New Business Strain:** Even for a unit-linked product, initial expenses (such as marketing, IT setup, and high initial commissions) may exceed the initial charges deducted from the single premiums. Accommodating ten times the expected volume could place a strain on the company's capital position.

### Part (ii) [6 marks]

Ways to manage the identified risks include:

*   **Operational Management:** Hire temporary staff, approve overtime for existing staff, and temporarily reallocate employees from other departments to assist with the application backlog. IT systems may need urgent scaling or upgrades.
*   **Investment Management:** Implement a temporary "soft closure" by delaying new business processing or heavily restricting further large single premiums until more suitable ESG assets can be sourced. Alternatively, transparently hold liquid assets or cash temporarily, ensuring this is permitted within the fund's Terms and Conditions.
*   **Regulatory and Mis-selling Mitigation:** Conduct immediate, independent audits of the sales and marketing channels (e.g., review intermediary practices or direct marketing materials) to ensure the product was sold appropriately and the target market was reached without misrepresentation.
*   **Capital Management:** If new business strain is a material issue, consider entering into a financing reinsurance arrangement to support the capital requirements, or inject further shareholder capital if available.
*   **Expectation Management:** Clearly communicate with the new policyholders about any potential initial delays in processing or unit allocation, and reiterate the investment philosophy and potential short-term cash holdings to prevent dissatisfaction.

## Question 2

### Part (i) [5 marks]

There are several possible reasons why the mortality experience on the term assurance portfolio is substantially higher than the pricing assumptions:

*   **Anti-selection due to Simplified Underwriting:** Standard pricing assumes normal or lightly impaired lives, but simplified underwriting often attracts individuals with known health issues who have been declined fully underwritten cover or prefer to avoid medical examinations.
*   **Distribution Channel Characteristics:** The internet-only sales route may have attracted a different target demographic than anticipated (e.g., higher risk occupations or lifestyles) who actively search for quick insurance solutions online.
*   **Inappropriate Pricing Basis:** The original pricing assumptions might have been overly optimistic, poorly extrapolated, or based on unsuitable standard population data rather than data specific to simplified underwriting products.
*   **Fraud and Non-disclosure:** Simplified underwriting, lacking the rigorous checks of medical reports, is highly susceptible to both deliberate fraud and innocent non-disclosure of medical conditions, leading to unexpected early claims.
*   **Systemic Event:** A discrete, wide-scale event, such as a localized epidemic or pandemic entirely unrelated to the product design, may have occurred over the last 3 years, causing a spike in population mortality.
*   **Statistical Fluctuation:** The portfolio is only 3 years old and may still be small. The higher mortality could simply be an adverse statistical fluctuation rather than indicative of a long-term trend.

### Part (ii) [6 marks]

Upon receiving indications of higher mortality, the company should consider the following actions:

*   **Detailed Analytics:** Perform a deeper investigation into the claims data to identify patterns. Are the excess claims concentrated in specific geographical areas, age profiles, or specific causes of death? This will help pinpoint the exact cause.
*   **Tighten Underwriting:** Immediately revise the simplified underwriting criteria. This could involve adding more targeted health questions to the online application form, or requiring full medical reports for sums assured above a new, lower threshold.
*   **Repricing New Business:** Increase the premium rates for all new term assurance business sold via this channel to reflect realistic expected mortality based on the emerging experience.
*   **Investigate Existing Claims:** Review recent death claims closely for signs of non-disclosure or misrepresentation at the application stage, and decline payout where the policy terms permit.
*   **Temporary Suspension:** If the financial impact is severe and cannot be immediately rectified, the company could temporarily withdraw the product from the internet channel until a full redesign and repricing is completed.
*   **Review Reinsurance:** Discuss the emerging experience with the reinsurer. Explore if reinsurance terms can be renegotiated or if higher levels of quota share or surplus risk can be ceded for future new business to limit downside exposure.
*   **Monitor Continually:** Establish more frequent (e.g., monthly) monitoring and reporting of mortality experience rather than waiting for long initial cycle reviews.

## Question 3

### Part (i) [9 marks]

For an expanding fund, the net unit movement is positive (more units are being created than cancelled). To maintain fairness and reflect the true cost of creating new units, the price paid per unit is calculated on an **offer basis**:

1.  **Start with the Underlying Assets:** The fundamental value of the fund begins with the total market value of all underlying investments. Because the fund is expanding and needs to purchase more assets, these should be valued at their **offer prices** (the market cost of acquiring them).
2.  **Add Uninvested Cash:** Include any cash currently held within the fund that has not yet been invested.
3.  **Add Purchasing Costs:** Add all necessary costs of dealing associated with purchasing new assets. This includes broker commissions, stamp duty, transaction taxes, and any other trading fees.
4.  **Accrue Income and Deduct Expenses:** Add any accrued income (such as dividends declared but not yet received, and accrued interest). Deduct any accrued management expenses and tax liabilities (such as capital gains tax provisions or income tax).
5.  **Calculate Fundamental Value:** Sum the above components. This gives the Total Net Asset Value (NAV) on an offer basis.
6.  **Calculate Base Unit Price:** Divide the Total NAV by the total number of units currently in issue to obtain the fundamental unit price (the base offer price).
7.  **Apply Spreads and Adjustments:** Depending on the product structure, the company may apply a bid/offer spread. The price paid by the new policyholder will be the "Offer Price" (or "Issue Price").
8.  **Management Charges:** If the company deducts its annual management charge by adjusting the unit price rather than cancelling units, this deduction is factored into the daily pricing formula.
9.  **Rounding:** The company will typically apply a rounding rule. For the offer price, the company usually rounds *up* (e.g., to the nearest 0.1 pence) to ensure it doesn't lose money on small fractions.

### Part (ii) [6 marks]

When unfavourable market movements result in encashments exceeding new investments for several consecutive days, the fund is now **contracting**.

*   **Shift to Bid Basis:** Because the fund needs to sell underlying assets to meet the net cash outflows, the assets must be valued on a **bid basis**.
*   **Asset Valuation:** The underlying assets are valued at the price they can currently be sold for in the market (the bid price).
*   **Deduct Selling Costs:** The costs associated with selling the assets (broker fees, taxes, transaction costs) are *deducted* from the asset value, rather than added.
*   **Impact on Unit Price:** The fundamental unit price is recalculated using this newly defined Total NAV divided by the units in issue. This naturally creates a much lower fundamental "bid" price.
*   **Price for New Policyholders:** New policyholders purchasing units will now do so based on this lower, contraction-based unit price.
*   **Fairness Argument:** It is crucial to switch the pricing basis to protect the *remaining* policyholders. If the pricing wasn't switched, departing policyholders would take more cash out than the assets could realize upon sale, diluting the fund and unfairly penalizing those who stay.

## Question 4

### Part (i) [2 marks]

The equating policy values method of calculating alteration terms involves setting the value of the policy immediately *after* the alteration equal to the value of the policy immediately *before* the alteration. 

Mathematically: $\text{Value of Benefits (After)} - \text{Value of Future Premiums (After)} = \text{Value of Benefits (Before)} - \text{Value of Future Premiums (Before)}$. These values are determined using a prospective valuation basis that is often realistic or slightly cautious.

### Part (ii) [8 marks]

The introduction of new terms where the altered policy is valued at 50% of its pre-alteration value, with the company retaining the other 50%, will have several impacts:

*   **Reduction in Alteration Volume:** As intended, the punitive terms will act as a significant deterrent. The volume of policyholders requesting alterations will drop dramatically once they are informed of the financial penalty.
*   **Expense Savings:** The reduced volume of alterations will directly result in lower administrative expenses, easing pressure on the company's back-office resources.
*   **Increase in Profitability per Alteration:** For those few policyholders who do proceed with an alteration, the company will secure a massive release of reserve/surplus, heavily contributing to profit for the period, as it only passes on 50% of the built-up value to the new contract.
*   **Customer Dissatisfaction and Reputational Damage:** These terms are exceptionally penal and hard to justify on a cost-recovery basis. Policyholders whose circumstances have genuinely changed will feel unfairly trapped or punished, leading to complaints, damaged brand reputation, and lost cross-selling opportunities.
*   **Increased Surrenders and Lapses:** If the company's standalone surrender values remain higher than 50% of the policy value, a massive arbitrage opportunity exists. Policyholders will simply opt to surrender their policies in full and use the proceeds to buy a new policy elsewhere (or even direct from the company), circumventing the alteration penalty completely.
*   **Regulatory Scrutiny:** Such a harsh penalty clearly risks breaching "Treating Customers Fairly" (TCF) or other consumer protection regulations. Regulators expect alteration terms to be equitable and broadly reflect the costs incurred, not to serve as an arbitrary confiscation of 50% of a policy's value.
*   **Valuation Model Changes:** Management will need to immediately update their pricing, valuation, and ALM models. Assumptions governing lapse rates, persistency, and the expected frequency of alterations must be completely overhauled, which may affect the reported value of in-force business (VIF).

## Question 5

### Part (i) [6 marks]

A life insurance company may use sensitivity analysis for several key purposes:

*   **Identify Key Risk Drivers:** To determine which specific assumptions have the most material impact on profitability or capital metrics if they deteriorate.
*   **Margin Setting:** To help establish the appropriate level of prudent margins to incorporate into pricing or valuation bases. Assumptions with high sensitivity usually require larger margins for adverse deviation.
*   **Product Design Optimization:** If an analysis shows the product is overly sensitive to a risk (e.g., investment return), the company may redesign the product to mitigate this (e.g., adding reviewable premiums, changing guarantees, or sharing the risk with the policyholder).
*   **Risk Management & Reinsurance:** To decide whether it is necessary to purchase reinsurance to cap exposure, or to adjust the ALM strategy if the interest rate sensitivity is too high.
*   **Management Information & Communication:** To present a range of plausible financial outcomes to the Board of Directors or regulators, rather than relying on a single deterministic "best estimate" projection.
*   **Capital Assessment:** To assist in understanding the capital requirements under various stress scenarios and contributing to the ORSA (Own Risk and Solvency Assessment) process.

### Part (ii) [7 marks]

For profit testing a whole of life assurance product using a deterministic model, the company should sensitivity test the following assumptions:

*   **Mortality Rates:** Since a whole of life policy fundamentally pays out upon death, it is highly sensitive to mortality. The company should test a uniform proportional increase in mortality (e.g., +10%), as well as changes to the 'shape' of the mortality curve (e.g., lighter mortality at younger ages but heavier at older ages). A mortality improvement/deterioration trend should also be tested.
*   **Investment Return (Discount Rate):** Whole of life policies naturally build up large reserves over a very long duration. Testing an increase and decrease (e.g., +/- 100 basis points) in the assumed investment return on the assets backing the reserves is critical, as well as its effect on the discount rate used to calculate the Net Present Value (NPV) of future profits.
*   **Expenses and Expense Inflation:** Test a percentage increase in initial acquisition expenses, renewal administration expenses, and the rate of future expense inflation. Given the long-term nature of the product, expense inflation can significantly erode distant future profits.
*   **Withdrawal (Lapse) Rates:** Test both an increase and a decrease in withdrawal rates. If the product is priced relying heavily on initial acquisition costs being recouped over time, early lapses will severely impact profit. Conversely, if surrender values are ungenerous, late lapses might be a source of profit, making low lapse rates a downside risk.
*   **Tax/Regulations:** Test plausible changes in corporate tax rates or regulatory capital demands. 
*   **Interactions:** Test combined scenarios where correlated assumptions perform badly simultaneously (e.g., an economic downturn causing low investment returns *and* high lapse rates simultaneously).

## Question 6

### Part (i) [2 marks]

The policyholder may consider the following primary actions:

*   **Surrender completely:** Cash in the policy now to receive the current surrender (bid) value of the units, permanently terminating the life cover and the investment.
*   **Make the policy Paid-Up:** Cease paying all future premiums but leave the accumulated unit fund invested for the remaining 5 years until the planned maturity, retaining a proportionate amount of life cover.
*   **Continue as planned:** Continue paying the regular premiums for the remaining 5 years until maturity.
*   **Fund Switching:** Alter the investment strategy of the existing unit fund balance, and/or the allocation of future premiums, into different unit-linked funds offered by the company (e.g., switching from higher-risk equities to lower-risk bonds or cash, or vice versa if they anticipate a market rebound).

### Part (ii) [8 marks]

The policyholder's decision will be influenced by a complex interplay of personal and financial factors:

*   **Immediate Financial Needs:** If the policyholder has encountered financial hardship or requires immediate liquidity (e.g., to pay off a high-interest debt), surrendering may be necessary regardless of market timing.
*   **Future Financial Goals:** If the intended purpose of the maturity payout in 5 years is still relevant (e.g., to pay off an interest-only mortgage), they may be strongly incentivized to continue premiums to try and reach their target.
*   **Market Outlook:** If they believe the recent market falls are temporary and a sharp recovery is imminent, they may maintain premiums and current fund choice (or switch to higher risk) to capitalize on lower unit prices. Conversely, if they fear a prolonged recession, they might switch to cash funds or surrender.
*   **Affordability of Premiums:** Changes in employment or personal circumstances might mean the regular premiums are no longer affordable, forcing a move to Paid-Up status or surrender.
*   **Need for Life Cover:** The policy likely includes an element of life cover. Replacing this cover externally for a 5-year term could be prohibitively expensive due to the policyholder now being 15 years older, or potentially being in poorer health. This encourages maintaining the policy or making it Paid-Up over surrendering.
*   **Product Charges and Penalties:** Reaching 15 years means early surrender penalties are less likely, but there may be fund-switching charges or a loss of terminal / loyalty bonuses that penalize early exit or paid-up status. Furthermore, fixed annual policy fees might erode a small paid-up fund significantly.
*   **Guarantees:** If the product features any minimum maturity guarantees (e.g., return of premiums or a minimum unit price at maturity), surrendering early completely forfeits these valuable guarantees.
*   **Tax Considerations:** Depending on the jurisdiction, a partial surrender, full surrender, or letting the policy reach maturity may have vastly different income or capital gains tax consequences for the individual.

## Question 7

### Part (i) [5 marks]

When receiving the data on policy and unit fund movements, the following checks should be performed:

*   **Reconciliation of Policy Numbers (Completeness):** The number of policies in force at the start of the period, plus new business additions, minus terminations (deaths, surrenders, lapses, maturities), must exactly equal the number of policies in force at the end of the period.
*   **Reconciliation of Unit Holdings:** The total number of units assigned across all individual policyholder records must exactly match the aggregate number of units held by the underlying unit fund.
*   **Cash Flow Consistency:** The premiums recorded as received and claims/surrenders recorded as paid out in the movement data must reconcile with the company's audited revenue accounts and the cash flows transferred into or out of the unit funds.
*   **Reasonableness and Edit Checks:** Verify that individual fields fall within logical bounds. For example, check that dates of birth are realistic, premium amounts correspond to policy types, sum assureds are positive, and withdrawal dates fall within the analysis period. Identify missing values or corrupt formatting.
*   **Unit Price Validation:** Check the chronological series of unit prices applied during the period for any sudden, inexplicable jumps or gaps that might indicate pricing errors or missing daily data.

### Part (ii) [6 marks]

To reduce the level of lapses on both existing and new unit-linked endowment policies, the company could take the following actions:

**For Existing Business:**
*   **Enhanced Communication:** Proactively contact policyholders, especially those whose funds have fallen in value, to explain market volatility, the long-term nature of endowment investing, and to manage expectations.
*   **Flexibility Options:** Offer struggling policyholders the option of temporary premium holidays or the ability to permanently reduce their premium payments rather than defaulting to full surrender.
*   **Offer Alternative Funds:** Contact policyholders who may have poorly suited risk profiles and highlight the availability of alternative, lower-volatility funds or specific lifestyle-switching strategies.
*   **Improve Servicing / Digital Access:** Upgrade the customer portal so policyholders can easily monitor their investments, understand their charges, and feel engaged with their financial planning.

**For New Business:**
*   **Review Target Market and Sales Process:** Ensure the product is only sold via channels that properly assess the customer's risk appetite and long-term financial stability. Re-train the sales force regarding suitability.
*   **Redesign Commission Structures:** Move away from high initial up-front commissions paid to intermediaries and instead introduce an 'as-earned' commission model or strict commission clawback provisions for early lapses. This misaligns the incentive to churn policies.
*   **Adjust Charge Design:** Introduce heavier early-surrender penalties (if permitted by regulations) or structure the management charges to be back-end loaded, heavily discouraging early lapse while rewarding persistency.
*   **Post-Sale Welcome Process:** Implement a robust post-sale engagement process to properly onboard the client, re-confirm the policy details, and establish a strong relationship before the first premium anniversary.

## Question 8

### Part (i) [4 marks]

The likelihood of a consumer purchasing a life insurance product can differ distinctively between channels:

*   **Trust and Independence:** Consumers utilizing independent insurance intermediaries may have a higher likelihood of purchasing because they perceive the advice as unbiased, impartial, and tailored specifically to their entire financial situation, instilling greater confidence in the recommendation.
*   **Proactive vs. Reactive:** Intermediaries often conduct comprehensive financial reviews for clients and identify protection gaps the client wasn't actively seeking to fill (e.g., IHT planning), actively creating a sale. A direct sales force, if relying heavily on cold-calling, will face much higher customer resistance and lower conversion rates.
*   **Target Market Motivation:** Customers who independently contact a direct sales force (e.g., resulting from a TV campaign) are usually already highly motivated to buy a product, leading to high immediate conversion. In contrast, a broker's client may just be 'shopping around' and ultimately take the business elsewhere.
*   **Simplicity vs Flexibility:** For simple, transactional purchases, a direct sales channel with a streamlined digital journey might have a superior conversion rate due to ease of use. However, for complex whole of life needs, the direct channel may fail to close the sale without the nuanced guidance an intermediary provides.

### Part (ii) [9 marks]

*   **Mortality Rates:** Business sold through intermediaries often exhibits *lighter* (better) mortality. This is because intermediaries tend to serve higher socio-economic demographics who generally have better health outcomes. Furthermore, an intermediary may 'pre-screen' clients, placing heavily impaired lives with specialist insurers rather than the company. Direct sales, especially if targeting a mass-market or relying on simplified underwriting without advice, typically face *heavier* mortality profiles and a higher degree of anti-selection.
*   **Expenses and Commission:** Intermediary business is characterized by high variable costs (significant up-front commission payments or heavily remunerated initial-year funding constraints) but has lower fixed corporate overheads. Direct sales forces carry massive fixed overheads (salaries, office space, compliance, extensive continuous training, management structure) but lower variable per-policy commission. The overall expense heavily depends on the economies of scale achieved by the direct sales force.
*   **Persistency Rates:** Persistency logic offers arguments in both directions. Intermediary business might suffer *worse* early persistency if brokers aggressively churn policies to secure new upfront commissions elsewhere. However, if the independent advice correctly matched the product to a long-term need, persistency might be very strong. Direct sales force persistency might be poor if aggressive, high-pressure targets led to inappropriate sales that customers quickly regret and cancel. Broadly, high-quality advised business typically yields better long-term persistency.

### Part (iii) [5 marks]

Beyond differences in expected mortality, expenses, and persistency, the premiums quoted might differ for the exact same applicant for several reasons:

*   **Different Profit Margin Targets:** The company may logically mandate completely different required profit margins or hurdle rates for business sourced directly versus intermediated business, reflecting different divisional strategies.
*   **Strategic Cross-Subsidization (Loss Leading):** The direct channel might offer a consciously underpriced "loss-leader" premium aimed at capturing market share and establishing a direct client relationship, with the intention of cross-selling highly profitable general insurance or investment products later.
*   **Market Competitiveness:** The intermediary market is highly transparent, with brokers actively comparing quoting portals. The company may have heavily sharpened its intermediary pricing specifically to win top-three spots on software comparison platforms. Direct pricing, unhindered by instant side-by-side comparison, may be deliberately higher.
*   **Underwriting Differences:** Even if it is the "same sum assured," the different channels might funnel the applicant into entirely different underwriting philosophies—the intermediary application might undergo rigorous full underwriting capturing mitigating factors, while the direct route might rely on rigid, automated, simplified criteria that loads the premium by default for minor disclosures.
*   **Differing Reinsurance Treaties:** The company may have separate treaties with different reinsurers for the distinct distribution channels, leading to different underlying net risk costs that are passed on to the applicant.
