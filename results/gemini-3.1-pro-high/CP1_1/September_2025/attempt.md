## Question 1

### Part (i) [3 marks]

To determine the proportion of the valuation surplus that could be invested in high-yield bonds, the insurance company should consider the following:

- **Risk appetite and tolerances:** Ensure the increased credit and default risk of high-yield bonds does not breach the company's stated risk policies.
- **Regulatory capital requirements:** Assess the impact on the regulatory balance sheet. High-yield bonds will have higher capital charges (e.g., higher spread risk) under risk-based capital regimes. The proportion should be sized so as not to reduce the coverage ratio below target working levels.
- **Asset-liability matching (ALM):** Evaluate the characteristics of the high-yield bonds (duration, currency, cashflow profile) relative to the liabilities. Surplus funds could theoretically be invested more freely than backing assets, but correlation with existing assets needs checking.
- **Solvency projections:** Run scenario testing and stress testing (e.g., widening of credit spreads or increased default rates) to observe how this reallocation affects solvency over the long term.
- **Liquidity needs:** Check if the surplus is likely to be needed to meet shorter-term cashflow requirements. High-yield bonds may be less liquid than safer government or corporate bonds.
- **Expertise and resource:** Confirm whether the internal investment team or external managers have the necessary skills and mandates to manage high-yield credit risk effectively.

### Part (ii) [3 marks]

Other possible uses for the valuation surplus include:

- **Distributing to shareholders:** Paying out the surplus as dividends to the company's owners.
- **Retaining as free capital:** Holding the surplus to provide a buffer against future adverse experience, increasing financial strength and resilience.
- **Funding new business:** Using the capital to support the writing of new business volumes, which will require initial capital strain.
- **Product development:** Reinvesting in the business via developing new products, improving IT systems, or expanding into new markets.
- **Acquisitions:** Using the funds to acquire other companies or blocks of business to achieve growth or diversify operations.
- **Improving policyholder benefits:** If the product line involves participating (with-profits) business, distributing the surplus to policyholders via higher bonuses.
- **Reducing prices/premiums:** Lowering premiums on new business to gain market share or improve competitiveness.

## Question 2

The consultancy may want to replace its current liability valuation model for the following reasons:

- **Obsolescence and limitations:** The current model may be built on outdated technology, be slow to run, or lack the capacity to handle increased data volumes and complex new product variants.
- **Regulatory changes:** New reporting or valuation standards (e.g., IFRS 17) may require fundamentally different calculation methods, cashflow projections, or granularities that the old model cannot easily accommodate.
- **Cost inefficiency:** Maintenance costs, licensing fees, or IT support for the old model may have become too high, making a new model more cost-effective.
- **Functionality and features:** New models might offer enhanced features like stochastic modelling capabilities, better data visualisation, cloud-based processing, modular architecture, and easier integration with upstream data systems or downstream reporting systems. 
- **Key-person dependency:** The old model might be an internally developed spreadsheet or legacy system understood by only a few individuals, posing an operational risk.
- **Control environment:** The new model may provide superior audit trails, access controls, and version control to satisfy internal risk and audit requirements.

### Part (ii)(a) [4 marks for (a) and (b)]

**Challenges the company may encounter in implementing the new model:**
- **Time constraints:** Achieving the 3-month target is a very aggressive timeline to onboard all clients, risking incomplete testing and migration errors.
- **Data compatibility:** Client data formats might not seamlessly transfer to the new model, requiring extensive data cleansing and transformation.
- **Reconciliation issues:** Valuation results from the new model will inevitably differ slightly from the old model, requiring time-consuming investigation to identify whether the differences are due to errors, different methodologies, or simply fewer approximations.
- **Staff training and expertise:** Consultants will need to learn how to use the new software while continuing to deliver business-as-usual work, leading to resource strain.
- **Configuration errors:** Setting up the specific products, assumptions, and validation rules for each individual client within a tight timeframe increases the risk of coding or parameterisation errors.
- **System integration:** Integrating the external model with the consultancy's internal IT network and databases may face unforeseen technical hurdles.

### Part (ii)(b)

**How the challenges may be overcome:**
- **Phased rollout / Prioritisation:** Relax the 3-month deadline or phase the rollout, starting with simpler clients or a pilot group before migrating more complex clients.
- **Parallel running:** Run both the old and new models concurrently for a short period to validate the output and investigate discrepancies systematically.
- **Dedicated project team:** Assign specific individuals exclusively to the implementation and data migration tasks, so that training and setup are not squeezed in alongside business-as-usual.
- **Vendor support:** Utilise support, training, and documentation provided by the external model provider to speed up learning and resolve technical issues quickly.
- **Automated testing and tools:** Develop automated scripts to aid the reconciliation process and data formatting to reduce manual errors and save time.
- **Strong governance:** Establish a clear project plan, regular steering group updates, and robust sign-off procedures to track progress and manage risks.

## Question 3

### Part (i) [4 marks]

Using debt to improve the free capital position for a mutual involves issuing subordinated debt:

- **What it is:** The mutual can issue subordinated debt (such as bonds or loan notes) to external investors.
- **Subordination:** To count towards regulatory capital, the debt must be strictly subordinated to the claims of policyholders. In the event of insolvency, debt holders are only paid after policyholder liabilities are fully met.
- **Accounting impact:** While it is a liability for accounting purposes, regulators allow certain forms of subordinated debt (often subject to limits and tiering rules) to be treated as capital due to its loss-absorbing nature.
- **Improving free capital:** By raising cash through debt and investing it in admissible assets, the total available assets increase. Because the regulatory liability associated with the debt is offset or treated as capital, the excess of available assets over regulatory liabilities (free capital) improves.
- **Gearing effect:** It provides a way for a mutual, which cannot issue equity shares, to raise external capital and support short-term capital needs without structurally altering the mutual ownership model. It does, however, introduce regular interest payment obligations.

### Part (ii) [3 marks]

Other possible management actions the mutual could take to improve its free capital position include:

- **De-risking the asset portfolio:** Selling higher-risk assets (like equities or low-grade corporate bonds) and buying lower-risk assets (like government bonds). This reduces the regulatory capital requirements, thereby increasing available free capital.
- **Reinsurance:** Entering into reinsurance arrangements (e.g., financial reinsurance or quota share) to transfer risk to a reinsurer, relieving capital strain and reducing required capital.
- **Reducing new business volumes:** Writing less new business or altering the business mix towards less capital-intensive products to dramatically reduce the initial capital strain.
- **Expense reduction:** Implementing cost-cutting programs across the business to improve operational efficiency and retain more surplus.
- **Altering bonuses/payouts:** For with-profits business, reducing future regular or terminal bonuses, provided this complies with the Principles and Practices of Financial Management (PPFM) and policyholders' reasonable expectations.
- **Changing valuation assumptions:** If there are margins for prudence, reducing these margins (subject to regulatory approval/guidance) reduces the value of liabilities and increases free capital.

## Question 5

### Part (i) [4 marks]

Possible reasons why the bank's profits may have fallen last year:

- **Higher default/impairment rates:** Individuals with lower-than-average incomes are more vulnerable to economic downturns, cost-of-living crises, or rising unemployment, leading to a spike in mortgage defaults and bad debt write-offs.
- **Interest rate environment:** If central bank base rates increased and the bank was locked into fixed-rate mortgages on the asset side while paying floating/higher rates on its funding liabilities, its net interest margin would have been squeezed.
- **Increased cost of funding:** The bank may have faced higher borrowing costs in the wholesale markets due to increased perceived risk of its sub-prime lending portfolio, or it had to offer higher deposit rates to attract retail funds.
- **Falling property prices:** If house prices fell, the value of the collateral backing the mortgages would decrease. This would mean higher losses given default (LGD) when properties were repossessed and sold.
- **Increased operational expenses:** The bank might have spent heavily on new IT systems, faced higher staff costs due to inflation, or incurred regulatory fines.
- **Lower new business volumes:** Economic uncertainty might have deterred individuals from taking out new mortgages, reducing origination fees and interest income growth.

### Part (ii) [2 marks]

Ratios the bank could use to explain the fall in profits:

- **Net Interest Margin (NIM):** (Interest Income - Interest Expense) / Average Earning Assets. Demonstrates whether the spread between lending rates and funding costs has compressed.
- **Non-Performing Loan (NPL) Ratio:** Total Non-Performing Mortgages / Total Mortgage Portfolio. Indicates the deterioration in credit quality and rising defaults among the lower-income borrowers.
- **Cost-to-Income Ratio:** Operating Expenses / Operating Income. Shows if operational inefficiency or rising costs contributed to the profit fall.
- **Return on Equity (ROE) / Return on Assets (ROA):** Measures overall profitability relative to capital or asset base.

### Part (iii) [3 marks]

Types of information to include in the annual report commentary:

- **Macroeconomic context:** Explanation of the broader economic environment (e.g., inflation rates, unemployment trends, central bank interest rate changes) and how these uniquely affected their low-income customer base.
- **Portfolio performance metrics:** Disclosing the increase in arrears, default rates, and the provisions taken for bad debts, linking this explicitly to the drop in profit.
- **Strategic responses and mitigations:** Outlining the management actions taken to address the situation, such as tightening lending criteria, restructuring loans for struggling customers, or launching cost-reduction initiatives.
- **Future outlook:** Providing forward-looking statements regarding expected future performance, potential ongoing risks, and the anticipated timeline for returning to historical profit levels.
- **Impact on stakeholders:** Commentary on how the fall in profit affects dividend payments to shareholders and capital adequacy metrics relative to regulatory requirements.

## Question 6

### Part (i) [3 marks]

Demographic assumptions for a defined benefit pension scheme funding valuation could include:

- **Mortality rates (pre-retirement and post-retirement):** The probability of members dying, including base tables and assumptions for future mortality improvements.
- **Withdrawal / Leaver rates:** The rates at which active members leave the scheme prior to retirement (e.g., resigning from the employer).
- **Early / Ill-health retirement rates:** The incidence of members taking their benefits early, either voluntarily or due to ill health.
- **Proportions married / Family statistics:** The proportion of members who have an eligible spouse or dependants at death, and the assumed age difference between the member and the spouse.
- **New entrant profiling:** Assumptions about the ages, salaries, and gender of future new entrants (if the scheme is open and an ongoing valuation method is used).

### Part (ii) [4 marks]

Reasons for using each assumption:

- **Pre-retirement mortality:** Required to value lump sum death benefits, spouse's pensions payable upon death in service, and the release of reserves if a member dies before drawing a pension.
- **Post-retirement mortality:** Crucial for determining how long a pension will be in payment, directly driving the present value of the core retirement liability. Future improvements are needed because life expectancy is generally rising.
- **Withdrawal rates:** Leaving service before retirement typically changes the benefit structure (e.g., breaking the final salary link, valuing deferred pensions rather than active final salary pensions), usually altering the cost to the scheme.
- **Proportions married & age differences:** Necessary to calculate the expected cost of contingent survivor pensions, as the benefit only pays out if a spouse exists, and the spouse's age dictates the payout duration.
- **Ill-health retirement rates:** Ill-health retirements are typically more expensive because the pension starts earlier with no (or lenient) reduction factors, and may include added years of service.

### Part (iii) [3 marks]

Comparing the level of detail for a small scheme vs a very large scheme:

- **Use of scheme-specific experience:** A very large scheme will have sufficient data volumes to conduct credible statistical investigations into its own past demographic experience. A small scheme will lack statistical credibility and must heavily rely on standard national or industry tables.
- **Granularity of assumptions:** A large scheme might subdivide assumptions by fine categories (e.g., different mortality rates by specific postcode, job grade, pension size, or blue-collar vs white-collar). A small scheme is more likely to use a single blended assumption or standard tables with simple rating adjustments across the whole membership.
- **Cost vs benefit:** The high cost of detailed demographic analysis (e.g., commissioning a bespoke mortality study) is justifiable for a large scheme due to the massive monetary impact of small assumption changes. For a small scheme, such analysis is disproportionately expensive, so a simpler, slightly more prudent approach is adopted.

## Question 7

### Part (i) [3 marks]

Why a financial institution may want to securitise assets:

- **Capital release / Regulatory capital:** By selling the assets (e.g., mortgages) to a Special Purpose Vehicle (SPV), the assets are removed from the institution's balance sheet, reducing its risk-weighted assets and freeing up regulatory capital to write new business.
- **Raising liquidity:** It converts illiquid long-term assets into immediate cash upfront from the investors who buy the securitised notes.
- **Risk transfer:** The institution transfers the credit risk, default risk, and potentially interest rate risk associated with the underlying assets to the end investors.
- **Alternative funding source:** It diversifies the institution's funding base, potentially providing cheaper funding than issuing unsecured corporate debt or relying purely on retail deposits.
- **Generating fee income:** The institution can continue to service the mortgages (collecting payments) for a fee, creating a steady income stream without holding the principal risk.

### Part (ii) [3 marks]

Stakeholders for the securitisation of a mortgage portfolio:

- **The Originator:** The financial institution that originally wrote the mortgages and wishes to securitise them.
- **The Investors:** The individuals, funds, or institutions who purchase the tranches of securities (e.g., senior, mezzanine, or equity tranches) issued by the SPV.
- **The Borrowers:** The homeowners who took out the mortgages. (Though their interaction is usually unchanged, their debts are the underlying asset).
- **The Special Purpose Vehicle (SPV):** The separate legal entity created specifically to purchase the mortgages and issue the securities.
- **Credit Rating Agencies:** Agencies that assess the credit quality and assign ratings to the different tranches of the issued securities to make them marketable.
- **Investment Banks / Arrangers:** The entities that structure the transaction, underwrite the issuance, and place the securities in the market.
- **The Servicer:** The entity (often the originator) responsible for collecting monthly mortgage payments and pursuing arrears.
- **Regulators:** Regulatory bodies overseeing the financial stability and capital adequacy of the originator and the fairness of the transaction.

### Part (iii) [3 marks]

Issues a financial organisation may face when marketing a securitisation:

- **Complexity of the structure:** Investors may struggle to understand the complex cashflow waterfalls, the various tranches of risk, and the underlying correlation of defaults, making it harder to sell.
- **Credit quality of underlying assets:** If the underlying mortgages are perceived as sub-prime or poor quality, it will be difficult to find buyers, especially for the junior/equity tranches, without offering very high yields.
- **Market conditions:** In periods of economic downturn, rising interest rates, or following a credit crunch, overall demand for securitised products can dry up entirely due to risk aversion.
- **Information asymmetry:** Investors must rely heavily on the data provided by the originator. Proving transparency and the reliability of the historical default data is critical to building trust.
- **Attaining target credit ratings:** Marketing senior tranches usually requires AAA ratings. If the rating agencies require excessive credit enhancement (e.g., high over-collateralisation or guarantees) to grant these ratings, the deal may become economically unviable.

## Question 8

### Part (i) [3 marks]

Benefits to a bank of having effective risk management processes:

- **Fewer operational and financial losses:** Identifying, mitigating, and controlling risks (credit, market, operational) prevents unexpected severe financial impacts and bad debt write-offs.
- **Optimised capital allocation:** A strong understanding of risk enables the bank to allocate its economic and regulatory capital efficiently, improving return on equity and avoiding holding excessive idle capital.
- **Regulatory compliance:** It ensures the bank meets the strict requirements of banking regulators regarding risk management, avoiding fines, sanctions, or increased regulatory capital add-ons.
- **Improved reputation and trust:** Sound risk management reassures depositors, investors, and rating agencies, enhancing the bank's reputation and lowering its cost of funding.
- **Better strategic decision-making:** Providing the board and management with accurate risk-reward profiles allows them to make informed choices on new product launches, business expansion, or pricing strategies.

### Part (ii) [3 marks]

Possible reasons for the regulator's request to review existing risk management processes:

- **Recent losses or near-misses:** The bank may have recently suffered an unexpected financial loss, trading scandal, or operational failure, highlighting weaknesses in its current controls.
- **Change in external environment:** Rapidly shifting economic conditions (e.g., soaring inflation, rising interest rates) may mean the existing risk models are no longer fit for purpose.
- **Rapid growth or new activities:** The bank may have expanded rapidly into new, complex product lines (e.g., derivatives, crypto-assets) or new geographical markets, outpacing its legacy risk management framework.
- **Systemic sector reviews:** The regulator might be conducting a market-wide thematic review of risk management standards across all banks and identified the bank as an outlier in its practices.
- **Poor regulatory reporting:** The bank may have submitted inaccurate, delayed, or incomplete risk data to the regulator, prompting concerns over its underlying data governance and risk reporting systems.

### Part (iii) [3 marks]

How individuals are part of the risk management process:

**(a) Junior analyst in trading department:** Operates at the "first line of defence". They must strictly adhere to trading limits, follow internal procedures, accurately capture trade data in systems, and proactively report any anomalies, limit breaches, or suspicious activities to their line manager.

**(b) Chief Risk Officer (CRO):** Leads the "second line of defence". They are responsible for designing the overarching enterprise risk management framework, setting risk appetites, independently monitoring the risks taken by the front office, reporting risk aggregated data to the Board, and challenging the business units on their risk-taking.

**(c) Institutional shareholder:** Exerts influence through corporate governance. They analyse the bank’s public risk disclosures, engage with the Board and management regarding the bank's risk culture, and vote on resolutions (e.g., executive remuneration packages) to ensure management’s incentives are aligned with long-term sound risk-taking, rather than short-term reckless behaviour.

### Part (iv) [2 marks]

Information included in the risk reporting section of a bank's published annual report:

- **Risk strategy and appetite:** The board's approach to taking and managing risk, and the defined limits for key risks (credit, market, operational, liquidity).
- **Principal risks and uncertainties:** A description of the major material risks currently facing the bank, including emerging risks (e.g., cyber threats, climate risk).
- **Risk mitigation and controls:** Qualitative descriptions of the governance structures, stress testing frameworks, and internal controls used to manage risks.
- **Quantitative risk metrics:** Data tables showing credit risk exposures (e.g., loan-to-value distributions, NPL ratios), Value-at-Risk (VaR) for market risk, and liquidity coverage ratios.

## Question 9

### Part (i) [6 marks]

How scheme managers could use the actuary’s analysis of mortality experience:

- **Assess financial impact:** Understand how the doubling of pre-retirement mortality translates into an actual monetary strain on the scheme's funding position (as lump sums are currently paid directly from the fund).
- **Identify trends vs volatility:** The managers must work with the actuary to distinguish whether this was a one-off statistical fluctuation (random volatility), a specific short-term event (e.g., a localized pandemic or accident affecting a specific workplace site), or a persistent long-term trend in the workforce's health.
- **Review risk concentrations:** Analyze if the deaths were concentrated in certain demographics, job roles, or age bands, to see if the scheme is holding excessive concentrated risk.
- **Revise valuation bases:** Determine if the demographic assumptions for pre-retirement mortality need to be updated for future funding intervals, which would increase the liabilities and require higher future contributions from the employer.
- **Inform risk mitigation strategies:** Provide the analytical foundation for deciding whether the risk is too large or volatile to be retained. This includes pricing out the cost of insuring the benefit or redesigning the benefit (e.g., reducing the multiple or switching to dependents' pensions instead of huge lump sums).
- **Cashflow planning:** Understanding the frequency and size of these lump sums allows managers to ensure adequate liquid assets are held in the scheme instead of illiquid growth assets, preventing forced sales in bad markets when a death occurs.

### Part (ii) [5 marks]

Main factors the managers should take into account in making the decision to insure the lump sum death benefits:

- **Size of the scheme and volatility:** If the scheme is small, random fluctuations in death rates (volatility risk) can be financially devastating. A single large death claim (e.g., a high earner) could cause a severe funding deficit. Insurance smooths this volatility.
- **Cost of insurance premiums:** The premium charged by the insurer will include the expected cost of the claims plus the insurer’s expenses, profit margin, and cost of capital. The managers must assess if this premium represents good value compared to self-insuring.
- **Employer covenant and risk appetite:** If the sponsoring employer is financially strong and willing to underwrite the volatility (paying extra contributions when deaths are high), insurance might not be necessary. If the covenant is weak, protecting the fund via insurance is crucial.
- **Cashflow and liquidity:** Insuring the benefit transfers the liquidity risk to the insurer. The scheme avoids having to liquidate assets suddenly to pay out a 4x salary lump sum.
- **Administration and underwriting:** The insurer may impose conditions such as "actively at work" requirements or require medical underwriting (free cover limits) for high earners. The managers must consider the administrative burden of supplying data to the insurer and ensuring no member is inadvertently left uninsured.
- **Credit risk of the insurer:** By insuring, the scheme swaps mortality risk for counterparty credit risk (the risk the insurer fails to pay the claim). The managers must select a highly reputable, well-capitalised insurer.

## Question 10

### Part (i) [6 marks]

Four insurance products providing similar benefits to social security pre-SPA:

- **Income Protection Insurance (IPI):** Provides a regular replacement income for individuals if they are unable to work due to long-term illness or injury. This replaces state disability or incapacity benefits.
- **Private Medical Insurance (PMI):** Covers the cost of private healthcare treatment, surgeries, and consultations. While a state might have a public health service, in countries where social security covers healthcare costs directly, PMI would replace this.
- **Unemployment Insurance / Payment Protection:** Pays a regular income or covers specific loan/mortgage repayments if the policyholder becomes involuntarily unemployed. This provides a safety net akin to state unemployment benefits.
- **Critical Illness Cover (CIC):** Pays a tax-free lump sum if the policyholder is diagnosed with a specific severe illness covered by the policy (e.g., cancer, heart attack). This provides an immediate cash injection to help with lifestyle adjustments or medical costs, supplementing the lack of state support.
- **Term Assurance (Life Insurance):** Pays a lump sum or income to dependents if the breadwinner dies before retirement age, fulfilling the role of state bereavement or widow's benefits.

*(Note: The question asked for four; five are listed. IPI, Unemployment, CIC, and Term Assurance are the most direct replacements for typical pre-SPA social security).*

### Part (ii) [4 marks]

Advantages to a government of introducing a compulsory requirement for individuals to purchase long-term disability insurance:

- **Fiscal relief:** It drastically reduces the government's direct social security expenditure on disability benefits, relieving pressure on the national budget and allowing funds to be deployed elsewhere or taxes to be lowered.
- **Ensures universal coverage:** By making it compulsory, the risk of anti-selection is eliminated. The insurance pool will naturally be broad and diversified across healthy and unhealthy lives, lowering the average premium cost compared to a voluntary market.
- **Prevents under-insurance:** Many individuals suffer from myopia or an "it won't happen to me" mentality. Compulsion ensures that vulnerable citizens do not fall into severe poverty if they develop a long-term illness.
- **Incentivises rehabilitation:** Private insurers often have strong financial incentives to invest in early medical intervention, rehabilitation programs, and workplace adaptations to get claimants back to work faster than state systems typically manage.
- **Stimulates the financial sector:** A large new compulsory market will create significant revenue and growth for private insurance companies, generating jobs and corporate tax revenues for the government.

## Question 11

### Part (i) [6 marks]

Sources of fraud risk for the specified products:

- **Income Protection Insurance:**
  - **Exaggeration of severity:** Claiming a minor injury prevents them from working entirely, or extending the claim period long after they have medically recovered.
  - **Non-disclosure at underwriting:** Falsely completing medical questionnaires, hiding pre-existing conditions or dangerous hobbies that would have resulted in higher premiums or declined cover.
  - **Fictitious income:** Overstating their prior occupational income to receive a larger replacement benefit than they are entitled to, potentially aiming for over-replacement to disincentivise returning to work.

- **Term Assurance:**
  - **Non-disclosure of critical medical history:** Failing to declare a terminal illness, smoking status, or severe family medical history at the application stage to secure cheap cover.
  - **Faked death:** A highly coordinated fraud where the policyholder fakes their own death (often overseas), using forged death certificates to allow beneficiaries to claim the lump sum.

- **Home Contents Insurance:**
  - **Staged losses/theft:** Purposefully staging a break-in or claiming items were stolen while away from home (e.g., claiming a lost expensive watch on holiday) when the item is safely hidden.
  - **Exaggerating claim values:** Appropriately claiming for a genuine flood or fire, but inflating the value of the destroyed items, or adding items to the claim that were never owned in the first place.

### Part (ii) [6 marks]

How the insurance company can protect itself against these fraud risks:

- **Income Protection Insurance:**
  - **Strict claims management:** Employing specialist claims assessors, requiring regular medical certificates, and requesting independent medical examinations (IMEs) from company-appointed doctors to verify the severity and duration of the illness.
  - **Financial checks:** Requesting official tax returns or employer payslips to accurately verify pre-disability income, ensuring the benefit amount does not exceed the maximum allowed replacement ratio.
  - **Rehabilitation services:** Offering (and requiring participation in) rehabilitation to test the claimant’s real capacity and willingness to return to work.

- **Term Assurance:**
  - **Robust underwriting:** Depending on the sum assured, requiring access to comprehensive GP medical records or conducting independent medical screenings (e.g., blood tests for cotinine to verify non-smoking status) before accepting the risk.
  - **Rigorous claims investigation:** For claims involving sudden unexplained deaths, especially soon after the policy starts or in regions with weak civil registration systems, deploying specialist investigative agencies to verify the authenticity of the death certificate and the events.

- **Home Contents Insurance:**
  - **Proof of ownership:** Requiring clear evidence of ownership for high-value items at the claims stage (or upfront at underwriting), such as original purchase receipts, valuations, photographs, or warranty documentation.
  - **Data analytics and sharing:** Using advanced analytics to spot suspicious claim patterns (e.g., frequent claims from the same address or linked individuals) and sharing data on known fraudsters through industry-wide databases (e.g., the Claims and Underwriting Exchange).
  - **Salvage and inspection:** Sending loss adjusters to inspect the damage physically to ensure the severity matches the claim, and taking control of the salvage to prevent it from being claimed multiple times.
