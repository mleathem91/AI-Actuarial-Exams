## Question 1

### Part (i) [3 marks]

The advantages and disadvantages to an insurance company of offering coverage on a ‘new for old’ basis for a household contents insurance policy include:

**Advantages:**
*   **Commercial attractiveness:** The policy is much more appealing to customers as it guarantees full replacement no matter the age of the item, increasing sales, market share, and retention.
*   **Ease of claims settlement:** It streamlines the claims process by removing the need for assessors to calculate depreciation and argue with policyholders over the current value of old items, leading to faster settlement and higher customer satisfaction.
*   **Higher premium income:** The insurer can charge a higher premium for the superior coverage provided, potentially leading to higher absolute profits.

**Disadvantages:**
*   **Increased claims severity:** Claim payments will be financially larger because replacing an item at modern retail prices costs significantly more than its depreciated second-hand value.
*   **Moral hazard and fraud:** It introduces a significant moral hazard. Policyholders might deliberately damage or 'lose' old, worn-out items (e.g., old laptops, faded carpets) specifically to receive brand-new replacements, leading to higher claims frequency.
*   **Inflation risk:** The insurer is directly exposed to inflation in consumer goods. If the cost of electronics or furniture spikes, claims costs will rise accordingly, necessitating careful pricing and reserving.

### Part (ii) [4 marks]

Longer-tailed liability insurance business typically results in a higher capital charge compared to short-tailed property insurance business for several reasons:

*   **Greater reserving uncertainty:** The long delay between the premium being earned, the claim being incurred, reported, and finally settled means there is a much greater level of uncertainty in the ultimate cost of claims over a multi-year horizon, fundamentally driving up reserve risk capital.
*   **Exposure to inflation:** Long-tail classes are exposed to compounding economic and superimposed inflation over many years. A spike in inflation will have a dramatic effect on the value of reserves held, requiring a significant capital buffer.
*   **Court and legislative changes:** Over the long tail, there is a substantial risk that societal attitudes towards compensation, court precedents (e.g., changes to discount rates for structured settlements), or legislative changes will retrospectively increase the cost of claims already incurred.
*   **Latent claims risk:** Liability business can be subject to latent claims (e.g., industrial diseases like mesothelioma) that only manifest decades after the policy exposure, representing a major risk that requires long-term capital backing.
*   **Asset-Liability Mismatch (ALM) risk:** The insurer must hold reserves backed by assets for a much longer period. This introduces significant market and credit risks on the asset portfolio, as well as the risk that the duration and yields of the assets fail to match the long-term liabilities.

## Question 2

### Part (i) [4 marks]

The possible risks associated with the rapid doubling of premiums in one calendar year include:

*   **Underwriting and Pricing Risk:** To achieve such rapid growth, the company may have significantly underpriced its products to win market share or relaxed its underwriting standards to accept poorer quality risks. This could lead to a severe deterioration in the loss ratio and underwriting losses.
*   **Reserving Risk:** The historical data of the company will no longer be representative of the new, much larger portfolio. Actuaries will face heightened uncertainty in setting appropriate reserves, increasing the risk of material under-reserving if the new business performs worse than expected.
*   **Operational Risk:** The massive increase in volume can overwhelm the company’s existing infrastructure. IT systems, customer service, underwriting, and claims handling departments may lack the capacity to cope, leading to administrative errors, poor customer service, backlogs, and reputational damage.
*   **Capital Risk:** An expanding business requires capital to support the increased premium and reserve risk profiles. If the growth is too fast, the company may strain its capital resources and breach regulatory solvency requirements unless new capital is rapidly injected.
*   **Reinsurance Risk:** Existing reinsurance treaties may not have sufficient capacity limits to absorb the doubled volume. Alternatively, proportional reinsurance costs might skyrocket, or accumulation risks might breach clash or catastrophe limits, leaving the company heavily exposed on a net basis.

### Part (ii) [3 marks]

The regulator's concern regarding reserve sufficiency may not be valid for the following reasons:

*   **Business mix changes towards short-tail lines:** The growth might be entirely driven by a very short-tailed class of business (e.g., single-trip travel insurance or short-term event cancellation). For such policies, the risk exposure may have already fully expired and all claims settled before the 31 December year-end, meaning no additional unearned premium or claims reserves are required.
*   **Timing of the new business written:** If the new business was heavily weighted towards the very beginning of the year, a vast majority of the premium would be earned, and many claims settled. Conversely, if older historical cohorts of business experienced faster-than-expected run-off (claims closing rapidly), the release of old reserves might offset the introduction of new reserves.
*   **Favourable claims experience:** The underlying claims frequency or severity might have been exceptionally benign during 2024. If the overall loss environment was much better than expected, the absolute amount of case reserves and IBNR needed per unit of exposure would be lower, offsetting the volume growth.
*   **Faster claims settlement speed:** The company might have implemented a highly efficient new claims settlement process during the year. Faster finalisation of claims means fewer outstanding case reserves and lower IBNR at year-end, pulling total reserves down despite top-line growth.

### Part (iii) [4 marks]

To investigate reserve sufficiency, the regulator could calculate and analyse the following ratios:

*   **Reserve-to-Premium Ratio (Outstanding Reserves / Earned Premium):** This is the primary indicator. Given a doubling of premium, a massive drop in this ratio would flag potential under-reserving, assuming the business mix is static.
*   **Loss Ratio (Incurred Claims / Earned Premium):** By comparing the current year's loss ratio with historical averages, an unusually low figure alongside rapid growth strongly suggests the company is underestimating its IBNR or case reserves to artificially show a profit.
*   **Run-off Result / Prior Year Development to Opening Reserves:** Analysing how reserves for prior accident years developed during 2024. If the company is showing significant adverse development (strengthening prior reserves), it historically under-reserved, which casts doubt on the current year's reserve adequacy.
*   **Paid-to-Incurred Ratio (Paid Claims / Incurred Claims):** A sudden, significant drop in this ratio could indicate that while claims are being incurred (and case reserved), payments are slow, or IBNR is being aggressively increased. Alternatively, an unusually high ratio might indicate inadequate IBNR being held above the paid amounts.
*   **IBNR-to-Total-Reserves Ratio:** Helps determine if the nature of the reserves has changed, or if the company is neglecting IBNR provisions for the massive influx of new business.

## Question 3

### Part (i) [2 marks]

Four natural catastrophe events that could impact a commercial property insurer include:
1.  Tropical cyclones (Hurricanes / Typhoons)
2.  Earthquakes
3.  Floods (Inland riverine or coastal storm surge)
4.  Wildfires

### Part (ii) [3 marks]

To reduce its exposure to natural catastrophe events impacting single large risks, Company A could take the following actions:

*   **Underwriting restrictions and risk selection:** Mandate strict underwriting guidelines that decline risks situated in known high-hazard zones (e.g., properties on fault lines, coastal hurricane zones, or flood plains), or place strict aggregation limits in specific postcodes.
*   **Modify coverage terms:** Introduce location-specific high deductibles for catastrophe perils, or implement sub-limits that cap the maximum payout specifically for damage caused by earthquakes, windstorms, or floods on these large risks.
*   **Purchase Facultative Reinsurance:** Transfer the peak catastrophe risk of specific, highly exposed single commercial properties directly to reinsurers on a case-by-case (facultative) basis.
*   **Require robust risk engineering and mitigation:** Mandate that policyholders implement physical risk improvements (e.g., flood defences, earthquake retrofitting, automatic shut-off valves) as a strict condition before coverage is bound.

### Part (iii) [7 marks]

**Assumption made:** The losses calculated below are from a single, defined natural catastrophe event that triggers both the risk and catastrophe excess of loss arrangements, and sufficient reinstatements and aggregate limits exist on both contracts to pay the claims in full. It is also assumed that 'Accumulation of losses from other risks' consists of many individual losses that each fall entirely below the $10m attachment point of the Risk XL. 

**(a) Risk Excess of Loss Recovery ($20m xs $10m per risk):**
The Risk XL pays the portion of each individual loss that exceeds $10m, up to a maximum payout of $20m per risk.

*   **Risk A Loss = $20,000,000:**
    Recovery = Min(Loss - Attachment, Limit) = Min(20m - 10m, 20m) = **$10,000,000**
*   **Risk B Loss = $50,000,000:**
    Recovery = Min(Loss - Attachment, Limit) = Min(50m - 10m, 20m) = **$20,000,000**
*   **Risk C Loss = $7,500,000:**
    Recovery = $0 (does not breach the $10m attachment point).
*   **Accumulation of other losses:** $0 recovery as no single loss breaches the attachment point.

**Total Risk XL Recovery** = $10,000,000 + $20,000,000 + $0 = **$30,000,000**

**(b) Catastrophe Excess of Loss Recovery ($100m xs $200m per event):**
The Cat XL responds to the aggregate event loss, *net* of the Risk XL recoveries.

*   **Total Gross Event Loss** = Risk A + Risk B + Risk C + Accumulation
    Gross Loss = $20m + $50m + $7.5m + $240m = **$317,500,000**
    
*   **Net Event Loss (after Risk XL)** = Gross Loss - Total Risk XL Recovery
    Net Loss = $317,500,000 - $30,000,000 = **$287,500,000**
    
*   **Catastrophe XL Recovery** = Min(Net Loss - Attachment, Limit)
    Cat XL Recovery = Min(287.5m - 200m, 100m) = Min(87.5m, 100m) = **$87,500,000**

### Part (iv) [3 marks]

The removal of natural catastrophe coverage from the risk excess of loss reinsurance arrangement will impact the capital model in the following ways:

*   **Catastrophe Risk:** Capital requirements for natural catastrophe risk will increase materially. Without the risk XL capping individual large property losses, the net losses feeding into the catastrophe XL calculation will be higher. The $200m attachment point of the Cat XL will be breached more frequently, and gross losses could more easily exceed the $300m exhaustion point, increasing net tail risk.
*   **Premium / Underwriting Risk:** The volatility of the net underwriting result for the property class will increase significantly. If a severe natural catastrophe occurs, Company A will now bear the entirety of massive single-risk losses (like Risk B's $50m loss) on a net basis up until the *aggregate* event loss reaches the Cat XL attachment point.
*   **Liquidity Risk:** Since Company A has to pay the gross individual property claims out of its own funds before accumulating enough to recover from the Cat XL, modelled short-term liquidity risk in stress scenarios will increase.
*   **Credit risk:** Will marginally decrease for the risk XL counterparty, as they no longer face cat-driven claims, but will increase for the cat XL counterparty, as more losses flow into that treaty.

## Question 4

### Part (i) [3 marks]

When structuring a cyber catastrophe bond, Company E must consider the following factors:

*   **Trigger mechanism:** E must decide between an indemnity trigger (based on E's actual incurred losses, minimising basis risk but taking longer to settle), a modelled loss trigger, or an industry loss index trigger (faster settlement but introduces basis risk where E suffers a loss but the bond doesn't trigger).
*   **Definition of covered perils and events:** The bond must clearly define what constitutes a "cyber catastrophe" (e.g., widespread ransomware attack, major cloud infrastructure outage, systemic zero-day exploit) to ensure it aligns identically with E's portfolio exposures. 
*   **Risk profile and pricing:** Determining the attachment probability (how often investors are expected to face a loss) and expected loss, which will directly dictate the coupon rate (interest) required to attract capital market investors.
*   **Duration:** While 3 years is standard, E must consider if locking into a multi-year wordings matches the extreme dynamism of the evolving cyber threat landscape.

### Part (ii) [6 marks]

**Option A: Issue a 3-year cyber catastrophe bond**

**Advantages:**
*   **Multi-year price and capacity certainty:** Locks in protection levels and costs for 3 years, insulating Company E from price spikes or capacity crunches in the highly volatile conventional cyber reinsurance market.
*   **Elimination of credit risk:** Catastrophe bonds are fully collateralised upfront by highly-rated, safe investments held in a Special Purpose Vehicle (SPV). If a catastrophe occurs, the funds are immediately available, removing counterparty default risk.
*   **Diversification of capital:** Accesses broad capital markets (pension funds, specialised ILS funds) rather than relying exclusively on the finite capacity of traditional reinsurers.

**Disadvantages:**
*   **High frictional costs:** Structuring a catastrophe bond involves immense legal, modelling, investment banking, and SPV setup fees. For a "newly formed" company, these fixed costs may be prohibitively expensive relative to the size of cover.
*   **Inflexibility:** Cyber risk evolves incredibly fast. A 3-year static contract is highly inflexible; it may not cover new types of cyber threats that emerge in year 2 or 3, leaving E exposed.
*   **Basis risk:** If structured with a non-indemnity trigger to appeal to investors, E runs the severe risk of suffering massive cyber losses that fail to activate the bond payout.

**Option B: Purchase new annual cyber catastrophe non-proportional treaty reinsurance**

**Advantages:**
*   **Flexibility:** As a newly formed company with an evolving portfolio, an annual treaty allows E to continually renegotiate limits, attachment points, and specific exclusions each year to perfectly match its growing risk profile and the latest cyber threats.
*   **Customisable indemnity cover:** Traditional treaties are almost always indemnity-based, perfectly tracking Company E's actual claims and virtually eliminating basis risk.
*   **Lower execution cost and speed:** Much cheaper and faster to arrange annually via a broker than structuring a capital markets SPV.

**Disadvantages:**
*   **Reinsurance cycle risk:** Company E is highly exposed to the annual renewal cycle. If a massive global cyber event occurs, reinsurers might drastically raise prices next year or withdraw capacity from the market entirely, leaving E unprotected.
*   **Credit risk:** Following a systemic global cyber catastrophe, traditional reinsurers will face immense correlated losses, introducing the risk that E's reinsurers may downgrade or default and fail to pay claims.

## Question 5

### Part (i) [4 marks]

**Advantages of Lump Sum Settlements:**
*   **Finality of risk:** Once paid, Company R extinguishes all future liabilities for that claim, eliminating ongoing exposure to extreme longevity risk (claimant living longer than expected) and specific care cost inflation.
*   **Capital and Reserve Release:** Settling the claim allows for the immediate release of the substantial reserves and associated capital buffer held against the long-term uncertainty, improving the balance sheet and return on capital.
*   **Operational Simplicity:** Removes the administrative burden and ongoing cost of monitoring claimant survival, managing investments, and making regular payments over decades.

**Disadvantages of Lump Sum Settlements:**
*   **Risk of Overcompensation / High upfront cost:** Courts calculate lump sums using discount rates (like the Ogden tables in the UK) that are often extremely conservative (low or negative real yields). This forces the insurer to pay a massive upfront sum that heavily penalises them, often more than the true economic cost.
*   **Mismatch of Claimant Need:** A lump sum places the investment and inflation risk onto the severely injured claimant, which may not guarantee their long-term care needs are met if they mismanage the funds.

**Advantages of Regular Intervals (Periodic Payment Orders - PPOs):**
*   **Accurate compensation cost:** The insurer only pays for exactly as long as the claimant is alive. There is no risk of a massive overpayment windfall if the claimant's life expectancy was overestimated and they die prematurely.

**Disadvantages of Regular Intervals:**
*   **Severe ALM and Inflation Risk:** Company R retains extreme, open-ended exposure to superimposed inflation (e.g., carer wage inflation) and longevity risk. It is nearly impossible to find financial assets in the market that perfectly match and hedge these specific, long-duration, highly-inflation-linked liability cash flows.
*   **Capital intensity:** Requires massive amounts of capital to be held for decades to support the unhedged longevity and inflation tail risks.

### Part (ii) [4 marks]

Superimposed inflation refers to claims cost inflation that exceeds the underlying base economic inflation rate (like CPI or RPI). For an Employers' Liability (EL) portfolio, the likely components include:

*   **Medical and Technological Advancements:** Unprecedented improvements in medical technology, prosthetics, and rehabilitative care continually emerge. These treatments significantly extend life expectancies for severely injured workers and cost vastly more than older treatments, driving up periodic care costs faster than general inflation.
*   **Carer Wage Inflation:** The costs of full-time nursing and carer support tend to rise faster than general retail price inflation, driven by skilled labour shortages, minimum wage legislation, and changing care industry standards.
*   **Judicial and Legal Trends (Court Awards):** Courts may steadily increase the generosity of awards for non-pecuniary damages (pain and suffering), alter discount rates to favour claimants (e.g., Ogden rate changes), or expand the scope of what constitutes an employer's duty of care, systematically driving up claim severities.
*   **Compensation Culture and 'Claims Farming':** A societal shift making individuals more disposed to sue their employers, often facilitated by 'no-win, no-fee' legal frameworks. This leads to higher plaintiff legal costs being added to settlements, and a higher frequency of smaller claims that might previously not have been pursued.

### Part (iii) [4 marks]

Allowing for explicit inflation in IBNR estimation (projecting claims in 'real' terms and then applying a specific inflation curve) instead of implicit (assuming past inflation embedded in the triangles will perfectly repeat):

**Advantages:**
*   **Greater Accuracy in Volatile Environments:** Crucial during a "recent period of high inflation". Explicit modelling allows the actuary to utilize the most up-to-date, forward-looking economic forecasts and yield curves, rather than relying on historical run-off data that contains an outdated, structurally lower inflation environment.
*   **Scenario Testing and Sensitivities:** It allows management and the regulator to easily stress-test the reserves under various precise economic scenarios (e.g., "what if care inflation spikes by 3%?"), which is difficult when inflation is tangled inside the development factors.
*   **Tailored Assumptions:** Actuaries can apply different inflation rates to different components of the EL claim (e.g., assigning a high carer wage index to the care component, and CPI to the loss of earnings component) for accuracy.

**Disadvantages:**
*   **Modelling Complexity and Cost:** Separating historical data into base layers, stripping out past inflation to find 'real' development factors, and managing complex projection engines greatly increases the time, cost, and potential for coding errors in the reserving process.
*   **Subjectivity and Model Error:** Projecting long-term explicit economic variables and future superimposed inflation is highly uncertain and subjective. Incorrectly setting these long-term assumptions can lead to massive misstatements of IBNR. The complexity makes peer review harder.
*   **Granular Data Requirements:** Explicit modelling often requires highly granular claims data to adequately separate past claims payments by their constituent parts (medical, legal, economic) and properly deflate them.

### Part (iv) [6 marks]

To effectively model the impact of inflation on various risks in Company R’s capital model:

*   **Implement an Economic Scenario Generator (ESG):** The core of the capital model must use an ESG to simulate thousands of stochastic, correlated paths for key macroeconomic variables, specifically base economic inflation (CPI), wage inflation, and interest rates.
*   **Reserve Risk Modelling:** The projected run-off of the EL claims reserves should be dynamically linked to the ESG. Each simulation should project the 'real' cash flows and then inflate them using the specific stochastic inflation path for that simulation, alongside a stochastic distribution for superimposed inflation. This quantifies the volatility of reserves driven directly by inflation shocks.
*   **Premium Risk Modelling:** Similarly, the simulation of future claim severities arising from unexpired or planned written exposures must be inflated using the simulated inflation paths, capturing the risk that the priced premiums are insufficient due to an inflation spike.
*   **Asset-Liability Matching (ALM) Risk:** The ESG must project asset returns (bond yields, equities) consistently with the inflation paths. The model calculates the value of assets and the inflated EL liabilities simultaneously in each simulation. This captures 'stagflation' risk, where high inflation drives up care costs while simultaneously causing fixed-income asset values to plummet due to rising interest rates, exposing massive capital deficits.
*   **Specific PPO Modelling:** For regular interval payments, the capital model requires a specific module that simulates claimant mortality (longevity risk) correlated with the highest care-wage inflation indices over extreme durations (30-50 years).

## Question 6

### Part (i) [3 marks]

Calculating IBNR as a fixed percentage of case reserves is a highly simplistic approach.

**Advantages:**
*   **Ease of calculation:** Extremely simple, fast, and cheap to implement. It requires no complex data extraction, statistical software, or specialised actuarial expertise.
*   **Understandability:** Highly transparent and easy for non-actuarial management and claims handlers to understand and communicate.

**Disadvantages:**
*   **Highly inaccurate and unresponsive:** It completely fails to account for changes in the speed of claims reporting or settlement. If claims handlers speed up case reserving, total reserves artificially balloon; if they slow down, IBNR is dangerously underestimated.
*   **Ignores exposure changes:** It cannot adjust for the steep increase in written premiums. Before claims emerge to form case reserves, the massive new exposure has no IBNR held against it, drastically understating the true liability.
*   **Ignores claims inflation:** Fails to capture changes in claims severity trends or fundamental shifts in the business mix.

### Part (ii) [3 marks]

The regulator likely made these proposals (establishing an independent actuarial function and mandating external peer review) for the following reasons:

*   **Eliminate Conflicts of Interest:** Having actuarial, finance, and claims acting as a single department creates massive governance failings. Claims staff might suppress case reserves to hide operational issues, while finance might suppress IBNR to boost short-term profits. An independent actuarial function ensures objective reserving.
*   **Address Severe Technical Deficiencies:** The steep premium growth combined with an alarmingly simplistic reserving methodology (fixed percentage) strongly suggests the companies lack the technical competence to safely manage their expanding risk profiles. A dedicated actuarial function forces the adoption of appropriate methodologies (like Chain Ladder).
*   **Protect Policyholders from Systemic Weakness:** Since there are only two insurers in Country G, the failure of either due to rapid, blindly-reserved growth poses a systemic risk to the country's economy. The regulator mandating external peer review acts as an emergency safeguard and expert audit to ensure the reserves are robust before a solvency crisis hits.

### Part (iii) [2 marks]

Implementing these proposals may not be practical because:

*   **Disproportionate Cost:** The companies are "small". The fixed cost of breaking up departments, hiring dedicated internal Chief Actuaries, and continually paying expensive external consultants for peer review may destroy their profitability and viability.
*   **Actuarial Resource Scarcity:** In a small market like Country G, there may simply be an absolute lack of qualified local actuaries available to staff two distinct new departments and provide a completely independent third party for peer review.

### Part (iv) [4 marks]

To perform a robust peer review of the end-to-end reserving process, the external actuary will request:

*   **Detailed Claims Data Triangles:** Incremental paid claims, incurred claims, and claim counts, triangulated by accident year and development year. *Reason:* To build independent projections using standard actuarial methodologies (Chain Ladder, Bornhuetter-Ferguson) to compare against the company's fixed percentage results.
*   **Exposure Data:** Historical earned and written premiums by accident year. *Reason:* Essential for methods like Bornhuetter-Ferguson, and critical for understanding how the "steep increase" in premium translates to actual exposure at risk.
*   **Underwriting and Pricing Information:** Details on why premiums grew so steeply—was it rate increases, new products, or loosened underwriting standards? *Reason:* To appropriately select initial expected loss ratios and understand underlying profitability trends.
*   **Claims Handling Philosophy and Manuals:** Information on any changes in how quickly claims are reported, recorded, and settled. *Reason:* Changes in claims operations distort data triangles, and the external actuary needs this context to select appropriate development factors.

## Question 7

### Part (i) [2 marks]

The difference between a Lloyd’s Syndicate and a Managing Agent is:

*   **Lloyd's Syndicate:** This is the pool or group of capital providers (members/Names) who group together to underwrite insurance risk in the Lloyd's market. It takes on the actual insurance risk and liability but is not a legal entity itself and has no employees.
*   **Managing Agent:** This is an incorporated company established specifically to manage the affairs of one or more syndicates. It is the legal entity that employs the underwriters, actuaries, and claims staff who conduct the daily business and bind risks on behalf of the syndicate's capital.

### Part (ii) [4 marks]

**(a) Advantages and disadvantages to Company B (Managing Agent):**
*   **Advantages:** Provides exceptionally efficient, low-cost access to a high volume of specialised engineering construction projects. Company B leverages broker C's established client relationships and niche distribution network without needing an extensive internal sales team.
*   **Disadvantages:** B delegates binding authority to an external party. If C is overly motivated by volume commissions rather than underwriting discipline, they may bind poor-quality risks, write inadequate premiums, or breach aggregation limits, leading to severe underwriting losses for B.

**(b) Advantages and disadvantages to Company C (Broker):**
*   **Advantages:** Allows C to provide immediate, excellent service to its clients by quoting and binding cover on the spot without referring to underwriters. It also generates significantly higher revenue through binding authority commissions and potential profit-sharing agreements.
*   **Disadvantages:** C takes on significant administrative and compliance burdens, acting effectively as an underwriter. If the portfolio performs terribly, B may abruptly withdraw the binding authority, severely damaging C's reputation and leaving them unable to place their clients' risks.

### Part (iii) [3 marks]

Company C might independently estimate IBNR separately from Company B to:

*   **Verify Profit Commissions:** Binding authorities often include a profit-sharing mechanism where C receives extra commission if the portfolio is profitable. Profitability depends heavily on IBNR. C needs its own estimate to ensure B is not setting overly conservative, artificially high IBNR reserves to eliminate C's profit commission.
*   **Strategic Underwriting Management:** Although a broker, C is acting as the *de facto* underwriter. Accurately knowing the required IBNR helps C assess the true long-term profitability of the risks they are writing, allowing them to adjust pricing or underwriting guidelines proactively.
*   **Protect Capacity:** If C relies on this facility, they need to know its true performance. If their independent IBNR shows the book is running poorly before B realises, C can take corrective action or begin securing alternative capacity in the market before B cancels the arrangement out of frustration.

## Question 8

### Part (i) [3 marks]

Key factors affecting claims inflation for a household insurance policy include:
1.  Increases in the cost of raw building materials (e.g., timber, bricks, slates, copper).
2.  Labour cost inflation for skilled tradespeople (e.g., builders, plumbers, electricians).
3.  Surges in demand (and thus prices) for labour and materials following a localised natural disaster (e.g., flood or severe freeze).

### Part (ii) [3 marks]

Possible perils covered by a pet insurance policy include:
1.  Veterinary fees for the treatment of illness or injury.
2.  Third-party liability (covering legal costs and damages if the pet, notably a dog, injures someone or damages property).
3.  Death of the dog/pet due to accident or illness, or costs associated with loss/theft (e.g., reward money, purchase price reimbursement).

### Part (iii) [6 marks]

The immediate ban on a dog breed constituting 40% of premium income will drastically affect Company P's financial statements:

**Income Statement / P&L:**
*   **Written Premium:** Will experience an immediate, catastrophic drop of roughly 40% as no new policies can be sold for this breed and renewals are legally prohibited.
*   **Earned Premium:** Will steadily decline over the next 12 months as the remaining exposure on active policies runs-off to expiry.
*   **Incurred Claims:** While claims frequency will eventually fall, there may be a massive, short-term frequency and severity spike. If policies cover euthanasia, or if panicked owners abandon dogs leading to third-party liability incidents, the losses will hit the P&L heavily before the risk expires.
*   **Expenses & Profitability:** The sudden loss of 40% of top-line revenue will destroy the expense ratio. Fixed operational costs (staff, IT systems, offices) will remain constant, but the premium denominator will shrink, turning the portfolio heavily loss-making and causing severe damage to overall profitability.

**Balance Sheet:**
*   **Unearned Premium Reserve (UPR):** Will run down much faster than usual. If the government mandates immediate cancellation of all active policies for the banned breed, UPR will instantly drop to zero for that cohort, matched by a massive outward cash flow to refund policyholders their unexpired premiums.
*   **Claims Provisions (Outstanding and IBNR):** Current reserves will be maintained but will run-off heavily over the next 1-3 years. Due to the ban, no new claims will be generated for this class after the policies expire, meaning the absolute size of the reserve balance will shrink permanently.

### Part (iv) [4 marks]

The ban will impact Company P's capital requirements in the following ways:

*   **Premium Risk Capital Requirement:** Will decrease substantially. Given 40% of the volume has been eliminated, the absolute measure of exposure (future written and unearned premium) used in standard formula capital models will drop, lowering the capital required against pricing inadequacy or catastrophic events on future business.
*   **Reserve Risk Capital Requirement:** Will decrease gradually in the medium term as the existing liabilities for the banned breed are settled and run off, reducing the volume of reserves subject to volatility and development risk.
*   **Expense Risk Capital:** Will likely increase. The extreme drop in premium volume leaves the company with stranded fixed costs. Capital models will penalise the company for the high risk of expense overruns relative to its new, smaller premium base.
*   **Operational Risk Capital:** The immediate term will see a spike in operational risk (mass cancellations, refund processing, potential legal disputes with policyholders, regulatory scrutiny). Overall, however, the massive reduction in underwriting volume will likely lead to an overall reduction in the absolute total capital required.

### Part (v) [3 marks]

The claims reserving process for travel insurance differs from household and pet insurance in several key ways:

*   **Extreme Short Tail:** Travel insurance claims (medical emergencies abroad, flight cancellations) are reported and settled incredibly quickly, almost concurrently with the short exposure period. Consequently, the IBNR reserve will represent a tiny fraction of the total reserves, heavily weighting the methodology towards simplistic expected loss ratio methods for the unexpired portion rather than complex Chain Ladder development.
*   **Different Exposure Measures:** Unlike household or pet insurance which uses annual premium, travel insurance exposure is highly volatile and seasonal (summer holidays, ski seasons). Reserving actuaries must use more granular exposure measures—such as number of trips, passenger days, or destination risk factors—to accurately establish unearned premium and expected claims.
*   **Currency and Catastrophe Risk:** Travel medical claims are incurred locally worldwide, meaning reserves must account for extreme foreign exchange volatility. Furthermore, travel is exposed to unique accumulation flash-events (e.g., volcanic ash clouds, global pandemics, airline collapses) requiring rapid, manual reserving adjustments that are entirely different from household windstorm cats.
