## Question 1

### Part (i) [4 marks]

When reviewing the investment strategy of a defined benefit pension scheme, the following key considerations relating to the nature of the scheme should be taken into account:
* **Maturity of the scheme:** The proportion of pensioner liabilities relative to active/deferred liabilities. A very mature scheme will need a higher proportion of liquid, income-generating assets (such as bonds) to pay the imminent benefits.
* **Duration of liabilities:** Assessing how far into the future benefit payments fall due to ensure asset cash flows are appropriately matched against inflation and interest rate movements.
* **Funding level:** A poorly funded scheme might need to take more investment risk to generate higher returns (subject to the employer's covenant), whereas a well-funded scheme might de-risk to protect its position.
* **Nature of the liabilities (Benefit structure):** Consideration of whether benefits are linked to inflation (e.g., LPI pension increases) or fixed, which will dictate whether real/index-linked or nominal assets are more appropriate.
* **Status of the scheme:** Whether the scheme is open to new entrants, closed to new entrants but open to future accrual, or completely closed. Closed schemes have a finite life and shortening duration.
* **Size of the scheme:** Larger schemes may have access to a wider range of asset classes (e.g., direct property, illiquid alternatives) due to economies of scale and lower proportional governance costs.
* **Liquidity requirements:** The expected profile of benefit outgo, possible bulk transfers, or member options (such as cash commutation) requiring liquid assets in the short term.

### Part (ii) [4 marks]

The key characteristics of bonds are:
* **Fixed or predictable income stream:** Bonds typically pay a regular, defined coupon (interest payment) at specified intervals (e.g., semi-annually or annually).
* **Return of principal:** A defined capital sum (the principal/nominal value) is returned to the investor at a specified maturity date.
* **Security/Ranking:** In the event of the issuer's insolvency, bondholders rank above equity holders but may be structured with different levels of seniority (e.g., senior vs. subordinated debt). Some bonds are secured against specific assets.
* **Inflation linkage:** Bonds can be nominal (fixed coupon and principal) or index-linked (coupon and principal tied to an inflation index, such as RPI or CPI), providing protection against inflation.
* **Marketability/Liquidity:** Government bonds are usually highly marketable and liquid, whereas some corporate bonds can be less liquid.
* **Credit risk:** The risk that the issuer defaults on coupon or principal payments. Government bonds (typically of developed nations) have low credit risk, while corporate bonds carry higher credit risk, reflected in a yield premium.

### Part (iii) [4 marks]

**(a) Objectives of a LDI strategy:**
* The primary objective of LDI (Liability Driven Investment) is to reduce the volatility of the scheme's funding level by matching the sensitivity of the scheme's assets to the sensitivity of its liabilities in respect of changes in key economic variables.
* Specifically, it aims to hedge the scheme's exposure to interest rate risk and inflation risk.
* It aims to provide the required liability hedging efficiently, minimizing capital tie-up so that other capital can continue to appropriately invest in growth assets to generate return-seeking outperformance.

**(b) How assets may be chosen to meet these objectives:**
* **Bonds:** Holding a physical portfolio of government and high-quality corporate bonds (both nominal and index-linked) whose duration and cash flows intuitively match the expected benefit outgo.
* **Derivatives:** Utilizing derivative instruments such as interest rate swaps (to hedge interest rate risk) and inflation swaps (to hedge inflation risk). This allows for a precise hedge profile with a much smaller upfront capital and margin outlay.
* **Leverage:** Using repurchase agreements (repos) on physical bonds to borrow cash and reinvest, thus increasing the hedging ratio or freeing up funds for growth assets.

## Question 2 [9 marks]

There are several reasons why a 21-year-old recent graduate may choose to opt out of their employer's defined contribution pension scheme:

1. **Affordability and immediate cash constraints:** A recent graduate may have a low starting salary and high immediate living expenses, such as rent, moving costs, or a deposit for a house. They might prioritize having higher take-home pay over saving for a retirement that is over 40 years away.
2. **Paying off student debt:** The individual is likely to have significant student loans, overdrafts, or other short-term debts. They may prefer to use the extra income to clear expensive debts rather than locking it away in an inaccessible pension.
3. **Lack of understanding or engagement:** Pensions rely on compound interest and delayed gratification, which are complex concepts. Young individuals may not understand the benefits of the scheme, particularly missing out on the "free money" of the employer matching contribution, leading them to opt out through apathy or ignorance.
4. **Preference for short-to-medium term savings:** The individual may want to save for more immediate life events, such as a wedding, buying a car, or funding travel. Pension funds are locked away strictly until minimum pension age, so they may prefer using an accessible vehicle like an ISA.
5. **Alternative investment preferences:** They might believe they can achieve better investment outcomes using alternative investment vehicles (e.g., saving in a lifetime ISA which also receives a government bonus and can be used for a first home deposit, or direct equity investing).
6. **Time horizon to retirement:** At 21, retirement seems extremely distant. The psychological discount rate applied to future benefits is very high, meaning they place a very low perceived value on the deferred benefits compared to immediate consumption.
7. **Expectation of future career progression:** They might expect their salary to increase significantly over the course of their career, assuming they can easily make up for lost pension contributions later in life when they are more financially comfortable.
8. **Distrust of the financial system or government:** They may have concerns about the security of the pension system, stock market crashes, or frequent government changes to pension legislation (such as increasing the minimum access age or altering tax reliefs in the future) and prefer not to be bound by those rules.

## Question 3

### Part (i) [4 marks]

The role of employers in the provision of defined benefit (DB) pension schemes typically includes:
* **Sponsorship and establishment:** Initiating the scheme, determining the initial benefit structure, and establishing the legal framework (e.g., signing the trust deed and rules).
* **Funding the benefits:** Taking on the ultimate balance of cost risk. The employer pays regular contributions to fund future accrual and pays deficit-reduction contributions to clear any shortfalls that arise due to adverse experience.
* **Administrative and operational support:** Deducting member contributions via payroll, providing necessary data to scheme administrators, and often bearing the cost of administration, actuarial advisory, and regulatory levies.
* **Governance interaction:** The employer usually has the power to appoint some or all of the trustees (subject to member-nominated trustee requirements) and typically must be consulted regarding, or agree to, the Statement of Investment Principles and the actuarial valuation basis.
* **Scheme modification:** The employer retains the power to propose changes to the scheme design (e.g., closing to new entrants, changing accrual rates, or ceasing future accrual entirely), subject to consultation requirements.

### Part (ii) [5 marks]

Under accounting standards, the key disclosure requirements in the sponsoring employer’s financial statements regarding the risks of an existing DB pension scheme include:
* **Description of the risks:** A qualitative explanation covering how the scheme exposes the employer to specific risks, such as investment volatility, interest rate risk, inflation risk, and longevity risk.
* **Sensitivity analysis:** Quantitative disclosures showing how the defined benefit obligation (liability) and the current service cost would be impacted if key actuarial assumptions (e.g., discount rate, inflation rate, life expectancy) were altered by a reasonable margin.
* **Asset matching/LDI strategies:** Information about any strategies the scheme has in place to manage or mitigate risk, such as liability-driven investment (LDI), use of derivatives, or journey plans to reduce balance sheet volatility.
* **Funding arrangements:** Details regarding the funding policy agreed with trustees, the expected cash contributions required for the next reporting period, and the agreed recovery plan for addressing any funding deficit.
* **Asset categorisation:** A detailed breakdown of the fair value of the plan assets by distinct class (e.g., quoted equities, government bonds, property, unquoted assets, derivatives).
* **Maturity profile:** Information on the weighted average duration of the defined benefit obligation or the expected timing profile of future benefit cash outflows.

### Part (iii) [6 marks]

Three alternative methods the actuary might use to determine the discount rate for the current year's pension cost in financial statements (in situations where a single yield from an index is deemed insufficient) are:

1. **Yield curve approach:** Instead of using a single flat yield from a bond index, the actuary constructs a full corporate bond yield curve spanning all durations. The expected future benefit cash flows of the scheme are projected year-by-year and discounted using the specific spot rate from the yield curve that corresponds precisely to the timing of each cash flow. This creates a more accurate, duration-matched single equivalent discount rate.
2. **Bond matching portfolio (Synthetic portfolio):** The actuary identifies a theoretical, dedicated portfolio of high-quality corporate bonds whose aggregate principal and coupon cash flows best match the expected benefit payment profile of the scheme. The internal rate of return (IRR) of this specific theoretical bond portfolio is then calculated and adopted as the discount rate, which removes the basis risk inherent in a generic corporate bond index.
3. **Reference curve with extrapolation:** If there is a lack of deep and liquid high-quality corporate bonds at the very long durations required (which is common for mature pension liabilities), the actuary could use observable market yields for the shorter durations where a deep market exists and then extrapolate the yield curve out to long durations. This extrapolation might be based on macro-economic models, government bond curves plus a long-term credit spread assumption, or blending to an ultimate forward rate.

## Question 4

### Part (i) [4 marks]

The key financial and demographic assumptions the actuarial adviser would need to estimate the cost of transferring the Scheme to a consolidator include:
* **Financial Assumptions:**
  * **Discount rate:** To value the liabilities. This will likely be based on a risk-free rate curve (e.g., gilt yields or swap rates) plus a carefully determined risk margin reflecting the consolidator's targeted, heavily collateralised investment strategy.
  * **Inflation rate:** Assumptions for RPI and CPI curves, which are needed to project future pension increases in payment and revaluation in deferment.
  * **Pension increases:** Projected rates of increase for different tranches of benefits reflecting scheme caps and collars (e.g., LPI capped at 5%).
* **Demographic Assumptions:**
  * **Base mortality:** The current life expectancy of members based on standard tables, adjusted using postcode or scheme-specific experience data.
  * **Mortality improvements:** Future trends in longevity, usually using a standard projection model (such as the CMI model) with an appropriate long-term improvement rate.
  * **Family statistics:** Proportion of members married/with dependants at death, and the age difference between spouses, for assessing the value of contingent survivor pensions.

### Part (ii) [5 marks]

Reasons why the estimated cost of Option A (Consolidator at £10m) and Option B (Insurance buy-out at £40m) are different to each other:
* **Capital reserving regimes:** Insurers are subject to strict regulatory capital regimes (e.g., Solvency II/Solvency UK), which force them to hold extremely large capital buffers with a very high probability of sufficiency. Consolidators operate under a bespoke, often less stringent statutory capital regime allowing for lower reserves.
* **Investment strategy restrictions:** Insurers are heavily constrained in their asset allocation to closely match liabilities (mostly holding Gilts and matching adjustment eligible corporate bonds). Consolidators have more freedom to invest a proportion of their assets in a wider array of return-seeking assets to target higher long-term returns, lowering the upfront required premium.
* **Profit margins for shareholders:** Insurers must provide a commercial return on the extensive capital their shareholders have locked away over decades. The consolidator's capital providers may have different hurdle rates or structural efficiencies requiring a smaller embedded profit margin in the premium.
* **Level of guarantee / Residual Risk:** An insurance buy-out unconditionally and fundamentally guarantees the benefits will be paid. A consolidator transaction severs the employer link but replaces it with the consolidator's capital buffer; if that fails, members could theoretically suffer benefit cuts. This fractionally higher residual risk translates directly to a cheaper price.

### Part (iii) [12 marks]

**From the viewpoint of the Company:**
* *Benefits:* Both options achieve the company's ultimate objective of severing the link with the Scheme completely ("clean break"), removing the ongoing balance of cost risk, removing balance sheet volatility, and freeing up management time. Option A accomplishes this at a substantially lower cash cost (£10m vs £40m).
* *Risks:* Under Option A, if the consolidator's framework is not robust and it later fails (falling into the PPF), there may still be severe reputational damage to the company for offloading the scheme cheaply, even if legal obligations were severed.
* *Implications:* The company only has a £5m budget. Neither option is immediately affordable without further negotiation, deferred payment plans, or capital raising.

**From the viewpoint of the Scheme's Trustees:**
* *Benefits:* Option B is the ultimate "gold standard" of benefit security. It guarantees benefits are met by a highly regulated entity (backed by the industry compensation scheme, FSCS) and fully discharges the trustees of their duties. Option A also permanently removes reliance on a potentially deteriorating employer covenant and replaces it with a dedicated capital pool.
* *Risks:* For Option A, trustees must carry out immense due diligence to get comfortable that the consolidator's structure, covenant, and capital buffer are sufficiently strong to replace the current employer link. If the consolidator fails, the trustees could be criticized for not holding out for a full buy-out.
* *Implications:* Trustees have a fiduciary duty to act in members' best interests. They would need compelling evidence that transferring to the consolidator significantly improves member security relative to maintaining the status quo with the current employer covenant.

**From the viewpoint of the Scheme members:**
* *Benefits:* Their pensions are secured inside a robust financial vehicle and entirely disconnected from their sponsor's potential future trading or insolvency difficulties. Option B provides absolute peace of mind.
* *Risks:* If the trustees choose Option A over Option B, members lose the link to their former employer, but the consolidator is generally perceived as carrying a marginally higher risk of insolvency than a Solvency II regulated insurer.
* *Implications:* Scheme members are likely to feel unsettled about their pensions being transferred to a novel "consolidator" compared to a household-name insurance company. The change will require very careful communication to reassure them that their benefits remain secure.

### Part (iv) [4 marks]

Actions the company may take to achieve its aim of removing all risks given the £5 million maximum limit:
1. **Phased transfer to consolidator / Deferral arrangements:** The company could proceed with Option A by paying the £5m upfront and establishing a legally binding escrow or deferred payment schedule to pay the remaining £5m to the consolidator over a period of years as cashflow permits inside the company.
2. **Implementation of liability management exercises (LMEs):** The company could fund and execute an exercise such as a Pension Increase Exchange (PIE) or an Enhanced Transfer Value (ETV) exercise. If enough members accept the options, the size of the total liabilities shrinks, naturally reducing the consolidator shortfall down to £5m or less.
3. **A de-risked Run-on mechanism (Journey Plan):** Pay the £5m into the scheme now, whilst simultaneously adjusting the investment strategy to highly optimize LDI hedging alongside targeted growth assets. Maintain the status quo for 3-5 years, aiming for asset outperformance to organically bridge the remaining £5m gap before transacting with the consolidator.
4. **Partial buy-in/transfer structure:** Transfer only a specific demographic subset of the liabilities (e.g., older pensioners) to the consolidator or an insurer that the current assets plus £5m will comfortably cover, isolating risks, leaving a heavily reduced residuary scheme on the balance sheet.

## Question 5

### Part (i) [4 marks]

The Scheme's funding level on a buy-out basis (85%) is significantly lower than on the Scheme's funding basis (99%) for the following reasons:
* **The discount rate formulation:** The buy-out discount rate reflects the pricing of an insurance company, which is heavily constrained by regulation to hold low-yielding matching adjustment assets (primarily Gilts). The ongoing funding basis likely uses a higher discount rate that anticipates additional risk premium/future returns on the scheme's growth assets (e.g., the 20% allocated to equities).
* **Mortality/Longevity prudence:** An insurer taking on the liabilities eternally will inject extreme prudence (longevity risk margins) compared to the "best estimate plus a smaller margin" typically used for a scheme's ongoing funding assessment.
* **Profit margin and capital cost:** The buy-out basis embeds the insurer's commercial profit margin and the cost of servicing the regulatory Solvency capital requirement, neither of which feature in an ongoing scheme valuation.
* **Capitalisation of expenses:** The insurer will capitalize all future administration, transitional, and investment expenses out for decades into the upfront liability value. The ongoing scheme valuation often accounts for some portion of expenses through future employer contributions as they arise.

### Part (ii) [10 marks]

**Changing the Scheme’s funding method to the basic form of the current unit method:**
* *Impact:* A mature scheme that is closed to new members and to future accrual (as this one is) typically evaluates past service liabilities using the Projected Unit Method (PUM).
* For a scheme completely closed to accrual, members' benefits are already crystallised and based on final salary at the date of closure. Revaluations in deferment are statutory indexation, not salary linkage.
* Because the scheme is closed to future accrual, there are no active members accruing future service. Therefore, predicting future salary increases for past service calculation purposes is irrelevant. 
* Consequently, the Projected Unit method effectively equates perfectly to the Current Unit method. 
* Therefore, changing the funding methodology to the Current Unit method will have **nil or strictly minimal impact** on the calculation of the defined benefit obligation, the deficit, and thus the deficit reduction contributions required from the employer.

**Increasing the proportion of the Scheme’s assets invested in equities to 30%:**
* *Impact:* Moving allocations from government bonds to equities (from 20% to 30%) shifts the portfolio toward higher-risk, higher-expected-return assets.
* If the actuary utilizes an asset-led discount rate methodology for the ongoing valuation, they will consequently increase the discount rate to account for the higher expected outperformance of a 30% equity portfolio.
* An increased discount rate mathematically reduces the present value of the scheme's liabilities on the ongoing funding basis.
* This would improve the ongoing funding level (likely pushing it from 99% to comfortably over 100%) and extinguish the technical provisions deficit.
* Consequently, the scheme would be fully funded, and the required deficit reduction contributions from the employer would cease entirely, fulfilling the finance team's goal.
* However, this severely increases the investment, volatility, and downside risk. Should the equities perform poorly, the scheme could swing back into a massive deficit, triggering erratic and much more severe future contribution demands on the employer.

### Part (iii) [2 marks]

These measures may not be implemented in practice because:
1. **Trustee fiduciary duty and resistance:** The trustees have a legal duty to protect members' benefits and the scheme’s security. They are highly unlikely to agree to increase investment risk (30% equities) in a mature, closed scheme primarily to relieve a financially strained employer, as it heavily increases downside risk for members.
2. **Actuarial/Regulatory intervention:** The scheme actuary must certify that the assumptions and funding methods are robust and prudent. Furthermore, the Pensions Regulator explicitly monitors and discourages covenant-weak employers taking unjustifiable investment risks within scheme portfolios just to suppress deficit metrics.

### Part (iv) [7 marks]

Assessing the employer's covenant is critical when they alert the trustees to trading difficulties. The trustees could assess the covenant through:

1. **In-house assessment using direct management information:**
   * *Advantages:* Highly cost-effective; very fast; utilizes the trustees' existing intimate knowledge of the employer's operations; allows for private, frank discussions with the company's financial directors.
   * *Disadvantages:* Trustees generally lack forensic restructuring or deep financial analysis expertise; there are often massive conflicts of interest if some trustees are also company directors; output may lack objectivity.

2. **Commissioning an independent covenant review by a specialist external firm:**
   * *Advantages:* Completely objective and independent; accesses expert corporate restructuring accountants; provides a robust benchmark against sector peers; thoroughly satisfies regulatory documentation and governance expectations.
   * *Disadvantages:* Highly expensive, which adds further financial strain to the scheme or the struggling company itself; it takes significant time to produce a robust report.

3. **Using credit rating agencies' metrics and public accounts analysis:**
   * *Advantages:* Data is easy, fast, and very cheap to access; allows for continuous monitoring of the company's bond yields or public credit scores without intrusion.
   * *Disadvantages:* Backward-looking (trailing indicators); only available for large/listed entities; may not accurately reflect the specific, direct legal recourse the scheme has against the exact sponsoring entity in a complex corporate group.

### Part (v) [4 marks]

Actions the Scheme’s trustees could take to reduce the employer’s immediate cash contributions, without compromising the long-term security of members’ benefits:
1. **Extend the length of the recovery plan:** Allowing the employer to spread the deficit reduction payments over a longer duration (e.g., stretching it out so the annual cash burden drops significantly).
2. **Back-end loading the recovery plan:** Restructuring the schedule so that cash contributions are very low over the immediate years (during trading difficulties) but strictly step up significantly later when trading is legally and expectedly projected to recover.
3. **Accepting contingent assets in lieu of cash:** Taking security via contingent assets (e.g., a priority legal charge over company real estate, negative pledges, or a parent company group guarantee) to secure the deficit without draining the employer's immediate operating cash flow.
4. **Funding from scheme surplus/suspending administrative payments:** Temporarily allowing scheme administration, advisory, and PPF levy expenses to be paid from the scheme's own asset returns rather than being recharged directly to the sponsor's cash balance sheet.

### Part (vi) [12 marks]

The employer is now insolvent, meaning it can no longer support the scheme. The scheme has an ongoing funding level of 108% and a buy-out level of 92%. The trustees could consider the following THREE methods to ensure benefit provision:

**1. Wind-up and execution of a Partial Buy-out with an Insurer**
* *Description / How it works:* Since the scheme cannot afford a full buy-out (92% funded), the trustees officially wind up the scheme and instruct an insurance company to supply individual annuity policies for members with the available assets. Because assets are insufficient, benefits must be irrevocably scaled back according to the strict statutory priority order in that jurisdiction.
* *Risks:* Members (typically deferred pensioners and those with non-guaranteed discretionary increases, given their low rank in the priority order) will face permanent, severe reductions to their legitimately accrued pension benefits.
* *Benefits:* Provides absolute finality. The scaled-back benefits are permanently secured, backed by Solvency II reserving regulations, and guaranteed by an insurance company (and covered by the FSCS), permanently shielding members from any future investment or longevity risks.

**2. Scheme 'Run-on' as a Closed Scheme without a Sponsor**
* *Description / How it works:* The trustees choose not to wind up, instead electing to continue operating the scheme as an independent trust. Relying entirely on the current assets (108% on an ongoing basis), they de-risk the investment strategy and operate to pay out benefits directly as they fall due over the coming decades, hoping investment returns organically bridge the 8% absolute buy-out gap over time.
* *Risks:* Immensely high risk. Without a sponsoring employer covenant to fall back on, there is absolutely no safety net. If investments underperform or members live significantly longer than expected, the scheme will eventually run dry, and younger deferred members could face catastrophic cuts to their retirement income later. Furthermore, the scheme must bear burdensome ongoing fixed administrative and advisory costs permanently.
* *Benefits:* Gives a mathematical and practical chance of paying 100% of the originally promised benefits (since they are fully funded on a best estimate basis) without forcing an immediate 8% catastrophic cut via an insurer partial buy-out.

**3. Transfer to a Commercial Consolidator (Superfund)**
* *Description / How it works:* The scheme securely transfers all its assets and liabilities into a non-insurance commercial consolidator (assuming regulatory approval). Following the mechanics in Question 4, consolidators require a smaller capital buffer upfront. It is highly plausible that their 92% buy-out funding level equates to ~100% funding on the consolidator's pricing metric, allowing a full transfer without requiring further cash injections.
* *Risks:* The long-term security is marginally lower than an insurance buy-out. Members rely on the consolidator's private capital buffer; if the consolidator mathematically fails decades down the line, members could suffer future benefit reductions or fall into a compensation scheme (PPF).
* *Benefits:* Much like the run-on option, this avoids an immediate cut in benefits and maintains the 100% promise. Critically, it does so much more securely than running on alone because it transfers the demographic and investment risk into an aggregated, massively scaled vehicle with a dedicated pool of risk capital shielding the members.
