## Question 1

**Draft Paper for the Trustees of the ABC Pension Scheme**

**To:** The Trustees of the ABC Pension Scheme
**From:** Sidney Green, Actuary, Top Consultants
**Date:** 21 February 2026
**Subject:** Understanding the "Broke Judgement" and its impact on the Scheme 

### 1. Introduction
Recently, an important legal ruling known as the "Broke Judgement" was issued by the High Court. This ruling changes how pension schemes like ours must calculate certain pension payments. This paper explains what the ruling means, sets out two available options for updating our approach, and illustrates how these options would affect members. We must decide which option to implement within one year from the ruling date (1 September 2025).

### 2. Basic Details of the Broke Judgement
Historically, under the ABC Pension Scheme rules, a man and a woman with exactly the same earnings and years of service could receive different pension amounts at different ages. This difference arose because parts of their pensions increased at different rates each year. Typically, a man might receive a higher pension immediately after retiring, while a woman's pension would catch up and eventually be higher at older ages.

The Broke Judgement has ruled that this is no longer legally acceptable. The ruling requires that, for all members retiring on or after 1 September 2025, men and women who have identical work histories must be guaranteed the exact same pension payment from the scheme from retirement age onwards.

### 3. The Two Options Available
The judgement permits pension schemes to choose one of two ways to comply with the equalisation requirement:

- **Option 1: Always Pay the Higher Amount.** Under this option, the scheme would simply compare what a man and a woman would have received at any given age and always pay whichever is higher. 
- **Option 2: Offset Differences Later.** Under this option, the scheme still ensures overall equality but takes a different path. If a member receives a higher pension in their early retirement years than the opposite sex would have received, the scheme is allowed to temporarily limit future pension increases to "offset" these extra payments. Once the extra amounts paid out early on are balanced out, the pension aligns fully with the higher amount going forward.

### 4. How the Options May Impact Members
To illustrate how the two options impact members in practice, we have prepared two tables based on a typical scheme member who retires at age 60 under the new rules. The figures compare Annual Pension values and Annual Increases every 5 years for a member whose baseline initial pension is $1,000 a year.

**Table 1: Annual Pension Paid at Sample Ages (Options 1 & 2)**

| Age | Option 1 Amount ($) | Option 2 Amount ($) |
|---|---|---|
| 60 | 1,000.00 | 1,000.00 |
| 65 | 1,146.26 | 1,146.26 |
| 70 | 1,322.32 | 1,319.40 |
| 75 | 1,591.80 | 1,525.43 |
| 80 | 1,925.79 | 1,925.79 |

As we can see in Table 1, under Option 1, the total pension amount grows steadily and is always equal to or higher than Option 2. Under Option 2, the pension grows more slowly between ages 65 and 75. However, by age 80, the two options catch up to each other and pay the same total amount from there onwards.

**Table 2: Annual Pension Increase Rates at Sample Ages**

| Age | Option 1 Increase (%) | Option 2 Increase (%) |
|---|---|---|
| 60 | 0.0 | 0.0 |
| 65 | 2.8 | 2.8 |
| 70 | 3.1 | 2.9 |
| 75 | 3.8 | 3.0 |
| 80 | 3.9 | 3.9 |

Table 2 highlights a potential downside of Option 2 from a member's perspective. The annual percentage increases for Option 1 grow smoothly over retirement. Option 2, however, involves more restricted and erratic annual increases in the middle years before sharply catching up. This means members under Option 2 may experience periods of lower-than-expected increases, making household budgeting more difficult. This erratic pattern aligns with the concerns raised in the newspaper article.

### 5. Other Relevant Aspects to Consider
Before making a decision, the trustees should also weigh the following points:

- **Cost to the Scheme:** Our estimated costs project that adopting Option 1 would require holding an additional $149,875 to cover the higher ongoing pension payouts. Option 2 would initially suppress payout increases, resulting in a somewhat lower additional cost of $120,135 to the scheme.
- **Administrative Complexity:** The ongoing administration costs for running Option 1 and Option 2 are the same. Our administration team has already successfully updated their systems to process whichever option we choose.
- **Member Communication and Queries:** Because Option 2 causes erratic pension increases, members are far more likely to be confused and complain. If dissatisfied members begin calling the administration helpline to ask about their smaller-than-expected increases, the Scheme will incur extra inquiry charges from the administrators (expected to be nearly $10,000). A smooth approach under Option 1 is simpler to communicate and is likely to result in fewer complaints and lower inquiry charges.

**Summary:** The choice essentially lies between Option 1, which provides smoother, easier-to-understand increases for members but costs the scheme more in upfront pension funding, and Option 2, which saves the scheme some money on payouts but could confuse members, disrupt their personal budgeting, and lead to additional administration query costs.

### Part (i) [16 marks]

**(a) Describe how you decided which graphs or tables to include.**

I chose to include two tables: one showing the absolute pension amount payable at various sample ages, and a second showing the annual percentage increase at those same ages. I opted for tables rather than line graphs because the primary medium for this communication paper was text and the trustees previously complimented clear numerical illustrations. To decide these metrics:
1. Identifying the core message to communicate: The prompt specifically alluded to the newspaper article’s concern regarding the erratic pension increases from Option 2 versus the smooth increases from Option 1.
2. Demonstrating absolute impact (Table 1): I provided the total payable pension amount over a snapshot of ages (every 5 years between 60 and 80) to give trustees a sense of proportion of what is changing in pounds and pence.
3. Highlighting the erratic nature (Table 2): I provided the annual increase percentages explicitly because the discrepancy is much clearer to see numerically when the percentage rate suddenly stalls and alters format relative to a smooth projection.

**(b) Justify any decisions you made for formatting the graphs or tables.**

**Formatting Table 1 (Absolute Amounts):**
- **Interval sampling:** To avoid overwhelming the non-technical trustees with 40 rows of continuous data, I extracted the numbers in 5-year intervals. This clearly demonstrates the trend without unnecessary clutter.
- **Currency symbols and decimal precision:** I included the `$` symbol in the header and presented values to two decimal places to reinforce that these represent real cash amounts impacting a member's pocket.
- **Side-by-side arrangement:** The columns for Option 1 and Option 2 are placed directly adjacent to allow simple, intuitive comparison at each specific age.

**Formatting Table 2 (Percentage Increases):**
- **Using percentages:** Representing the increase as a percentage helps ground the measure against factors members usually track, like general inflation. I included `%` in the column headers for clarity.
- **Decimal alignment:** Ensuring a consistent single decimal point for the percentages allows the trustees to read the table neatly and quickly ascertain that changes represent small but tangible percentage points.
- **Cross-referencing structure:** I kept the identical 5-year sampling ages as Table 1 so the reader could mentally map the percentage increase back to the nominal cash effect occurring at that matching age.

**Give three examples of jargon from the scenario material or exam question that you decided to exclude from your paper. For each example, please explain the alternative terminology that you used.**

1. **Jargon Excluded:** "Liabilities" (and "Additional Liabilities")
   *Alternative terminology:* I replaced this with "Estimated costs" and "Additional cost". Trustees without a technical actuarial background better understand 'costs' as the money required to fund the change, rather than the complicated discounted present cash flow value calculation implied by 'liabilities'.
2. **Jargon Excluded:** "Cross-over age (N)" and mathematical formulas
   *Alternative terminology:* I completely removed the algebraic formula evaluating `N`, instead describing the concept simply in words: "when the extra amounts paid out early on are balanced out, the pension aligns fully". This conveys the impact and outcome without needing algebra or abstract variables.
3. **Jargon Excluded:** Reference to "Funding best estimate basis", demographic basis assumptions, or specific mortality tables (e.g., "Continuous Mortality Association 2021"). 
   *Alternative terminology:* These were omitted outright. I simply framed the cost projections as "Our estimated costs project..." because explaining demographic assumptions, yield curve discounts, and mortality improvements diverges greatly from what the trustees actually need to know to make their decision between Option 1 and 2.
