## Question 1

### Part (i) [4 marks]

The trustees of a pension scheme would wish to measure the performance of the scheme's investments for several important reasons:
*   **Monitoring achievement of objectives:** To determine whether the overall investment strategy is meeting the scheme's primary long-term objectives (e.g., generating sufficient returns to meet future pension liabilities, acting as a check on the asset-liability modelling).
*   **Manager accountability and evaluation:** To hold the appointed investment managers accountable against their specific mandates, risk parameters, and benchmark targets.
*   **Skill versus luck:** To assess whether any outperformance is due to genuine and repeatable manager skill, or simply due to luck or taking on unrewarded market risk.
*   **Decisions on manager retention:** To provide objective data that informs decisions on whether to retain, replace, or reallocate capital away from underperforming managers.
*   **Strategy review:** To help inform and shape the formulation of future investment strategy and asset allocation decisions, highlighting which asset classes are adding value.
*   **Reporting and communication:** To provide transparent and accurate information for the trustees to report back to the scheme sponsors (the employer) and the scheme members regarding the financial health of the scheme.
*   **Fee calculations:** Where managers have performance-related fee structures, accurate measurement is required to calculate the fees owed.
*   **Risk monitoring:** To ensure that the returns achieved are commensurate with the level of risk originally agreed to and mandated by the trustees.

### Part (ii) [5 marks]

Measuring the performance of an infrastructure mandate presents several specific challenges for the trustees:
*   **Illiquidity and infrequent valuation:** Infrastructure investments are unlisted and highly illiquid. There are no daily market prices. Valuations are typically infrequent (e.g., quarterly or annually) and rely on subjective appraisal methods (like discounted cash flows) rather than observable market transactions. This subjectivity makes performance figures less robust.
*   **Lack of suitable benchmarks:** Finding an appropriate benchmark for unlisted infrastructure is notoriously difficult. Listed infrastructure indices may have a high correlation to broader equity markets and possess different risk, liquidity, and leverage characteristics compared to direct, unlisted infrastructure projects.
*   **Irregular cash flows:** Capital is often drawn down irregularly as construction progresses, and distributions are paid out sporadically. This renders simple Time-Weighted Returns (TWR) inappropriate; trustees must instead use Internal Rate of Return (IRR) or cash multiples (like Multiple on Invested Capital, MOIC).
*   **The "J-curve" effect:** In the early years of an infrastructure fund or project, returns are often negative because capital is drawn to pay management fees, acquisition costs, and construction expenses before the asset becomes operational and starts generating yield. This early illusion of poor performance requires patience to evaluate properly.
*   **High asset specificity (idiosyncratic risk):** Every infrastructure asset (e.g., a specific toll road or wind farm) is unique. Regulatory environments, construction risks, and local political risks mean that comparing one manager's infrastructure returns with a peer group is difficult because the underlying assets are not homogenous.
*   **Leverage variations:** Infrastructure investments often utilise significant and varying degrees of debt (project finance). Two managers might produce the same return but take on vastly different levels of financial risk through leverage, complicating 'like-for-like' performance measurement.
*   **Currency effects:** If the infrastructure mandate is global, long-term unhedged currency movements can significantly distort the underlying asset's performance when translated back to the scheme's base currency.

## Question 2

### Part (i) [2 marks]

#### (a) Overconfidence
Overconfidence bias occurs when investors overestimate their knowledge, skill, and ability to predict the market. This explains the **portfolio turnover** (70% for individuals vs 15% for the fund): overconfident investors believe they have superior information or insight and thus trade frequently in an attempt to outsmart the market. It also explains the **higher beta (1.40) and smaller market capitalisation ($800m)**: overconfident investors generally underestimate risks, leading them to hold under-diversified portfolios concentrated in riskier, more volatile (higher beta), and smaller, potentially high-growth companies.

#### (b) Disposition effect
The disposition effect is the psychological tendency of investors to sell assets that have increased in value too early (to lock in the pride of a "win") while simultaneously holding onto assets that have decreased in value for too long (to avoid the regret of realising a "loss"). This leads to poor overall **investment performance** (12.5% vs 17.9%) because the individuals cut their winners short, missing out on further momentum gains, while letting their losers run, dragging down overall portfolio returns. 

### Part (ii) [7 marks]

Possible non-behavioural reasons for the difference in net annualised performance include:
*   **Frictional trading costs:** The individuals have an annual turnover of almost five times that of the index fund (70% vs 15%). The frequent trading will incur substantial transaction costs, including broker commissions, bid-offer spreads, and market impact costs, all of which directly erode net returns.
*   **Economies of scale:** Small individual investors face structurally higher transaction and administrative costs compared to a large, multi-billion-dollar index fund that negotiates institutional rates and achieves immense economies of scale.
*   **Style bias over the period:** The tracking fund has a median market cap of $6bn (large-cap bias), while the individuals lean toward $800m (small-mid cap bias). Over this specific 10-year period, large-cap equities may have simply outperformed small-cap equities on a systematic, macroeconomic basis.
*   **Unrewarded idiosyncratic risk:** The tracking fund is almost perfectly diversified by design. Individual portfolios are typically heavily concentrated in a small number of stocks. This exposes the individuals to massive stock-specific risk that is not compensated by higher expected returns, likely leading to worse median outcomes.
*   **Cash drag:** Individuals often hold a proportion of their portfolio in cash (due to dividends received, pending trades, or market timing attempts). Given that equities generally outperformed over this period (as evidenced by the 17.9% return), holding cash would dilute the individuals' compounded returns compared to the fully-invested tracking fund.
*   **Dividend reinvestment:** Institutional index funds automatically and immediately reinvest dividends. Individual investors may delay reinvestment, face higher friction to reinvest small amounts, or consume the dividends entirely. If the performance metric includes total return, the lag in reinvesting severely hampers compounding.
*   **Information and resources:** Individual investors operate with asymmetric information. They lack the institutional-grade research, direct management access, algorithmic execution, and sophisticated data terminals that the market (and thus the tracking index) internalises. 
*   **Adverse selection (Market Timing):** The average retail investor may exhibit poor timing of cash flows—buying into the market at peaks when sentiment is exuberant and withdrawing at the bottom during panics. A tracking fund passively holds the index regardless of sentiment.

### Part (iii) [4 marks]

Possible non-behavioural reasons for the difference in annual portfolio turnover include:
*   **Liquidity requirements:** Individuals face real-life liquidity needs. They may need to sell equities to fund major purchases (e.g., a house deposit, education costs), manage unemployment, or adjust for retirement income. The tracking fund only trades when it receives net inflows/outflows or when the underlying index composition changes.
*   **Tax management:** Individuals may actively trade to manage their specific tax liabilities (e.g., tax-loss harvesting to offset gains elsewhere, or selling to utilise an annual tax-free capital gains allowance before the fiscal year ends). An institutional tracking fund has a different core tax structure that rarely drives this type of active rotation.
*   **Lifecycle asset allocation shifts:** As individuals age over a 10-year period, prudent financial planning dictates "lifestyling"—gradually selling down equities to rebalance into lower-risk assets like government bonds. 
*   **Corporate actions and unbundled activity:** If an individual investor holds an individual company that is acquired for cash, or spins out a subsidiary, this creates forced turnover. An index fund might simply adjust its holdings mechanically, maintaining very low turnover (e.g., 15%). Furthermore, individuals might trade across smaller positional sizes making the percentage churn look larger relative to holding pure, broad indices.

## Question 3

### Part (i) [3 marks]

Three distinct performance metrics based on past results are:
1.  **Sharpe Ratio:** This measures the excess return of the portfolio (return above the risk-free rate) divided by the standard deviation of the portfolio's returns. It assesses the risk-adjusted return relative to total risk.
2.  **Information Ratio (IR):** This calculates the portfolio's active return (the return above the benchmark index) divided by the tracking error (the standard deviation of the active return). It evaluates how much excess return the manager achieved per unit of active risk taken.
3.  **Treynor Ratio:** This measures the excess return of the portfolio over the risk-free rate, divided by the portfolio's systematic risk (its Beta). It is used to assess risk-adjusted performance when the portfolio forms part of a larger, well-diversified fund.

### Part (ii) [3 marks]

Reliance on a small number of quantitative metrics may fail to provide a complete picture for the following reasons:
*   **Backward-looking nature:** Metrics rely entirely on past data, and past performance is not a reliable indicator of future results. They completely ignore forward-looking indicators and qualitative factors such as staff turnover, changes in investment process, or changes to the firm's ownership structure.
*   **Statistical assumptions:** Many metrics (such as the Sharpe Ratio) assume that investment returns are normally distributed. In reality, returns—particularly in alternative assets—exhibit negative skewness and 'fat tails' (kurtosis). A manager might appear to have a great Sharpe ratio while hiding extreme tail risk.
*   **Benchmark dependency and manipulation:** Metrics like the Information Ratio are highly sensitive to the choice of benchmark. If an inappropriate benchmark is selected, the metric is meaningless. Furthermore, quantitative metrics can be susceptible to 'window dressing' or short-term risk manipulation by the manager just prior to reporting dates.

### Part (iii) [3 marks]

Private equity causes specific difficulties in evaluating scheme performance because:
*   **Illiquidity and subjective valuations:** Private equity investments are not publicly traded, meaning there are no observable 'mark-to-market' prices. Valuations are infrequent, smoothed, and based on subjective management or third-party appraisals, leading to understated volatility and artificially inflated traditional risk-adjusted return metrics.
*   **Cash flow control:** In private equity, the manager (General Partner) has control over the timing and magnitude of cash flows (capital calls and distributions). This cash flow uncertainty disrupts the calculation of Time-Weighted Returns (TWR), which is the standard method used for listed equities.
*   **Lack of direct comparators:** Identifying a suitable benchmark is highly problematic due to the unique characteristics, varying vintage years, and extreme dispersion of returns among private equity vehicles. Standard equity indices do not reflect the leverage and illiquidity inherent in private equity.

### Part (iv) [3 marks]

To address these private equity evaluation difficulties, the manager could:
*   **Use money-weighted returns (Internal Rate of Return - IRR):** Since the manager controls cash flows, the "Since Inception IRR" should be used instead of TWR to accurately reflect the performance based on capital actually deployed and returned.
*   **Implement cash multiples:** Assess performance using absolute cash-on-cash metrics that don't rely on subjective interim valuations, such as the Multiple on Invested Capital (MOIC) or Distributions to Paid-In capital (DPI).
*   **Utilise Public Market Equivalents (PME) and Vintage comparisons:** Evaluate performance against a Public Market Equivalent (which assumes the same cash flows were invested in a public index like the S&P 500) and compare the fund strictly against a peer-group benchmark of funds from the exact same 'vintage year' to strip out macroeconomic timing factors.

## Question 4

### Part (i) [3 marks]

The general principles that the legislation should promote to ensure safety include:
*   **Consumer Protection and Fair Treatment:** A bedrock principle to ensure that participants (many of whom will be financially unsophisticated) are shielded from excessive risk, fraud, and misselling, fulfilling a broad "Treating Customers Fairly" mandate.
*   **Prudential Solvency and Financial Stability:** Ensuring that the financial services companies managing the money are financially robust, adequately capitalised, and closely monitored to prevent provider default and systemic failure.
*   **Transparency and Disclosure:** Mandating completely transparent, accurate, and comprehensible disclosure regarding charges, risks, and where money is being invested, ensuring no hidden fees erode the retirement savings.
*   **Good Governance:** Promoting strong internal oversight within the companies, ensuring accountability, mitigation of conflicts of interest, and alignment between the providers and the end-investors.

### Part (ii) [3 marks]

To safeguard customer assets, the financial companies should be structured with:
*   **Ring-fencing / Segregation of client assets:** The pension assets MUST be kept entirely legally separate from the financial services company's own corporate balance sheet. This ensures that if the company goes bankrupt, creditors cannot access the pension scheme's capital.
*   **Independent Custodians:** The physical holding and safekeeping of the assets should be undertaken by a regulated, independent third-party custodian rather than the asset manager themselves.
*   **Independent Governance Boards/Committees:** The scheme should be overseen by an independent board of trustees or an independent governance committee (IGC) whose primary fiduciary duty is to assess the value-for-money and act solely in the best interests of the scheme members, separated from the commercial aims of the financial services firm.
*   **Adequate Capitalisation:** The corporate entity must be structured with sufficient regulatory capital continuously maintained to absorb operational shocks without impacting client funds.

### Part (iii) [3 marks]

In relation to dealings with customers, the proposed legislation should contain:
*   **Standardised pre-investment disclosures:** Mandatory provision of a 'Key Features Document' (or equivalent) in simple, jargon-free language outlining the risks, the investment objective, and a unified, standardised breakdown of all costs and charges (e.g. Total Expense Ratio or Ongoing Charges Figure) before investment.
*   **Mandatory default funds and charge capping:** Because it is a compulsory initiative, many employees will be disengaged. Legislation must mandate a robust, diversified, lifecycle 'default fund' for those who do not actively choose, and importantly, impose a strict cap on the charges for this default fund.
*   **Regular communications and redress:** A requirement to provide clear annual benefit statements (showing current value, charges deducted, and projected retirement paths). There must also be a formal, transparent complaints procedure and access to an independent financial ombudsman or compensation scheme in the event of maladministration.

### Part (iv) [5 marks]

Potential investors (employees) should look for the following information before deciding where to invest their compulsory contributions:
*   **Costs and Total Charges:** This is arguably the most crucial decision factor, as high fees massively erode long-term compounding. Investors need to find the Total Expense Ratio (TER), including Annual Management Charges (AMC), platform fees, and underlying transaction costs.
*   **Investment Objectives and Risk Profiles:** Details of the range of funds available. An investor needs to ensure the fund aligns with their personal risk appetite, time horizon to retirement, and capacity for loss (e.g., choosing high equity exposure for a younger employee vs. greater fixed income for an older employee).
*   **Past Performance:** While not a reliable indicator of future results, investors will want to review medium and long-term (e.g., 5, 10-year) historical returns relative to an appropriate benchmark or peer group to check for consistent underperformance or high volatility.
*   **Provider Reputation, Size, and Strength:** Information on the financial security and market experience of the provider. While assets are separated, larger established providers may offer better security, better economies of scale, and advanced ongoing services.
*   **Platform Features and Usability:** The quality of the digital interface, educational materials, retirement forecasting calculators, and the degree of flexibility (such as how easy and costly it is to switch between funds or transfer out to another provider).
*   **Options at Retirement:** Clear information on the decumulation options the provider will eventually offer (e.g., flexible drawdown, annuity purchase, lump sums), ensuring the end-game aligns with the investor's retirement strategy.
*   **ESG Factors:** If the investor has strong ethical preferences, they should look for information on how the funds integrate Environmental, Social, and Governance scoring and active stewardship into their investment process.

## Question 5

### Part (i) [4 marks]

The manufacturing company may wish to increase its borrowings for several reasons:
*   **Capital Expenditure to fuel growth:** To finance major long-term structural expansion, such as purchasing new plant/machinery, expanding geographical operations, or acquiring a competitor, without diluting current ownership by issuing new equity.
*   **Managing working capital:** To smooth out short-term cash flow fluctuations in the business cycle, particularly to fund inventory build-ups or to cover the timing mismatch between paying suppliers and receiving payment from customers.
*   **Utilising the tax shield:** Interest payments on debt are generally tax-deductible (an allowable expense), whereas dividend payments on equity are not. Increasing borrowing can lower the firm's overall weighted average cost of capital (WACC) and decrease its tax burden.
*   **Refinancing existing obligations:** To replace existing, more expensive debt with cheaper debt, or to alter the maturity profile of their liabilities (e.g., paying off a short-term overdraft by issuing a long-term bond).
*   **Increasing shareholder returns:** By introducing financial leverage, the company can increase the potential Return on Equity (ROE) for its shareholders, provided the return on the new capital exceeds the interest rate of the debt. They might also borrow to fund a special dividend or a share repurchase scheme.

### Part (ii) [2 marks]

Sources of borrowing that may be open to the company include:
1.  **Bank debt:** This spans from short-term overdrafts and revolving credit facilities to long-term bilateral or syndicated bank loans.
2.  **Debt capital markets:** Issuing corporate bonds (debentures) or, for short term needs, commercial paper.
3.  **Alternative / Asset-backed finance:** Finance leasing, hire purchase, or factoring (invoice discounting).
4.  **Mezzanine finance:** Subordinated debt or debt with equity warrants attached.

### Part (iii) [5 marks]

**1. Bank loans (Medium/Long-term)**
*   *Advantages:* Can be arranged relatively quickly; flexible terms can be tailored to the company's needs; confidential (avoids the public disclosure required for bond issuance).
*   *Disadvantages:* Often heavily secured against company assets; typically comes with restrictive operating/financial covenants; frequently carries variable/floating interest rates, exposing the firm to interest rate risk.

**2. Corporate Bonds**
*   *Advantages:* Secures funding for very long periods; typically has a fixed interest rate (giving cost certainty); covenants are often less restrictive than tailored bank loans.
*   *Disadvantages:* High issuance costs (prospectus, underwriting fees, rating agencies); inflexible once issued (expensive to retire early); requires high levels of public disclosure and transparency; usually only viable for large borrowing amounts.

**3. Overdraft / Revolving Credit Facility**
*   *Advantages:* Extremely flexible for daily working capital needs; interest is only paid on the amount actively drawn down.
*   *Disadvantages:* Technically repayable on demand by the bank, making it a highly risky way to fund long-term assets; interest rates are variable and often high compared to secured long-term debt.

**4. Finance Leasing**
*   *Advantages:* Directly ties the financing to specific assets without needing lump sum capital up front; can sometimes be easier to obtain than general loans as the asset acts as security.
*   *Disadvantages:* The company does not gain legal ownership of the asset fully until the end of the term (if at all); overall cost over the lifetime is often higher than simply borrowing cash and buying the equipment outright.

### Part (iv) [2 marks]

Four ratios that a debt analyst may use to evaluate the company are:
1.  **Interest Cover (Times Interest Earned):** Measures the ability to service debt payments from operating profit.
2.  **Gearing (Leverage):** Measures the proportion of debt in the company's capital structure.
3.  **Current Ratio:** Measures short-term liquidity and the ability to meet immediate liabilities.
4.  **Quick Ratio (Acid Test):** Similar to the Current Ratio but excludes inventories, providing a more stringent test of immediate liquidity.

## Question 6

### Part (i) [2 marks]

**(a) Swaps:** An over-the-counter (OTC) derivative contract in which two counterparties agree to exchange a series of cash flows over a specified period of time, calculated on a notional principal amount.
**(b) Futures:** Standardised, exchange-traded derivative contracts that oblige the buyer to purchase (and the seller to deliver) a specific quantity of an underlying asset at a predetermined price on a specific future date.
**(c) Forwards:** Customised, over-the-counter (OTC) derivative contracts between two parties to buy or sell an asset at a specified price on a future date. (Unlike futures, they are not standardised or exchange-traded).
**(d) Options:** Derivative contracts that give the buyer the right, but crucially not the obligation, to buy (a call option) or sell (a put option) a specific quantity of an underlying asset at an agreed 'strike' price on or before a specified expiration date.

### Part (ii) [8 marks]

Main features of the four instruments:
**1. Swaps**
*   **Market:** OTC, meaning terms are privately negotiated, highly customisable, and generally illiquid.
*   **Cost:** Normally structured so that the initial present value is zero, requiring no upfront premium payment.
*   **Risk:** Counterparty credit risk is present, though it is usually mitigated through collateral agreements (ISDA annexes) and periodic netting.
*   **Usage:** Primarily used by institutions to manage/hedge interest rate risk (e.g., swapping floating rate payments for fixed rate) or currency exposure.

**2. Futures**
*   **Market:** Exchange-traded, providing a highly liquid and transparent secondary market.
*   **Standardisation:** The exchange rigidly defines the contract size, delivery grade, and expiry dates.
*   **Risk Mitigation:** The clearing house acts as the central counterparty to all trades, virtually eliminating counterparty credit risk.
*   **Margining:** Requires the deposit of an "initial margin" and is subject to daily "variation margin" (mark-to-market), meaning gains and losses are settled daily.

**3. Forwards**
*   **Market & Customisation:** OTC. They are tailored precisely to the hedging needs of the parties regarding the exact date, size, and underlying asset characteristics.
*   **Liquidity & Settlement:** Illiquid, typically held to maturity. Settlement usually occurs only once, at the final maturity date.
*   **Risk:** Carries significant counterparty credit risk, as there is usually no central clearing house and daily margining is less common than with futures.

**4. Options**
*   **Market:** Can be both exchange-traded (standardised) or OTC (customised).
*   **Asymmetric Payoff:** They possess a non-linear payoff profile. The buyer's downside risk is strictly limited to the premium paid, while possessing potentially unlimited upside. The seller (writer) faces limited upside (the premium) with potentially huge downside risks.
*   **Cost:** The buyer must pay an upfront cash premium to the seller to acquire this right. The seller must often post margin.

### Part (iii) [1 mark]

The future price is equal to the current spot price of the underlying asset, plus the cost of holding the asset (such as financing, storage, and insurance costs), minus the income generated by the asset (such as yield or dividends) over the period up to the delivery date.

### Part (iv) [4 marks]

Items expected to be explicitly detailed in a futures contract specification:
1.  **Contract Size:** The exact quantity of the underlying asset per single contract (e.g., 5,000 bushels of wheat).
2.  **Asset Quality/Grade:** The precise quality standard that is acceptable for delivery (e.g., No. 2 Soft Red Winter Wheat).
3.  **Delivery Months:** The specific calendar months in which the contract can be settled/delivered.
4.  **Minimum Price Fluctuation (Tick Size):** The smallest allowable increment by which the price can move.
5.  **Delivery Location/Method:** Where the physical asset must be delivered (e.g., specific exchange-approved silos), or whether the contract is cash-settled against an index.
6.  **Trading Hours:** The times during which the contract can be bought and sold on the exchange.
7.  **Daily Price Limits:** The maximum permitted percentage price change in a single trading session before trading is halted.

### Part (v) [6 marks]

If the farmer uses CME wheat futures to hedge their crop, they are exposed to several risks:
*   **Basis Risk:** This is the most significant risk. The futures price is based on the specific grade underlying the CME contract and delivery at specific US locations. The farmer's actual physical wheat may differ in quality or local market conditions. Therefore, the physical price they receive at their local elevator may diverge from the futures price, meaning the hedge is not perfectly effective.
*   **Liquidity / Margin Risk:** Because futures are marked-to-market daily, if the price of wheat spikes upward significantly prior to harvest, the farmer's short futures position will incur severe daily losses. The farmer will be hit with margin calls requiring immediate cash funding. If they lack the cash, their position may be liquidated at a loss, leaving them unhedged, even though their underlying physical crop is increasing in value.
*   **Yield / Volume Risk:** The farmer must hedge an *estimated* volume of wheat. If adverse weather (drought, flooding) results in a poor harvest, the farmer will have sold futures contracts for wheat they don't possess. This leaves them holding naked, speculative short derivative positions, exposing them to massive losses if prices rise.
*   **Currency Risk:** If the farmer is not located in the US (e.g., in the UK or Eurozone), the CME futures are priced and settled in US Dollars. Fixing the USD price does not fix their local currency revenue, exposing them to FX fluctuations unless they concurrently implement a currency hedge.
*   **Operational / Execution Risk:** The risk that the farmer miscalculates the hedge ratio, executes the trade incorrectly, or fails to roll contracts if the harvest date shifts slightly, rendering the hedge ineffective.

## Question 7

### Part (i) [1 mark]

An SPV (Special Purpose Vehicle) is used to ensure the transaction is "bankruptcy remote". This legally isolates and rings-fences the underlying mortgage assets from the originator, meaning that if the originator or government-backed institution goes bankrupt, the cash flows from the mortgages are safely preserved for the MBS investors and cannot be seized by the originator's general creditors.

### Part (ii) [7 marks]

An analyst would need the following information to comprehensive assess the MBS:
*   **Underlying Pool Characteristics:** Data on the individual mortgages, including the average Loan-To-Value (LTV) ratios, borrower credit scores, the geographical concentration of the properties, loan purposes (e.g., owner-occupied vs buy-to-let), and the maturity profile of the loans.
*   **Tranching and Capital Structure:** The priority of payments (the 'waterfall' structure). The analyst needs to know the attachment and detachment points for the specific tranche they are analysing (senior, mezzanine, or equity/first-loss), and how principal and interest cash flows are distributed.
*   **Prepayment Speeds and Assumptions:** Historical prepayment data and model assumptions regarding how sensitive the underlying borrowers are to interest rate changes (e.g., the Constant Prepayment Rate or PSA benchmark). 
*   **Credit Enhancements:** Details of any structural, internal, or external protection designed to absorb losses. This includes overcollateralisation levels, reserve accounts, excess spread, and any external guarantees or insurance (e.g., guarantees from the government-backed institution).
*   **Originator and Servicer Quality:** Assessment of the institution that originated the loans (their underwriting standards and historical default rates) and the quality of the servicer responsible for actively collecting the mortgage payments and managing defaults.
*   **Yield Metrics and Pricing:** The quoted spread over the risk-free rate, the estimated weighted average life (WAL), effective duration, and convexity of the security under various interest rate scenarios.
*   **Macroeconomic Context:** Information on housing market trends, expected interest rate pathways, and employment stability, as these drive systemic default and prepayment behaviors.

### Part (iii) [4 marks]

An investor in this MBS would face several key risks:
*   **Credit/Default Risk:** The primary risk that homeowners default on their underlying mortgage payments. If the defaults exceed the protections provided by the credit enhancements and lower tranches, the investor will suffer a loss of interest and principal.
*   **Prepayment Risk (Contraction Risk):** If general interest rates fall, homeowners are incentivised to refinance their mortgages at lower rates. The mortgages are paid off early, meaning the MBS investor receives their principal back sooner than expected and is forced to reinvest it in the new, lower interest rate environment.
*   **Extension Risk:** Conversely, if general interest rates rise, homeowners will stay put and prepayments will dramatically slow down. The investor is then locked into a below-market yield for a much longer period than anticipated, causing the capital value of the MBS to plummet.
*   **Liquidity Risk:** MBS are complex, often over-the-counter instruments. During periods of financial stress or credit crunches, the secondary market for MBS can freeze entirely, making it virtually impossible for the investor to sell the security at a fair price without accepting a massive liquidity discount.
