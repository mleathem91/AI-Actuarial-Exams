## Question 1

### Part (i) [6 marks]

**Advantages of corporate and risk management structure:**
* **Lean Central Structure:** Company A has a small staff, reducing central overheads and administrative costs. The subsidiaries handle day-to-day operations.
* **Autonomy for Subsidiaries:** By delegating operations to subsidiaries (B, C, D, E), management can focus on their specific niche within the senior market, allowing for specialised expertise and agility.
* **Regular Reporting:** Monthly reporting of the top three risks from each CEO to the CRO ensures that the Company A board has frequent updates on the most pressing issues.
* **Clear Accountability:** Subsidiary CEOs are directly responsible for managing risks within their own business units and escalating the primary concerns.
* **Brand Leverage:** The centralised model allows Company A to maintain control over its strong brand, whilst subsidiaries benefit from its positive reputation for growth.

**Disadvantages of corporate and risk management structure:**
* **Oversimplified Risk Reporting:** Limiting the reporting to only the 'top three' risks per subsidiary means that emerging risks, or risks that sit just below the top three but are growing rapidly, may be missed by the central board.
* **Lack of Aggregation:** The structure lacks a mechanism to view risks holistically. A minor risk in Subsidiary B and a minor risk in Subsidiary C could combined represent a major, correlated risk to Company A, but this will not be reported.
* **Siloed Risk Management:** Operating entirely bottom-up with CEOs reporting upwards may lead to a siloed approach. There is little indication of cross-subsidiary risk sharing or top-down strategic risk identification by the CRO.
* **Brand Concentration Risk:** Company A's primary asset is its brand. All subsidiaries rely on it. A failure in one subsidiary (e.g. poor practices in funeral plans) could damage the brand, immediately impacting the other subsidiaries and Company A's dividend income. 
* **Potential Conflict of Interest:** CEOs reporting their own risks may have an incentive to downplay severe risks to protect their own position or performance metrics.

### Part (ii) [6 marks]

**Potential actions to improve the corporate structure:**
* **Establish a Centralised Risk Dashboard:** Move away from just reporting the 'top three' risks and require subsidiaries to maintain and share a comprehensive risk register. Establish key risk indicators (KRIs) that are monitored centrally.
* **Implement Top-Down Risk Oversight:** The CRO should lead top-down strategic risk assessments and scenario testing (e.g., demographic shifts, macroeconomic shocks) rather than relying solely on bottom-up reporting from CEOs.
* **Create a Group Risk Committee:** Form a committee comprising the CRO and risk representatives from each subsidiary to encourage the sharing of best practice and identification of interconnected risks.
* **Independent Second Line of Defence:** Ensure Company A has sufficient independent risk resource to challenge the CEOs' assessments, ensuring risks are not being understated.
* **Diversify Revenue:** Reduce the full reliance on licensing the brand. Company A could consider developing central services (e.g., shared IT, HR, or data analytics) that provide value and a different income stream.
* **Contagion Management:** Implement strict operational and legal firewalls to ensure that financial or reputational distress in one subsidiary does not automatically cascade to others.

### Part (iii) [4 marks]

**Interactions between Subsidiary B (Holidays) and E (Social Network):**
* **Marketing Synergy (Opportunity):** Subsidiary E's social network provides a highly targeted advertising platform for Subsidiary B's holiday packages. Data on users' interests in E can be used to offer bespoke holiday deals, reducing customer acquisition costs.
* **Feedback Loop (Opportunity):** Subsidiary B can use Subsidiary E to build communities around its holidays, driving customer loyalty and generating positive reviews that fuel further sales.
* **Reputational Contagion (Risk):** If Subsidiary B provides a poor holiday experience and customers complain strongly on Subsidiary E's platform, the negative feedback will be amplified directly to Company A's target demographic, damaging both brands rapidly.
* **Cyber and Data Risk (Risk):** If the two subsidiaries share IT infrastructure or customer data to exploit synergies, a data breach or cyber-attack on Subsidiary E (which is a high-risk tech platform) could compromise Subsidiary B's customer data and operations.

### Part (iv) [4 marks]

**Effect of diversification on financial performance:**
* **Product Diversification:** Company A operates in diverse sectors (travel, insurance, retail, technology). These sectors have different drivers. For example, a downturn in the travel industry (B) due to a pandemic might be offset by increased engagement in the social network (E). This reduces the volatility of Company A's overall dividend income.
* **Concentration Risk (Demographic):** Despite product diversification, Company A is entirely concentrated on one demographic: the 50-75 age group in Country Z. 
* **Macro-economic exposure:** This group's spending power relies heavily on pensions, savings, and healthcare costs in Country Z. Changes to state pension ages, inflation eroding fixed incomes, or healthcare crises in Country Z would impact all subsidiaries simultaneously, limiting the true diversification benefit.
* **Brand Correlation:** Since all subsidiaries trade heavily on Company A's brand, a severe reputational shock would impact the financial performance of all subsidiaries concurrently, undermining the diversification of the product lines.

### Part (v)(a) [4 marks]

**Key risks impacting the value of brand and reputation:**
1. **Data Security and Privacy Breach:** Particularly concerning Subsidiary E (social network) and potentially C (insurance). A major leak of customer personal or financial information.
2. **Poor Customer Outcomes / Mis-selling:** If Subsidiary C is found to be aggressively mis-selling funeral plans, or failing to pay out valid claims to vulnerable seniors.
3. **Product or Service Failure:** A significant health and safety incident on a Subsidiary B holiday, or a major quality issue with Subsidiary D's clothing.
4. **Negative Media/Social Media Campaigns:** A viral complaint on Subsidiary E about any of the companies' practices could rapidly compromise the brand among the core demographic.

### Part (v)(b) [2 marks]

**Mitigating actions:**
1. **Data risk:** Implement state-of-the-art cybersecurity protocols, strict access controls, and regular independent penetration testing.
2. **Mis-selling risk:** Enforce strict compliance training, transparent product literature, and independent audits of the sales and claims processes.
3. **Service failure risk:** Comprehensive health and safety vetting for all holiday vendors and strict quality control checks for clothing manufacturers.
4. **Media risk:** Maintain a robust public relations and crisis management team to swiftly handle and resolve complaints before they escalate.

### Part (vi) [7 marks]

**Appropriateness of linking bonus directly to dividends:**
* **Short-termism:** This suggestion is highly inappropriate. It incentivises CEOs to maximise short-term cash generation at the expense of long-term value.
* **Underinvestment:** A CEO might cut essential spending on R&D, IT upgrades, or marketing to boost short-term profits and dividends, irreparably damaging the subsidiary’s future prospects.
* **Ignoring Risk:** This metric ignores the risk taken to generate the dividend. A CEO might take excessively risky bets to boost returns, knowing they receive the upside while Company A bears the long-term downside.
* **Capital Depletion:** It could encourage stripping capital from the subsidiaries (especially the insurance subsidiary C, which has strict regulatory capital requirements), threatening their solvency.
* **Inequity:** Subsidiaries are in vastly different industries. A tech platform (E) might need to retain earnings for growth, while a mature insurance book (C) naturally throws off cash. Punishing the CEO of a growing business is counterproductive.

**Alternative remuneration approaches:**
* **Balanced Scorecard:** Use a mix of financial (e.g., profit before tax, return on equity) and non-financial metrics (e.g., customer satisfaction metrics, employee turnover, risk management audit scores).
* **Risk-Adjusted Return:** Link bonuses to risk-adjusted metrics like Risk-Adjusted Return on Capital (RAROC) to penalise excessive risk-taking.
* **Long-Term Incentive Plans (LTIPs):** Defer a significant portion of the bonus over 3-5 years, or award it in Company A shares that must be held, aligning the CEO's interests with the long-term brand value.
* **Clawback Provisions:** Ensure bonuses can be recovered if risks materialise later or if compliance breaches are discovered.

### Part (vii) [5 marks]

**Processes to identify risks of the family history service:**
1. **Brainstorming / Workshops:** Gather experts from legal, IT, marketing, and the proposed service team in a facilitated session to freely generate a list of potential risks regarding DNA handling and data.
2. **Scenario Analysis:** Develop specific "what-if" scenarios (e.g., "What if a customer discovers a disturbing family secret via DNA?", "What if the DNA database is hacked?") and trace the consequences.
3. **Consultation / Delphi Technique:** Seek anonymous, iterative input from internal and external subject matter experts (e.g., genetic privacy lawyers, cybersecurity experts) to identify niche risks.
4. **Desktop Review / Competitor Analysis:** Research existing family history and DNA companies to identify risks that have materialised for them (e.g., data breaches, regulatory fines, ethical controversies).
5. **Process Flow Analysis:** Map out the entire customer journey, from DNA kit delivery to uploading results online, identifying points of failure, data vulnerabilities, or operational bottlenecks at each stage.

### Part (viii) [6 marks]

**Key stakeholders impacted:**
1. **Customers:** 
   * *Impact:* They gain the benefit of discovering their ancestry, but bear immense privacy risks. Their deeply personal genetic data and family records are being stored. They face emotional risks if unexpected or distressing family relationships are uncovered (e.g., non-paternity events).
2. **Data Protection / Healthcare Regulators:** 
   * *Impact:* Regulators in Country Z will need to oversee Company A's compliance with strict biological data and privacy laws. They face the burden of ensuring Company A is not misusing DNA for other purposes (like sharing with insurers) and must act if there is a breach.
3. **Shareholders of Company A:** 
   * *Impact:* They stand to benefit from a potentially lucrative new revenue stream that capitalises on a current cultural trend. However, they are also exposed to significant new reputational and financial risks (e.g., massive regulatory fines for a DNA data breach) which could affect the share price and dividends.

### Part (ix) [4 marks]

**Option 1: Creating a new standalone subsidiary**
* **Pros:** Ring-fences the significant data and ethical risks associated with DNA testing away from the other subsidiaries. Provides clear management focus and accountability specifically for this complex new venture.
* **Cons:** Slower to set up and much higher initial capital and operating costs (needs its own HR, IT, management). Harder to instantly cross-sell to existing customers.

**Option 2: Offering the service as part of Subsidiary E (Social Network)**
* **Pros:** Rapid speed to market and lower costs by leveraging E's existing IT infrastructure, online platform, and large user base. Highly synergistic—users can easily share family trees on the social network, driving viral growth.
* **Cons:** If a severe privacy breach occurs with the sensitive DNA data, it will destroy trust in the core social network (E), potentially collapsing the subsidiary. It may also distract E's management from their core social network business.

## Question 2

### Part (i) [10 marks]

**Risks when evaluating Company Y as an acquisition target, with mitigants:**

1. **Overvaluation Risk:** Company P may overestimate the value of Y, its market share, or the synergies achievable, leading to paying too high a purchase price.
   * *Mitigation:* Conduct rigorous, independent financial and operational due diligence. Use multiple valuation methods (DCF, market multiples) and structure the deal with earn-outs (paying part of the price later based on Y's actual performance).
2. **Hidden Liabilities Risk:** Company Y may have undisclosed legal disputes, tax liabilities, or environmental issues regarding its production facilities that P will inherit.
   * *Mitigation:* Deep legal and environmental due diligence prior to purchase. Include robust warranties and indemnities in the sale and purchase agreement to hold Y's previous owners liable for historical issues.
3. **Cultural Mismatch:** The corporate culture, management style, and working practices of Company Y may be radically different from Company P and Company X, making future integration highly contentious.
   * *Mitigation:* Perform a cultural assessment during the evaluation phase. Engage closely with Y’s key management to understand their ethos and explicitly assess compatibility.
4. **Anti-Trust / Regulatory Risk:** Given Y has the 2nd largest and X the 3rd largest market share, acquiring Y might trigger local competition/monopoly regulations, causing the regulator to block the deal or force asset sales.
   * *Mitigation:* Engage with competition and regulatory authorities early in the evaluation process to seek pre-clearance or understand likely conditions before committing to the purchase. 

### Part (ii) [15 marks]

**Risks arising from the integration of Company X and Company Y, with mitigants:**

1. **Loss of Key Personnel:** The uncertainty of the merger may cause talented winemakers, managers, or sales staff in both X and Y to leave for competitors.
   * *Mitigation:* Implement clear, transparent communication regarding job security. Offer retention bonuses and long-term incentive plans (like the proposed share scheme) to crucial staff.
2. **IT System Incompatibility:** X and Y likely use different software for production, inventory, and accounting. Forcing them onto a single system could cause severe operational disruption, stock loss, or duplicated orders.
   * *Mitigation:* Run parallel systems initially. Develop a phased IT integration plan driven by dedicated IT project managers, with extensive testing before switching off legacy systems.
3. **Loss of Customers / Brand Dilution:** Customers loyal to Y's distinct brand may leave if they perceive a drop in quality or if Y's brand is aggressively replaced by X's brand.
   * *Mitigation:* Maintain separate branding initially. Carefully manage public relations to assure customers that product quality will remain identical. Gradual phased approach to any brand consolidation.
4. **Supply Chain Disruption:** Integrating procurement could alienate existing suppliers of grapes or packaging, leading to shortages.
   * *Mitigation:* Audit all supplier contracts early. Communicate proactively with key suppliers to provide certainty of ongoing business and negotiate unified contracts smoothly.
5. **Management Distraction:** The immense effort required to merge the two entities may distract X and Y's leadership from day-to-day operations, leading to declining sales and quality.
   * *Mitigation:* Appoint a dedicated Integration Management Office (IMO) comprising specialists to handle the merger logistics, allowing business-as-usual management to focus on operations.
6. **Failure to Realise Synergies:** The cost savings (e.g., economies of scale in distribution, combined head office) expected to justify the merger cost may simply fail to materialise.
   * *Mitigation:* Set strictly defined, measurable synergy targets with clear timelines. Assign specific executives accountability for delivering these targets, tying their remuneration to success.

### Part (iii) [5 marks]

**Purpose of the proposal (Offering P shares to Y management, held for 3 years):**
* The primary purpose is **retention and alignment of interests**. It creates a "golden handcuffs" scenario to prevent Y's crucial management team from leaving during the highly sensitive 3-year integration period.

**Advantages to Company P:**
* **Promotes Stability:** Retains institutional knowledge and leadership, greatly increasing the chances of a successful merger.
* **Aligns Objectives:** Y's management are financially incentivised to make the integration successful, as this will ultimately reflect in P's share price.
* **Cash Flow Management:** Offering shares reduces the immediate cash consideration required to complete the acquisition.

**Disadvantages to Company P:**
* **Dilution:** Issuing new shares dilutes the ownership and earnings per share of Company P's existing shareholders.
* **Cost Ineffectiveness:** If Y's management do not value P's shares (e.g., if P's share price is historically volatile or underperforming), it may fail as a retention tool, forcing P to offer cash bonuses anyway.
* **Risk Aversion:** Management might become overly cautious to protect the share price, stifling necessary aggressive integration actions.

### Part (iv) [8 marks]

**Appropriateness of X and Y adopting P's ERM framework:**

* **Pros:**
    * **Consistency:** Allows Company P's board to view consolidated risk reporting across the group in a unified language.
    * **Best Practice:** P, being a large supermarket, likely has a highly mature and robust ERM framework which could significantly improve the historically non-existent ERM practices of X and Y.
    * **Regulatory alignment:** As a publicly listed/large entity, P's strict ERM framework helps ensure its newly acquired subsidiaries do not expose the parent to compliance failures.

* **Cons:**
    * **Lack of Proportionality:** Company X and Y combined make up less than 2% of P's turnover. Imposing a framework designed for a massive supermarket chain onto small beverage producers is likely to be overly bureaucratic, suffocating them in red tape.
    * **Different Risk Profiles:** A supermarket (retail) faces vastly different risks (e.g., massive footfall liability, complex global logistics, retail property risk) compared to a winery (manufacturing/agriculture), which faces risks like crop failure, fermentation spoiling, and specific excise taxes. P's guidelines may be irrelevant or entirely miss X and Y's key risks.
    * **Resource Drain:** X and Y likely lack the dedicated risk personnel to adhere to heavy reporting requirements, distracting staff from production.

* **Conclusion:** The proposal is currently inappropriate in its "entirety". A proportionate approach is needed. X and Y should adopt the *core principles* and taxonomy of P's ERM framework (to allow for consolidated reporting), but the specific procedures, limits, and reporting frequency must be heavily tailored to fit the smaller scale and specific agricultural/manufacturing risk profile of the wine subsidiaries.

### Part (v) [8 marks]

**Evaluation of Company Y’s business performance and key risks:**

**Performance:**
* **Profitability:** Company Y is profitable. Profit post-tax is 158 on revenues of 725, representing a very strong net profit margin of 21.8%.
* **Return on Equity (ROE):** ROE is strong at roughly 22.6% (158 / 700), indicating good returns for shareholders.

**Key Risks:**
* **Severe Liquidity and Leverage Risk:** This is the most alarming area. Bank debt is very high at 2,000, dwarfing shareholders' funds (700). Gearing (Debt / (Debt+Equity)) is extremely high at 74%. 
* **Interest Rate Risk:** Interest on debt is 100. The interest coverage ratio (Pre-tax profit before interest / Interest) is (175+100)/100 = 2.75x. While currently manageable, any significant increase in interest rates on that 2,000 debt will rapidly wipe out their profitability.
* **High Debtors (Credit Risk):** Debtors stand at 600 against revenues of 725. This implies it takes nearly a year (600/725 * 365 = 302 days) on average to collect cash from customers. This ties up massive capital and presents a severe risk of bad debts if customers default. 
* **Cash Flow Constraint:** Cash is only 300 versus 2,050 in liabilities. If creditors demand payment or the bank recalls debt, Y has very little liquid buffer.
* **Intangible Asset Risk:** Intangible assets (650) make up nearly all of the shareholders' funds (700). If these intangibles (e.g., brand value) suffer impairment, the equity base is essentially wiped out.

### Part (vi)(a) [2 marks]

**Rationale for proposing futures contracts:**
Company Y wants to implement a hedging strategy to fix the future purchase price of its ingredients. This provides certainty over production costs, protecting the company's strong profit margins against the risk of continued inflation in commodity prices over the next 24 months.

### Part (vi)(b) [4 marks]

**Issues with using this mitigation strategy:**
* **Basis Risk:** The futures contracts available on the exchange (e.g., generic wheat or sugar) may not perfectly match the specific, potentially high-grade ingredients Y uses for its alcoholic beverages. Changes in the futures price may not perfectly offset changes in the actual ingredient cost.
* **Liquidity and Margin Calls:** Futures are exchange-traded and marked-to-market daily. If ingredient prices fall, Y's futures position will lose value, and they will be required to post immediate cash margin calls. Given Y's low cash balance (300), this could trigger a severe liquidity crisis.
* **Opportunity Cost:** If ingredient prices fall below the price locked in by the futures contract, Y is committed to paying the higher implied rate. This puts them at a competitive disadvantage against rivals who can buy cheaper ingredients in the spot market.
* **Expertise and Admin:** Y is a small entity and may lack the treasury expertise required to set up, monitor, and correctly account for complex derivative hedging strategies.
