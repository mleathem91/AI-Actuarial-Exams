## Question 1

### Part (i) [3 marks]

The main features of a group Critical Illness (CI) product are:
- **Benefit Trigger:** Pays a lump sum upon the survival (usually for a stated period, such as 14 or 28 days) of the diagnosis of one of a defined, specific list of critical illnesses (e.g. heart attack, stroke, cancer, organ transplant).
- **Structure:** It is an annually renewable contract sponsored by an employer for the benefit of their employees. The premium is typically paid by the employer, though voluntary employee-paid schemes exist.
- **Benefit Size:** The benefit amount is generally defined as a multiple of the employee's base salary (e.g. 1x to 4x) or a fixed flat monetary amount across all staff grades.
- **Underwriting:** A key feature is the Automatic Acceptance Limit (AAL) or Free Cover Limit (FCL). Employees whose benefit level falls below the FCL receive coverage automatically without individual medical underwriting, provided they meet "actively at work" criteria.
- **Cessation:** Coverage automatically ceases when the employee leaves the employer's service or reaches the scheme's cessation age (usually the normal retirement age).

### Part (ii) [5 marks]

Possible reasons why Company A would want to set up an insurance company in Country Y and specialise in selling group CI products:
- **Cultural and Geographic Synergies:** The similar language and culture mean Company A can easily transfer its product designs, marketing materials, and internal processes from Country X to Country Y with minimal friction and translation costs.
- **Market Opportunity:** There may be an untapped or growing demand for employee benefits in Country Y. Employers there might be increasingly looking for products like group CI to attract and retain high-quality staff.
- **Profitability and Volume:** Group risk business characteristically provides large business volumes per transaction compared to individual business, bringing in substantial premium income with lower per-capita distribution and acquisition costs.
- **Lower Underwriting Burden:** Because group CI relies heavily on Free Cover Limits, specialising in this product lowers the immediate need to set up a massive, complex medical underwriting team in the new country.
- **Diversification:** Expanding into Country Y diversifies Company A’s geographic risk and regulatory exposure, making the parent company less dependent solely on Country X’s economy.
- **Stepping Stone:** Establishing a dedicated insurance company for group CI provides a strategic foothold and brand presence in Country Y. This platform can be used later to pivot into selling PMI, Long Term Care, or individual life products.

### Part (iii) [10 marks]

Factors that Company A should consider before deciding whether to proceed with the proposal:
- **Market Demand:** Thorough market research to understand if Country Y’s employers actively desire to provide group CI. Do they currently use other employee benefits instead?
- **Competitive Landscape:** Identifying the current market participants in Country Y. Are there existing dominant insurers driving down prices? Can Company A achieve a competitive advantage in pricing, features, or service?
- **Regulatory and Legal Environment:** What are the capital requirements for establishing a new insurance license in Country Y? What is the regulatory reporting regime, and how burdensome is the authorisation process?
- **Economic Environment:** Current and expected economic growth, interest rates, and currency stability in Country Y. If Country Y uses a different currency, there will be exchange rate risk affecting the parent company's consolidated accounts and initial capital injections.
- **Availability of Data:** Is there credible, country-specific morbidity data for Country Y to accurately price the critical illness risks? If not, pricing using Country X data will carry significant basis risk.
- **Distribution Channels:** How is group risk business sold in Country Y? Assisting specialised employee benefit consultancies and brokers will be necessary. Company A must assess if it can build these intermediary relationships from scratch.
- **Capital and Financing Requirements:** The venture will require initial start-up capital to meet Country Y statutory solvency margins, set up IT infrastructure, and cover initial expense overruns. The parent must verify it can achieve its target Return on Capital.
- **Operational Feasibility:** The ability to recruit capable local staff with actuarial, underwriting, and claims management expertise. Whether IT systems from Country X can be extended to handle Country Y's taxation, regulatory, and linguistic nuances.
- **Reinsurance Support:** The availability of reinsurance in Country Y. A new entrant will heavily rely on reinsurers for pricing data, underwriting manuals, and capacity support to protect against catastrophic losses.
- **Integration and Reputational Risk:** The management distraction away from the core operations in Country X, and the reputational harm if the expansion fails or provides poor customer service.

### Part (iv) [6 marks]

**(a) Product Design**
- **Slightly higher average salary:** Since group CI benefits are often tied to a multiple of salary, the absolute benefit amounts will be higher. The product design must accommodate higher maximum benefit limits to ensure full coverage for higher-earning employees.
- **Lower morbidity and inflation:** Lower inflation reduces the need to build in complex index-linked benefit escalation options. Lower morbidity gives the company the "budget" to offer a wider, more comprehensive list of defined critical illnesses, or partial payments for less severe conditions, making the product highly attractive without becoming unaffordable.

**(b) Underwriting**
- **Slightly higher average salary:** The higher base salaries mean that standard salary multiples will generate higher sums assured. Company A will need to set suitably high Free Cover Limits (FCLs) to ensure the majority of employees continue to be automatically accepted, bypassing administrative bottlenecks.
- **Lower morbidity:** Better general health and lower incidence of illness in Country Y could justify the setting of more generous (higher) FCLs than in Country X, and potentially relaxing the strictness of the "Actively at Work" requirements, accepting slightly more risk.

**(c) Pricing**
- **Lower morbidity:** This is the most crucial factor directly impacting the risk premium. Lower expected claim frequencies mean Company A can price the CI product more aggressively/competitively. However, an experience margin must still be maintained, as past Country Y data may differ from the specifically insured corporate demographic.
- **Slightly higher average salary:** Because the premium represents a rate applied per mille of the sum assured, higher sums assured generate a larger absolute premium per member. This larger premium base means fixed per-policy expenses represent a smaller proportion of the premium, yielding economies of scale and allowing for competitive pricing.
- **Lower historical inflation:** This leads to lower assumptions for future expense inflation. Consequently, the loading for future maintenance and claim administration expenses built into the premium will be lower.

## Question 2

### Part (i) [4 marks]

**(a) Rehabilitation benefits**
These are features within Income Protection policies designed to support and encourage a financially secure return to work for a sick policyholder. They may include the insurer paying to cover the direct costs of medical rehabilitation, physiotherapy, counseling, or vocational retraining. They also include "proportionate benefits," where the insurer continues to pay a partial claim if the individual returns to work in a part-time capacity or a lower-paid job, ensuring the claimant is not financially penalised for making the effort to return to employment.

**(b) Linked claims period**
A policy provision stating that if a claimant recovers, ceases claiming, and returns to work, but subsequently suffers a relapse or claims again for the same (or a related) medical condition within a specified timeframe (e.g. 6 or 12 months), the two periods of sickness are treated as a single continuous claim. The policyholder benefits because they do not have to serve a new deferred period before the benefit payments recommence.

### Part (ii) [8 marks]

**Approach and Process:**
Company B will typically use a prospective, individual policy-by-policy approach to calculate claims in payment (CIP) reserves for its Income Protection business. 

1. **Extraction of Data:** Extract data on all currently active IP claimants at the valuation date. Data will include age, gender, exact cause of sickness, duration of sickness to date, current benefit amount, deferred period, and remaining term to policy expiry/retirement.
2. **Methodology:** For each individual claimant, the reserve is calculated as the present discounted value of all expected future benefit payments plus expected future claim handling expenses.
3. This is calculated using a continuous sickness annuity factor: `Reserve = (Benefit Level) x (Sick Life Annuity) + Expense Reserve`.
4. The annuity takes into account the probability of the claimant remaining sick at each future duration until the policy cessation age. The state transitions modelled are Sick -> Dead, and Sick -> Recovered (Healthy). 

**Assumptions Required:**
- **Claim Termination Rates (Recovery and Mortality):** The most critical assumptions. These must be modelled longitudinally, heavily dependent on the *duration* of sickness to date, the claimant's age, and the exact cause of claim. Standard tables (e.g., CIDA tables or Country Z specific tables) are typically used and adjusted with multipliers based on Company B's own recent recovery experience.
- **Discount Rate:** An assumption representing the expected future investment return on the assets backing the claims reserves, minus a margin for default/credit risk.
- **Claim Expenses:** An assumption for the ongoing cost of administering the claim. This is significant for IP as long-term claimants require frequent medical reviews, home visits, and rehabilitation management.
- **Inflation / Escalation:** If the IP benefit in payment includes escalation (e.g., increasing by inflation or a fixed % annually), an assumption for future price inflation is needed to project the increasing benefit cash flows. An expense inflation assumption is also required for the claim expenses.
- **Prudent Margins:** Depending on Country Z’s statutory requirements, margins for prudence (e.g., assuming lower recovery rates, higher inflation, lower discount rates) must be included.

### Part (iii) [8 marks]

**(a) Financial intermediaries (brokers):**
- **Mis-selling Risk:** Brokers might misrepresent the policy to secure a sale, selling IP to individuals whose employment status or needs are unsuitable, leading to customer complaints, regulatory fines, and brand damage.
- **Anti-selection and Non-disclosure Risk:** Brokers might encourage applicants to suppress relevant medical history to secure lower premiums or bypass underwriting exclusions, leading to worse-than-priced morbidity experience.
- **Credit Risk:** If the broker collects premiums directly from the policyholder, there is a risk the broker becomes insolvent and fails to pass the funds to Company B.

**(b) Third party administration (TPA) providers:**
- **Operational Risk:** The TPA might provide poor service, miscalculate IP benefit amounts, or severely delay claim payments, heavily impacting customer satisfaction and retention.
- **Data Security / Cyber Risk:** The TPA handles extremely sensitive medical and financial data. A cyber breach at the TPA could result in severe data protection regulatory fines for Company B.
- **Business Continuity Risk:** If the TPA goes out of business or its IT systems suffer a critical failure, Company B may be entirely unable to process business or pay legitimate claims.

**(c) Reinsurers:**
- **Counterparty Default / Credit Risk:** This is a major concern. If Company B relies on the reinsurer for significant IP claims payments and the reinsurer becomes insolvent, Company B remains fully legally liable to pay the policyholders, leading to a massive capital strain.
- **Dispute Risk:** The reinsurer may dispute Company B's claims management approach or the validity of complex IP claims, withholding reimbursement.

**(d) Professional advisers:**
- **Incompetence / Negligence Risk:** Actuaries or auditors might make calculation errors in setting the reserves or pricing. Lawyers may draft flawed policy conditions. The financial and legal consequences of acting on this bad advice fall entirely on Company B.
- **Conflict of Interest:** Advisers may lack independence and provide biased advice that serves their own commercial interests rather than protecting Company B’s solvency.

### Part (iv) [6 marks]

Measures to mitigate the risks identified in part (iii):

**(a) Brokers:**
- Implement strict Terms of Business Agreements (TOBAs) outlining compliance standards, with severe financial claw-back clauses for early lapses.
- Conduct regular, unannounced compliance audits and "mystery shopping" on broker sales practices.
- Collect premiums via direct debit straight from the customer's bank account, bypassing the broker.

**(b) TPAs:**
- Embed stringent, measurable Service Level Agreements (SLAs) within the contract, linked to harsh financial penalties for underperformance.
- Mandate strict data security standards and require the TPA to share regular external IT security audit reports (e.g., ISO 27001). Require a robust, tested Business Continuity Plan.

**(c) Reinsurers:**
- Mitigate credit risk through diversification by using a panel of multiple reinsurers rather than a single entity.
- Set strict financial criteria, ensuring reinsurers hold a high credit rating (e.g. Standard & Poor's AA- or better) and monitor these ratings proactively.
- Demand collateral arrangements (e.g., letters of credit or funds withheld) where the reinsurer deposits assets with Company B.

**(d) Professional advisers:**
- Ensure all external advisers are members of recognised professional bodies (e.g., IFoA for actuaries) and hold current practicing certificates.
- Mandate that advisers provide proof of substantial Professional Indemnity (PI) insurance to cover the financial cost of their potential negligence.
- Put consultancy contracts out to competitive tender every few years to ensure fresh, independent perspectives and avoid complacency.

## Question 3

### Part (i) [3 marks]

Differences between the main product features of pre-funded LTCI and immediate needs LTCI:
- **Target Market & Timing:** Pre-funded LTCI is purchased by healthy individuals well in advance of needing care (often decades earlier). Immediate needs LTCI is purchased by individuals who have already lost independence and are on the verge of, or already residing in, a long-term care facility.
- **Premium Payment:** Pre-funded products are usually paid for via regular, ongoing monthly or annual premiums over the working lifetime. Immediate needs products are purchased using a single, large upfront lump-sum premium (an impaired life annuity).
- **Underwriting Approach:** Pre-funded policies heavily utilize standard medical underwriting to exclude individuals with pre-existing conditions and select for good health. Immediate needs policies use impaired life underwriting (clinical assessment of immediate severity), where poor health and shorter life expectancy actually result in more favourable pricing for the customer.

### Part (ii) [5 marks]

Reasons why the pre-funded LTCI product sold lower levels of new business than the immediate needs product:
- **Affordability:** Regular premiums for pre-funded LTCI can be prohibitively expensive over a long duration. In contrast, immediate needs products are often funded directly from the liquidation of assets, predominantly the sale of the policyholder's house at the precise moment care is required.
- **Apathy and Myopia:** Younger, healthy individuals often suffer from myopia—they do not want to contemplate becoming old, frail, and needing care. Immediate needs purchases are driven by unavoidable, immediate necessity where apathy is no longer an option.
- **Complexity and Uncertainty:** Pre-funded contracts are complex. Consumers are uncertain if they will ever need care, and they may fear restrictive insurer definitions will prevent payout decades in the future.
- **Reliance on the State:** Consumers may falsely believe that the State will comprehensively pay for their eventual care. It is only at the point of immediate need that families realize the strict means-testing involved and are forced to rely on private solutions.
- **Reputational Issues in the Market:** Providers of pre-funded LTCI historically faced severe pricing problems and subsequently hiked premiums drastically on reviewable policies. This generated highly negative press, severely dampening consumer appetite.

### Part (iii) [12 marks]

The opportunities and risks to Company C of each of the government's expectations:

**1. Lower GDP growth:**
- *Risks:* A weaker economy leads to lower real wage growth and lower disposable incomes. Potential customers will struggle to afford regular premiums for pre-funded LTCI, leading to reduced new business and higher lapses. A stagnant property market could make it difficult for families to sell houses to fund the single premium for immediate needs products.
- *Opportunities:* Lower economic growth could lead to a lower interest rate environment, which makes new single premium immediate annuities incredibly expensive, generating high gross premium revenue (though capital intensity increases).

**2. Significantly higher inflation:**
- *Risks:* If Company C's policies offer "indemnity" style benefits (paying the exact cost of care home fees), the claims costs will explode, as care costs are highly correlated with staff wages and inflation. A massive reserve strengthening will be required, risking insolvency. Fixed premiums will be insufficient, and expense inflation will erode the profitability of administering the book.
- *Opportunities:* General anxiety regarding the rapidly escalating cost of care will make pre-funded LTCI an extraordinarily attractive proposition for consumers looking to lock in protection against future hyper-inflation in care costs.

**3. Government reducing direct spending, promoting private financing:**
- *Risks:* The government may heavily regulate the space to ensure consumer protection, potentially dictating strict product designs (e.g. maximum pricing, minimum benefit levels, preventing underwriting exclusions for the sick) making it less profitable.
- *Opportunities:* This provides a massive, state-sponsored marketing boost. The removal of the state safety net forces the population to purchase private LTCI. Company C could see a huge surge in volume for both pre-funded and immediate needs, gaining economies of scale.

**4. Average life expectancy increasing to 80 years:**
- *Risks:* The fundamental risk in pre-funded LTCI. A higher proportion of policyholders will survive into extreme old age, which is exactly when morbidity and cognitive decline (e.g. dementia) accelerate, leading to vastly higher claim frequencies. Once in claim, they may also live longer in the care state, increasing the total severity/cost per claim.
- *Opportunities:* For immediate needs annuities, if the prospective time spent in the impaired state is lengthened, Company C will quote higher single premiums up front, generating massive AUM (Assets Under Management).

**5. Increasing the state pension age:**
- *Risks:* People working later into life might delay their mental transition into "retirement planning", pushing the age of purchasing pre-funded LTCI older, where premiums become highly uncompetitive and expensive.
- *Opportunities:* Individuals working longer will accumulate more lifetime wealth and have an extended window of regular income, giving them greater financial capacity to afford pre-funded LTCI regular premiums.

### Part (iv) [8 marks]

Actions Company C could take to mitigate the identified risks:
- **Product Design (Inflation Risk):** Cease selling indemnity-based LTCI. Design new products that only pay a fixed, defined cash benefit (e.g. $1,000 per month), transferring the risk of spiralling care inflation back to the policyholder.
- **Premium Flexibility (Inflation & Longevity Risk):** Ensure all new pre-funded LTCI products are sold on a "guaranteed reviewable" premium basis. This gives Company C the legal right to continually review and increase premiums to counter adverse trends in life expectancy or care costs.
- **Asset Liability Management (Inflation Risk):** Alter the investment strategy backing the reserves. Invest heavily in inflation-linked government bonds, real estate, or infrastructure to ensure asset returns naturally match the rising claim and expense liabilities.
- **Underwriting and Claims Control (Longevity Risk):** Implement extremely strict objective triggers for care definitions (e.g. failing 3 out of 6 Activities of Daily Living) and aggressively manage claims to ensure individuals only receive benefits while strictly qualifying.
- **Reinsurance (Longevity & Capital Risk):** Purchase longevity reinsurance, stop-loss, or quota-share treaties from large international reinsurers. This transfers the long-tail risk of outliving assumptions off Company C’s balance sheet.
- **Innovate Product Offerings (Regulatory/Economic Risk):** Develop unit-linked LTCI products where the policyholder's premiums are invested in funds, and care costs are drawn from the fund value. This removes the investment and inflation risk from the company's shareholders.

## Question 4

### [12 Marks]

Factors that Company D should consider before agreeing to the marketing director’s proposal for a specialist athlete PMI product:

**1. Market Size and Target Demographics:**
- Despite the aspirational surge, what is the genuine numerical size of the elite/competitive athlete population in Country Y?
- Is this niche large enough to produce the premium volumes required to cover the heavy fixed costs of developing, marketing, and administering a bespoke product?

**2. Product Design and Coverage Implications:**
- The request for medical cover at "overseas events" and "higher level of injuries requiring specialist treatment" fundamentally changes standard PMI.
- International coverage requires arranging an international network of hospitals and repatriation services, which Company D currently lacks.
- Covering elite sports injuries removes the standard "hazardous pursuits" or "professional sports" exclusions, which protect insurers from virtually guaranteed severe claims.

**3. Lack of Data and Pricing Uncertainty:**
- Company D lacks historical morbidity data specifically for elite athletes' injuries and expected treatment costs.
- Elite athletes demand top-tier specialist surgeons and highly intensive, prolonged rehabilitation therapy (e.g. hyperbaric chambers, daily physio) which cost significantly more than standard treatments.
- The pricing basis risk is massive. If overpriced, sales will be zero; if underpriced, the company guarantees heavy losses.

**4. Anti-selection and Moral Hazard:**
- **Anti-selection:** Athletes who possess underlying/historic biomechanical issues or "niggles" will immediately purchase the product, knowing they will imminently require surgery. Strenuous pre-acceptance medical underwriting (e.g. mandatory MRI scans) will be intensely expensive.
- **Moral Hazard:** If an athlete has a minor injury, they might exploit the premium policy to seek excessively expensive elective surgery or luxury rehabilitation, knowing the insurer is footing the bill.

**5. Operational and Claims Management Challenges:**
- Verifying the validity of complex sports injuries is difficult. Insurers need specialist sports medicine doctors on staff to assess claims.
- Managing and resolving overseas claims brings massive complexity regarding foreign languages, varying healthcare regulatory standards, and significantly increased risk of fraud.

**6. Brand and Reputational Risk:**
- The marketing director views athletes as "role models" to enhance public image. However, athletes are highly vocal and publicly visible.
- If Company D declines a complex claim or delays overseas treatment due to policy terms, the athlete could easily take to social media. The ensuing negative PR would severely damage Company D’s brand, entirely backfiring the marketing director's intentions.

**7. Reinsurance Support:**
- Securing reinsurance for an untested, high-risk niche product is incredibly difficult. Company D must consult its reinsurers to verify if they will accept a quota share or provide stop-loss capacity. If reinsurers refuse, the entire capital strain falls on Company D.

**8. Capital Requirements:**
- As a highly volatile and uncertain product, regulators and internal models will demand Company D holds significant excess risk-based capital to cover the tail risks.

**Conclusion:** The proposal carries excessive risk due to unknown data, immense claims costs, and adverse selection. Company D should consider a severely restricted pilot launch, or partner with specific athletic governing bodies on an experience-rated scheme, rather than a direct-to-consumer individual PMI product.

## Question 5

### [10 Marks]

Factors Company E should take into consideration in assessing the potential implications of Country Z’s regulatory changes:

**1. Impact on Statutory Balance Sheet and Free Assets:**
- Calculating the difference between the current heavily prudent reserves and the new Best Estimate (BE) reserves. The BE liabilities will be significantly lower, causing an immediate release of reserves.
- However, the company must accurately model the total new requirement: BE Liabilities + Cost of Capital Risk Margin + new Solvency Capital Requirement (SCR).
- The total impact on the company’s net available free assets and its statutory solvency ratio must be quantified to see if capital raising is required, or if excess capital can be distributed as dividends.

**2. Capital Intensity of Different Product Lines:**
- The Cost of Capital approach penalises products with long-term, unhedgeable risks (e.g., Long-Term Care, prolonged IP claims). Projecting capital requirements into the remote future for these long-tail health products will result in massive Risk Margins.
- Conversely, short-tail products like annual PMI will have much lower Risk Margins.
- The company must assess which product lines remain viable and which may require strategic withdrawal or heavy repricing.

**3. Product Pricing and Profitability:**
- Because the company now has to hold the Risk Margin and a sophisticated SCR, the target return on these locked-up capital amounts must be built into product pricing.
- Higher capital-intensive products will require significant premium increases to remain profitable, potentially reducing Company E's competitiveness in the market.

**4. Asset Allocation and Investment Strategy:**
- If the SCR is implemented via a Value at Risk (VaR) approach, holding volatile risk assets (like equities or low-grade corporate bonds) will trigger severe capital penalty charges.
- Company E must review its investment strategy. It may be forced to de-risk its portfolio, moving heavily into low-yielding government bonds to optimise its SCR, which will lower overall expected investment returns.

**5. IT Systems, Data, and Computational Power:**
- Projecting future capital requirements at the end of each future year for the run-off of the business (to calculate the Cost of Capital Risk Margin) requires immense computing power, nested stochastic models, and highly granular policy data.
- Company E must review whether its existing IT and actuarial software infrastructure is fit for purpose, or if a multi-million-dollar upgrade is required.

**6. Human Resources and Actuarial Skills:**
- The new regime is mathematically sophisticated. Company E will require a larger team of highly skilled actuaries and risk professionals to build, validate, and maintain VaR models and Cost of Capital projections.

**7. Model Choice: Standard Formula vs. Internal Risk Measure Model:**
- The regulator allows a choice. Company E must conduct a cost-benefit analysis. A bespoke internal VaR model accurately reflecting Company E’s specific risk profile and diversification benefits might result in a lower SCR.
- However, developing an internal model is phenomenally expensive and requires rigid, lengthy regulatory approval. The Standard Formula is cheaper to implement but is a blunt instrument that might demand excessively high capital.

**8. Transitional Arrangements Strategy:**
- Assessing the timeline provided by the regulator for implementation. Will there be transitional relief smoothing the capital impact over several years? This dictates the urgency of any capital restructuring required.
